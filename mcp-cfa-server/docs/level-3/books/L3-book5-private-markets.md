READING M1

PRIVATE INVESTMENTS AND STRUCTURES

EXAM FOCUS

This reading focuses primarily on private investments, while also differentiating them from public investments. Candidates should understand the differences between private and public investments in terms of key characteristics such as liquidity, performance, and asset prices. Also important is understanding the different forms of investment structures available to private investors. Know the calculations and interpretations of performance measures such as the internal rate of return, return on investment, the different market fund multiples, and how to use a public market equivalent methodology. Finally, candidates should be familiar with the risks and associated return expectations for asset class investments in both the public and private spaces.

MODULE M1.1: COMPARING PRIVATE VS. PUBLIC INVESTMENTS AND MARKETS

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M1.a: Contrast the features of private and public investments, and discuss characteristics of private and public markets

Although both are often found in investor portfolios, public and private investments differ dramatically in terms of their characteristics. Public investments are typically listed equity or debt securities that trade on exchanges and through over-the-counter dealers. They tend to be issued by more mature entities with more stable cash flows, and they usually do not provide control positions. Traders can quickly buy and sell their investments, and valuation is easier due to greater information on current/historical prices as well as comparison benchmarks.

Private investments are, in many ways, opposite to their public counterparts. The assets are usually unlisted, there is no over-the-counter or exchange market, and the underlying company is often unwilling or unable to access public markets. Ownership stakes tend to be significant (if not, controlling), and investments are usually held for longer than they are for public companies. Contracts are negotiated and nonstandard, which differs considerably from public investments. Note that alternative investments (those that do not involve traditional cash instruments or public equity/fixed-income investments) are not synonymous with private investments, as alternative investments
may have public and/or private elements. Figure M1.1 provides a characteristic comparison of private versus public investments.

Figure M1.1: Private vs. Public Investments

| Characteristic | Private | Public |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquidity | Illiquid (sales restricted or even prohibited) | Mostly liquid (limited restrictions, if any) |
| Investment process | Closed-end | Open-end |
| Performance measurement | Compounded over a longer holding period | Periodic |
| Asset prices | Negotiated (based on estimates) | Observable and traded regularly |
| Management skills | Expertise needed in legal and financial analysis; experience in industry, management, and technical areas | Experience and expertise needed in company, industry, and financial analysis |
| Diversification within a portfolio | Dependent on uniqueness of asset type, as well as company and investment life cycle phases | Dependent on correlations of periodic returns |

# Liquidity and the Investment Process

Because public investments tend to be listed on exchanges (or traded over the counter) and are actively traded, they will have low transaction costs and high liquidity. In contrast, private investments will have far less liquidity. To invest privately, fund managers expect a longer time horizon and larger capital commitments from their investors. To avoid the negative impact of early liquidations, investors are often heavily restricted (or even prohibited) from selling their fund positions.

The investment structure and approach is more open ended for public investments because of their greater price transparency and liquidity. For private investments, the approach is more closed end with investments held for longer periods and expectations that managers will be compensated based on how well the investment performs.

The private asset investment life cycle involves four phases:

1. Precommitment. This is where investment strategy is developed, due diligence is performed, and limited partners are solicited, leading to a commitment of capital.
2. Capital deployment. This is where investments are selected, capital calls are made by the managers, and value is (hopefully) created.
3. Capital distribution. This is where debt is paid off, and the project or restructuring is completed.
4. Exit. This is where there is a public or private sale or transfer.

Returns are expected to be negative in the early phases (precommitment/commitment of capital and capital deployment) and positive in later phases (capital distribution and exit). The returns reflect a J-curve effect, shown in Figure M1.2.
![img-0.jpeg](img-0.jpeg)
Figure M1.2: J-Curve Effect

Management influence differs considerably between public and private investments. Investors in public markets have influence only to the extent of their voting rights, whereas private investment managers have active roles in management and asset control. A private investment manager may be involved in performing due diligence on potential targets, developing financing and business plans, making decisions on capital deployment and the eventual sale/exit of the investment, and return of capital to investors.

# Performance Measurement and Asset Prices

As noted earlier, having access to price data (both current and historical) and identifying relevant benchmarks are advantages of holding public investments. Price transparency for private market investors is much harder to find, as price estimates have to be derived using discounted cash flow (DCF) methodologies, relative valuation techniques, and recent transactions where applicable. Timing is also a challenge, as fund managers often provide valuation estimates far less frequently, and with more delays than they would for public investments.

Cash flows are substantially different between private and public, as private investments involve multiple inflows and outflows that are not very predictable. Public investments are valued using an initial outflow and relatively predictable future inflows such as dividends on equities, interest income on bonds, and net operating income (NOI) for real estate.

# Management Skills

Managers of public funds rely on the managers of their investee companies to create value, while they (the fund managers) perform the research across industries, companies, and individual securities. For managers of private investments, the skills they need to be successful are more nuanced as they have to take investments from the initial selection stage to the exit stage. Industry and operational expertise are needed, as well as established relationships with industry and experience in building and restructuring businesses. Expertise in tax, accounting, and law are critical as well.
Venture capital is a form of private equity investing where funds are given to early-stage companies with minimal (or no) revenue. The rate of failure is high, which means the risk level is high as well. The funds are provided by investors with the partnerships, contacts, and experience to help early companies be successful.

## Diversification

Private investments can provide portfolio diversification due to their low (or negative) correlations with public investments. Private markets offer the following sources of diversification:

- Return dynamics of private company equity and debt over their investment life cycles, which will differ from the return dynamics of more mature public companies
- The exposures of private company equity and debt to growth or restructuring opportunities during their life cycles, which are typically not available to public company investors
- Private investment return exposures (other than equity or debt) that show different return dynamics than public investments

Private investments typically span the entirety of a company's life cycle. The stages of a company life cycle range from start-up, to growth, to maturity, and finally to decline. Cash flows tend to be primarily outflows until the growth stage, after which they hopefully become positive for the duration of the company. Revenues also tend to begin during the growth stage, while some companies may generate revenues late in the start-up phase.

Another avenue for diversification is through private buyout equity, where public companies are taken private in an attempt to reorganize the company and sell it later at a higher price. Reduced correlations with public investments may arise from the private manager's ability to select, finance, restructure, or acquire other companies.

Real estate and/or infrastructure represent alternative asset classes that could provide additional diversification benefits. Public real estate investment trusts (REITs) tend to provide more stable income, while private real estate will involve new construction or significant renovations.

Illiquidity, large minimum investment size, minimal price transparency, and specialized knowledge have historically made it challenging for all but the wealthiest investors and entities to participate in private investments. However, recent evolutions have increased the accessibility of these investments to a wider range of high-net-worth and institutional investors.

# MODULE M1.2: PRIVATE INVESTMENTS: METHODS, STRUCTURES, AND USES

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M1.b: Discuss private investment methods and structures and their uses.

The limited partnership structure is a popular one for private investments. The partnership is run by a general partner (GP) who serves as the fund manager and controls investment operations and processes but has unlimited liability. The investors are limited partners (LPs) who have no investment control, have limited liability, and share in both the returns and the risks with the GP.

# Direct Investments

Investment methods can be both direct or indirect, depending on the investor's ability to actively manage and commit resources over the life cycle of the investment. A direct investment is a purchase of a private debt investment or equity ownership stake without involving an investment intermediary or a partner. The direct investor who purchases a majority ownership position in equity will be responsible for developing a business plan for creating value. This approach is more common with larger asset owners who can afford the investment and the associated risk.

Co-investment can take the form of either a direct co-investment or a limited partner co-investment. The direct co-investment will be a direct purchase (with partners) of a private debt or equity investment, with the investor benefiting from partner expertise, size reductions of private portfolio positions, and lower fees relative to indirect investments. A limited partner co-investment is a purchase of private debt or equity in a single investment run by a private fund manager. These co-investment opportunities are made available to a smaller group before wider distributions to the investment community.

Experience and expertise is absolutely critical with private investments, as these ventures tend to have high failure rates. Angel investors are high-net-worth investors who often have entrepreneurial expertise and industry experience and can provide early-stage financing for start-up ventures.

# Indirect Investments

Fund-based alternatives and co-investments are examples of indirect private market investments. The limited partner co-investment option affords LPs the opportunity to own larger stakes of assets while the management is left to the private fund manager (GP). The GP will share due diligence materials and investment information with LPs, giving them greater access than that afforded to other investors. LPs have a more active role in the investment process, while GPs get expanded access to investor capital,
shared investment risk, and access to investors outside of established partnerships. For LPs, they are exposed to long holding periods and illiquidity. In addition, capital commitments are required before investments, and the timing of capital calls and distributions can be highly uncertain.

# Private Investment Structures

Corporate governance differs considerably between private and public investments, as the former may allow investors (even with minority positions) the ability to negotiate for seats on the board or influence critical business decisions. While public investments tend to be standardized, private structures may result in new legal organizations created for specific purposes.

One such example is a take-private transaction, where private investors purchase a public company. This may also be referred to as a leveraged buyout (LBO) when a significant amount of debt financing is used to execute the acquisition. The target company's debt is usually refinanced due to change of control clauses in place to protect existing bondholders and lenders. Long-term financing is negotiated in the second buyout stage, where the acquirer and the target become a new merged company and the financing used in the acquisition is replaced with medium- and longer-term debt issued by the new entity. The debt structures used in private market transactions tend to be less standardized than those found in public markets.

Two types of loans often found in private market transactions include leveraged loans and mezzanine loans. A leveraged loan is a senior secured, prepayable loan with a floating-rate coupon and restrictive debt covenants. A mezzanine loan is a subordinated debt claim that lands between senior debt and common equity. The coupon rate tends to be fixed and higher than the leveraged loan floating rate, and the maturity tends to be longer. However, the debt is not prepayable, and there are fewer restrictive covenants. To gain exposure to these types of loans, private debt investors may invest indirectly as a limited partner or contract directly with a borrower.

A special purpose entity (SPE) may be established for investments in real estate or infrastructure. The entity will exist to facilitate the building, operations, and financing of the underlying real estate or infrastructure asset. The benefit of the SPE is that as its own legal entity, the revenues, expenses, cash flows, assets, liabilities, and contractual obligations are independent of the financial statements of the investment stakeholders.

A concession agreement is one where an operator (or developer) is tasked with building, operating, maintaining, and financing an infrastructure asset over a period according to the terms and conditions of the entity. An SPE is often used within these arrangements.

# MODULE M1.3: INVESTMENT PERFORMANCE AND RETURN METRICS

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M1.c: Discuss the difference between public and private market performance, and calculate, interpret, and discuss the use of performance metrics including distributed to paid-in, residual value to paid-in, and total value to paid-in.

Income (from dividends on equity securities and interest on debt securities) and asset appreciation serve as the most appropriate performance metrics for public investments. Because of the inherent liquidity and price transparency of public investments, it is far easier to calculate and act on these performance measures than it is for private investments.

For private investments, one valuable measure of return over a multiyear holding period is the internal rate of return (IRR). This represents the singular discount rate that produces a net present value (NPV) of zero for a series of cash flows over $n$ periods. An inherent (and potentially problematic) assumption is that interim cash flows can be reinvested at the IRR rate, which may be less realistic with private markets. The formula is shown here:


\mathrm {N P V} = 0 = \frac {\mathrm {C F} _ {1}}{(1 + \mathrm {I R R}) ^ {1}} + \frac {\mathrm {C F} _ {2}}{(1 + \mathrm {I R R}) ^ {2}} + \dots \dots \frac {\mathrm {C F} _ {n}}{(1 + \mathrm {I R R}) ^ {n}}


Another valuable measure is return on investment (ROI), which compares cash flows received to those invested. The investment holding period and time value of money are not factored into this calculation, shown here:


\mathrm {R O I} = \frac {\sum (\text {cash flows received})}{\sum (\text {cash flows invested})}


The following equation relates IRR to ROI and works well when there is a single outflow at the start of the holding period and a single inflow at the end:


\mathrm {R O I} = (1 + \mathrm {I R R}) ^ {\mathrm {n}}


# EXAMPLE: IRR and ROI

An investor is given an opportunity to invest $5.2 million in a project that is expected to return $8.3 million at the end of four years. The investor will only accept projects that exceed a target return of 10%. Calculate the ROI and the IRR and assess whether this investment will meet the investor's required return.
Answer:


\begin{array}{l}
\mathrm{ROI} = \frac{\$8.3 \text{ million}}{\$5.2 \text{ million}} = 1.596x \\
1.596x = (1 + \mathrm{IRR})^4 = 12.40\%
\end{array}


Because the IRR of 12.40% exceeds the investor's target return of 10%, the project will be accepted.

One of the unique elements of private market investments is the need for lengthy, upfront capital requirements. Funds are often committed well in advance (potentially years) of capital deployment, which means the magnitude and timing of capital calls can be highly variable. As a result, the funds used to meet these commitments must be held in highly liquid, public investments that will likely generate relatively low returns. For limited partnerships, the challenges include comparing return horizons relative to public markets and the impact of public market conditions on the timing of capital calls.

The longer that committed capital must be invested in liquid, public investments, the greater the negative impact to IRR. Public market conditions and the overall outlook of the economy will impact the timing of capital calls. When economic growth is strong and borrowing costs are low, GPs are likely to deploy capital quickly. But when interest rates are rising and economic trends lend to greater aversion to taking risk, capital deployments are less frequent.

The vintage year is the year where capital is first deployed to a project or investment and serves as a valuable comparison point for performance relative to other investments with the same vintage year. Private market fund performance is also heavily impacted by return and timing dynamics. After identifying target investments, a GP will deploy capital for lengthy periods with no cash flows to investors. The inflows coming in from early investments will eventually (and hopefully) offset outflows. Once all commitments are fully deployed, the inflows will also factor in exit values from individual investments.

EXAMPLE: IRR across a portfolio of investments

Baker Brothers PE Firm has capital commitments of $80 million in place for its new investment fund. Kyle Baker, the general partner, is looking at five assets over a four-year time frame for investment. The first investment (in Asset 1, or A1) will occur immediately, with the next investment in A2 happening in one year, the next investment in A3 happening one year after that, and so on. The investments required and the cash inflows forecasted over each investment's four-year time horizon are shown next, with amounts stated in millions:
| Asset | Initial Investment | Cash Inflow #1 | Cash Inflow #2 | Cash Inflow #3 | Final Cash Inflow | IRR |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A1 | –15 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 18 | 20.0% |
| A2 | –12 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 14 | 16.7% |
| A3 | –17 | 4 | 4 | 4 | 21 | 23.5% |
| A4 | –20 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 25 | 25.0% |
| A5 | –16 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 20 | 19.9% |

Given this information and the timing of the cash flows, determine the IRR of the overall fund.

## Answer:

The timing of the cash flows is critical in determining the IRR. Because each investment starts one year apart, the cash flows can be organized as follows:

| Asset | CF0 | CF1 | CF2 | CF3 | CF4 | CF5 | CF6 | CF7 | CF8 | Asset IRR |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A1 | –15 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 18 | | | | | 20.0% |
| A2 | | –12 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 14 | | | | 16.7% |
| A3 | | | –17 | 4 | 4 | 4 | 21 | | | 23.5% |
| A4 | | | | –20 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 25 | | 25.0% |
| A5 | | | | | –16 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 20 | 19.9% |
| Net cash flow | –15 | –9 | –12 | –11 | 13 | 26 | 29 | 28 | 20 | 21.1% |

The IRR of the overall fund is 21.1% (CF0 = –15, C01 = –9, C02 = –12, C03 = –11, C04 = 13, C05 = 26, C06 = 29, C07 = 28, C08 = 20, CPT IRR = 21.1). As shown in the previous example, the IRRs of private market fund investments reflect uneven cash flow timing. This makes it difficult to compare private market IRRs to public market returns. For comparison purposes, public market equivalent (PME) methods can be used to compare public and private market returns. The underlying logic behind the PME method is that private fund cash outflows are invested into a public market index, while private fund cash inflows are sold from the same index.

## EXAMPLE: Public market equivalents

A €50 million private equity fund with a five-year life was recently closed. The following table reflects the cash flows of the fund (in millions), as well as a public market index and public market returns:
| Year | Fund Cash Flows | Public Market Index | Public Market Return |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | –28 | 100 | NA |
| 1 | –22 | 115 | 15.00% |
| 2 | 5 | 109 | –5.22% |
| 3 | 10 | 122 | 11.93% |
| 4 | 15 | 145 | 18.85% |
| 5 | 46 | 158 | 8.96% |

Discuss how to interpret these results and show the year-end asset values for a PME associated with this fund.

## Answer:

The fund's IRR is 11.44% (CF0 = –28, C01 = –22, C02 = 5, C03 = 10, C04 = 15, C05 = 46, CPT IRR = 11.44), and the public market index compound return (annual) is 9.58% (PV = –100, FV = 158, N = 5, CPT I/Y = 9.58). Though the IRR of the fund is higher than the PME return, the latter is an annual return that does not reflect the uneven nature of cash flow timing for the private equity fund. So, this is not an appropriate comparison.

The following table shows asset values at year-end for a PME based on the cash flows (in millions) of the fund:

| Year | Fund PME Asset Values |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | 28 |
| 1 | 54.2 |
| 2 | 46.37 |
| 3 | 41.90 |
| 4 | 34.80 |
| 5 | 37.92 |

Calculations for asset values are as follows:

- Year 0: initial investment = 28
- Year 1: 28 × 1.15 (public market return of 15.00%) + 22 additional investment = 54.2
- Year 2: 54.2 × 0.9478 (public market return of –5.22%) – 5 outflow to investors = 46.37
- Year 3: 46.37 × 1.1193 (public market return of 11.93%) – 10 outflow to investors = 41.90
- Year 4: 41.90 × 1.1885 (public market return of 18.85%) – 15 outflow to investors = 34.80
- Year 5: 34.80 × 1.0896 (public market return of 8.96%) = PME terminal value of 37.92

The PME IRR based on cash flows for Years 0–4 and the PME Year 5 asset value is equal to 8.41% (CF0 = –28, C01 = –22, C02 = 5, C03 = 10, C04 = 15, C05 = 37.92, CPT IRR =
8.41). The fund's IRR of 11.44% exceeds the PME IRR of 8.41%. Note that this reflects a greater outperformance than the originally calculated difference of 11.44% versus 9.58% (the public market index compounded annual return). One of the challenges of the PME methodology is choosing the appropriate public market index. The composition of the investments in the private market fund should align with the constitution of the public market index, including in areas such as growth versus value, leverage factors, and capitalization. As evidenced by the previous examples, overall fund returns should be based on the net values of cash inflows and outflows over the life of a fund.

## Private Market Fund Multiples

While IRR is a valuable and relatively easy measure to calculate, there are various other fund return multiples that are useful for evaluating fund performance.

The first of these measures is **paid-in capital (PIC)**, which is calculated using this formula:


\mathrm{PIC} = \frac{\text{capital invested}}{\text{total capital committed}}


This formula is a measure of the extent to which the capital drawdown phase of the life cycle is complete. Another use of this measure is to gauge when a GP may need (or want) to return to the market to raise new funds (the higher the PIC, the more likely this will be).

The next measure is **distributed to paid-in (DPI)**, which is a ratio comparing cumulative distributions (to LPs) relative to capital invested. This "cash-on-cash" return is considered the investor's realized return, as shown here:


\mathrm{DPI} = \frac{\text{cumulative distributions}}{\text{total capital invested}}


Another return measure is **residual value to paid-in (RVPI)**, which is the fund's net asset value (NAV) relative to total invested capital. This is considered the investor's unrealized return, as shown here:


\mathrm{RVPI} = \frac{\text{net asset value}}{\text{total capital invested}}


The **total value to paid-in (TVPI)** serves as the overall investment value to the limited partner and is the summation of DPI and RVPI, as shown here:


\mathrm{TVPI} = \frac{\text{cumulative distributions} + \text{net asset value}}{\text{total capital invested}}

\mathrm{TVPI} = \mathrm{DPI} + \mathrm{RVPI}


A TVPI calculation that is gross of fees may also be referred to as the **multiple of money (MOM)** or the **multiple of invested capital (MOIC)**. While more mature industries tend to have TVPIs near 2x, emerging high-growth industries can have TVPIs near 3x.

MODULE M1.4: RISK AND RETURN FOR PRIVATE AND PUBLIC MARKETS

LOS M1.d: Compare the risk and return of investing in private
markets and public markets as part of a strategic asset allocation.

After the Global Financial Crisis (GFC) in 2008 and 2009, rising valuations, increases in risk appetite, and relatively low interest rates led private markets to outperform public markets—both in terms of attracting new capital and in generating returns.

Participation in private markets has not just been limited to institutional investors, as smaller investors have been able to gain access to these markets as well in both North America and Europe.

While private market returns have overall outperformed those in public markets, there is considerable variability in the former due to a broader range of life cycle phases and investments, as well as specialized manager skills. In looking at the median IRR from inception to late 2022 for private equity funds with vintage years from 2009 to 2019, the median net IRR was 20.1% compared to the average return of the S&amp;P 500 Index of 11.8%. However, the performance for private equity fund managers spanned a range between the top quartile (29.8%) and bottom quartile (11.4%) of 18.4%. Those in the bottom quartile actually slightly underperformed the S&amp;P. The range from the top to bottom quartile for other private asset classes was not as wide.

The three key drivers of periodic return over an investment period for private market investments are as follows:

1. Committed capital. Capital that has not been called (but committed) is typically held in liquid public investments with relatively low expected returns.
2. Deployed capital. Negative returns often occur in early drawdown periods, as no returns are realized, and investors are contributing committed funds that are also being assessed fees.
3. Returns distributed. An IRR of zero occurs when returns equal contributed capital. After time has passed, the IRR of the investment will align with its true compounded return at the end of its life cycle.

While public investments often generate stable cash flows (and hopefully, appreciation) over the life of the investment, private market investments are more reliant on price appreciation due to their long time horizons and J-curve effects. The illiquidity and greater uncertainty associated with private investments present more risk, which equates to higher expected returns for investors.

MODULE M2.1: GENERAL PARTNER ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES

LOS M2.a: Discuss a general partner's roles and responsibilities in managing private investment funds.

Video covering this content is available online.

Private investment funds are typically illiquid investments, structured as closed-end limited liability partnerships, with finite lives. LPs provide committed capital, which is then called over time by the GP, during the capital deployment phase, as investments in target companies are identified and established.

The GP defines the fund's strategy and identifies and invests in portfolio companies (investments). The GP then deploys value creation strategies to increase the value of investments before exiting and distributing returns to the LPs toward the end of the fund's life. Value creation requires hands-on active management of the portfolio companies. The GP must have specific knowledge and skills relating to the market in which the portfolio companies operate to facilitate the value creation process.

The investments made in target companies (portfolio companies) are controlling interests or holdings that give the GP significant interest; this allows the GP to actively control the strategy of the companies. As a result, these funds hold fewer investments,
are significantly less diversified, and have longer holding periods than funds that invest in public securities.

The portfolio companies held by the fund have low price transparency, resulting in the need to apply valuation methodologies (such as DCF or relative valuation multiples) to estimate the value of investments and the fund's net asset value. Reliable performance benchmarking over the life of an investment is also problematic in private markets. In addition, the value creation process and the ultimate exit timings and routes are uncertain, resulting in unpredictable distributions of capital and returns back from the fund to the LPs.

Funds typically have negative cash flows in their infancy and positive cash flows toward the end of their fixed lives, as shown in Figure M2.1. The fund's performance is often measured by the internal rate of return of these cash flows.

# Precommitment

The GP outlines the fund's strategy and proposed value creation based on the fund's specialized skill set and experience.

# Capital Commitment

Unfunded capital commitments, which may take the form of debt or equity, are secured by attracting investment from LPs. The committed capital is not immediately paid into the fund; rather, these funds will be drawn down as required by the GP to finance due diligence and the acquisition of portfolio companies.

LPs will need to conduct their own due diligence before committing capital, considering the GP's track record and the potential opportunity of the GP's strategy.

A limited partnership agreement is negotiated between the LPs and GP detailing the following:

- Rights of both parties
- Terms and conditions
- Fee structures

The agreement struck may differ for individual LPs depending on bargaining power, determined by the amount and timing of their capital commitment. Special terms that apply to one LP but not to others are stated in "side letters."

# Capital Deployment

The GP finalizes due diligence on target investments. A detailed business and financing plan for each portfolio company is created, detailing the sources of value creation and how they will be realized. The GP bids on potential targets and calls down equity and debt finance to fund the purchases.

Capital drawdowns may take place over numerous years to finance not only the initial acquisition but also fund subsequent rounds of financing to cover future capital requirements of existing investments. Some funds (e.g., private real estate and infrastructure) draw down capital as predetermined milestones (detailed in the limited partnership agreement) are met.

The drawdown period typically covers between three and five years. Debt is typically drawn down more frequently and over a longer period than equity, as it is used to cover the target investment's operating costs.

The initial year in which capital is drawn down from the LPs is referred to as the fund's vintage year. This enables performance comparisons to be made between different funds. Comparing fund performance with the same vintage year is logical, as they have been exposed to the same macroeconomic conditions since the first drawdown of capital.

The GP may use additional credit facilities, known as subscription lines. These are credit lines secured on the LP's committed capital, and they allow the GP to manage the uncertainty in amount and timing of drawdowns by avoiding irregular capital calls to the LPs. These borrowings can be used in the short term before being replaced by drawdowns from LPs. This gives the LPs time to liquidate assets in an orderly fashion to raise the required financing.

The GP is responsible for conducting corporate finance activities and structuring investment deals. The GP will use in-house expertise, but will also use external advisors for corporate finance, taxation, and legal advice.

# Capital Distributions and Exit

In comparison to and investment in public funds, LPs have considerable uncertainty regarding the timing and amounts of distributions from their private funds. This unpredictability leads to the use of compound return measures (such as IRRs) rather than periodic return measures for performance measurement. Distributions for private equity funds take the form of dividends and proceeds from investment exits. For private debt funds, the distributions comprise interest and principal payments from fund investments. Unlike plain vanilla bonds (common in public markets), the debt in private
funds may include embedded options, interest accruals, and other features that make the timing of cash flows less certain.

The investment that the LPs make in the private fund are long term and have limited liquidity. GPs, however, may offer the LPs the ability to trade in existing partnership stakes (known as **secondaries**). The secondary market typically has much wider bid-ask spreads than spreads in public markets. The size of the spread depends on the nature of the fund. For example, venture capital funds tend to have larger discounts to net asset value than buyout funds.

Advantages to LPs of an active secondary market include the ability to do the following:

- Purchase seasoned funds
- Diversify their exposure to GPs, fund strategies, and vintage years
- Free up capital for new investments
- Reduce their exposure to a specific fund
- Avoid investment delays by bypassing the capital deployment phase

The GP exits the fund’s investments toward the end of the fund’s life after the value creation process has been implemented, and the GP returns the proceeds to the LPs. Exit routes can include initial public offerings (IPOs), trade sales, and liquidations (for failed investments). The proceeds generated from exits are uncertain, depending on the success of the GP in implementing the value creation plan (and they have a big impact on LPs’ returns).

The GP also provides periodic valuations of the fund to the LPs. The portfolio companies held by the fund lack price transparency, which necessitates the use of valuation techniques to appraise the value of the fund’s investments and NAV. By definition, these valuations involve significant subjectivity.

# MODULE M2.2: INVESTOR (LP) PERSPECTIVES, FEES, AND PERFORMANCE MEASUREMENT

LOS M2.b: Discuss how private investment firms align their interests with those of their investors, and calculate, interpret, and discuss private market fund performance from an investor perspective, including management fees and carried interest.

Private funds are characterized by multiyear holding periods, illiquidity, uncertain timing of cash flow distributions, and a lack of price transparency. To reduce the impact of asymmetrical information between the GP and LPs, funds typically use a combination of management fees and performance-based fees.

# Management Fees

Management fees are designed to cover the operating costs of the fund. Public funds typically base these fees on a percentage of assets under management (AUM). Private funds base management fees on a percentage of committed capital. Typical management fees equate to 1% for private debt and 2% for private equity, but in practice, this will vary according to the nature of the fund.

Management fees initially are based on committed capital rather than deployed capital (invested capital). This provides income for the GP to cover up-front costs, such as target investment due diligence, and reduces the incentive for the GP to deploy capital too quickly in suboptimal investments simply to earn fee income. The use of a cost basis also eliminates the motivation for the GP to inflate the NAV of the fund to generate greater management fee income.

Once the fund has fully deployed capital, management fees are based on committed capital less the cumulative cost of investments exited or liquidated (net invested capital).

## Carried Interest

Carried interest consists of performance-related fees paid to the GP to incentivize and reward the GP for successful performance.

The methodology of computing carried interest will be set out in the limited partnership agreement and will depend on the bargaining power of the GP and LPs. The GP's bargaining power is determined by their track record, and the LP's bargaining power is driven by the amount of committed capital they are prepared to invest. Remember that different LPs may secure different agreements with the GP, and as a result, should undertake due diligence on the fee structure offered.

Carried interest is paid either at periodic intervals once committed capital has been distributed back to LPs, or alternatively at the end of the fund's predetermined life. Carried interest typically ranges between 15% and 20% of returns above a hurdle rate. Hurdle rates of 7%–8% are common for private funds with a greater dispersion shown for private real estate than private equity. Hurdle rates ensure that the LPs receive a minimum stated return before the GP is entitled to participate in fund returns. Hurdle rates are cumulative and compound annually and may be either soft or hard hurdle rates. The fund's actual returns are typically measured as IRRs.

With a hard hurdle, the GP receives their carried interest split (e.g., 15%) on returns that exceed the hurdle. These are often used when a fund's returns are predictable, or if the carried interest is only paid at the end of the fund's life.
EXAMPLE: Hard hurdle rates

Armstrong Partners (Armstrong) operates a private equity fund with €400m of committed capital. Armstrong receives carried interest of 20% at the end of the fund's life with a hard hurdle rate of 8% and no compounding. The fund generates returns of €500m in excess of deployed capital over its eight-year life span. Assume all capital is deployed at the start of the year.

Calculate carried interest to be paid by the LPs over the fund's lifetime.

Answer:

Carried interest will be paid if the fund's returns exceed the hard hurdle of €256m.

Hard hurdle = 0.08 × 8 years × €400m = €256m.

Carried interest = 0.2 × (€500m - €256m) = €48.8m.

PROFESSOR'S NOTE

In practice, hurdle rates typically compound over the fund's life. In the CFA Institute examples, the hurdle rates ignore compounding for the purposes of simplicity. In practice, the hurdle would be calculated based on deployed capital (invested capital) rather than committed capital so that it can be compared to the fund's IRR—rather than assuming that capital is fully drawn down at the outset, as per this example.

If a fund's returns are less predictable and carried interest is paid periodically over the fund's life, then a soft hurdle rate may be adopted. A soft hurdle applies a "catch-up rate" (which is greater than the GP's carried interest percentage) to profits in excess of the hurdle up to the point where the GP "catches up" to their full carried interest split on total returns. For example, consider a fund with returns of 14%, a hurdle rate of 8%, and 20% carried interest. A soft hurdle with a catch-up rate of 100% would result in the first 8% of gains going to the LPs and 100% of the next 2% going to the GP, allowing the GP to catch up to receiving 20% of the first 10% of gains. After the catch-up, further gains are split 80/20 between the LPs and the GP.

Once the soft hurdle rate is exceeded, 100% of the next €56.471m of returns will be paid to the GP. This enables the GP to catch up to and reach their 15% carried interest split on total returns. Returns in excess of this level generated over the fund's life will be split 15%/85% between the GP and LPs.

## Catch-up to GP:

Hurdle rate / (1 - carried interest split) = returns after which catch-up is complete.


\text{So, } \€320\text{m} / (1 - 0.15) = \€376.471\text{m}.


100% of returns between €376.47m and €320m = €56.471m are paid to the GP. So, €56.471m / €376.47 = 15%. Returns above €376.471m are split in the 15%/85% ratio.

| Fund Returns | LPs | GP |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Up to €320m | €320m | |
| Returns between €320m and €376.471m | | €56.471m |
| Returns between €376.471m and €500m (split 85/15) | €105m | €18.529m |
| Total share | €425m | €75m |
| Share % | €425m / €500m = 85% | €75m / €500m = 15% |

The switch to a soft hurdle increased the GP's carried interest despite a lower carried interest percentage. For the GP, the downside is that the lower carried interest percentage may result in lower carried interest (compared to the hard hurdle) if fund returns are significantly higher than expected.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

There are a couple of points here:

- The catch-up rate could be specified as any rate above the carried interest split of the GP, it does not have to be 100%. However, in the curriculum, all examples have an assumed catch-up rate of 100%. If a question does not specify a catch-up rate, you should assume it to be 100%.
- The calculation of the returns at which the manager catches up (done in this example) only works when the catch-up rate is 100%. This comes from the fact that with a catch-up rate of 100%, the manager catches up when 100% × (return - hurdle) = carried interest % × return.
- Note that if fund returns are high (in this case, above €376.47), then the GP catches up, and we can quickly calculate their carried interest as the carried interest % × total return.

Another feature that may be included in a fee schedule is a high-water mark, which means no performance fee is paid on gains that only offset prior losses. Performance fees are only paid to the extent that the current value of a fund is above the highest net-

Many funds allow the GP to receive carried interest before the end of the fund's life, providing that committed capital has been returned in full to the LPs. A clawback provision stipulates that if the GP accrues or receives carried interest on gains in the early years of the fund that are subsequently reversed by losses, the LPs can recover any carried interest paid to the GP in early years.

## EXAMPLE: Clawback provision

Honey Corporation operates a U.S. venture capital fund with a five-year life and committed capital of $100m. The fund has a hard hurdle rate of 8%, with no compounding. Carried interest is 20% and is paid at the end of each year, once committed capital has been returned to the LPs. A clawback provision is included. The fund makes three investments made at the beginning of each of the first three years. The following table summarizes capital deployment and exit values.
| Investment Year | Investment | Invested Capital | Exit | Exit Value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Start of Year 1 | $40m | End of Year 3 | $100m |
| 2 | Start of Year 2 | $30m | End of Year 4 | $30m |
| 3 | Start of Year 3 | $30m | End of Year 5 | $0 |

Here are the hard hurdles, at end of year:

- Year 3 = $16.8m [($40m × 8% × 3 years) + ($30m × 8% × 2 years) + ($30m × 8% × 1 year)]
- Year 4 = $21.6m [($40m × 8% × 3 years) + ($30m × 8% × 3 years) + ($30m × 8% × 2 years)]
- Year 5 = $24m [($40m × 8% × 3 years) + ($30m × 8% × 3 years) + ($30m × 8% × 3 years)]

Excess return = cash from exited investments, less the original capital:

- Year 3 = $100m - $40m = $60m
- Year 4 = $130m - $70m = $60m
- Year 5 = $130m - $100m = $30m

Here is the cumulative carried interest:

- Year 3 = ($60m - $16.8m) × 20% = $8.64m
- Year 4 = ($60m - $21.6m) × 20% = $7.68m
- Year 5 = ($30m - $24m) × 20% = $1.2m

The first investment was a success and resulted in carried interest amounting to $8.64m being paid. The second investment only generated an exit value equal to its invested capital, resulting in $0.96m ($8.64m - $7.68m) being returned to LPs. The third investment was a failure and written off. This resulted in the return of $6.48m ($7.68m - $1.2m) of carried interest previously paid to the LPs.

## Taxation of Carried Interest

Taxation of carried interest varies according to tax jurisdictions. In the United Kingdom and United States, carried interest is subject to capital gains tax rather than income tax. Capital gains are typically taxed at lower rates than income. Germany, however, taxes carried interest as ordinary income to the GP.

## Additional Risk for LPs

LPs must take care to review sources of potential conflict of interests, such as management service cost agreements between the GP and invested companies. Management service cost agreements allow the GP to claim advisory, consulting, and certain discretionary expenditures from the companies held by the fund.

Another consideration for the LPs is the need to retain undrawn committed capital in liquid investments to meet the GP's future capital calls, which are uncertain in both
amounts and timing. The opportunity cost of keeping funds in liquid public assets before capital deployment must be considered. Most funds measure returns as IRRs on capital deployed (invested capital) rather than committed capital.

## Answer:

Nante's return:


\text{annualized return} = \left[ (1.06)^3 \times (1.18)^9 \right]^{1/12} - 1 = 14.88\%


The delay to the capital call costs Nante 312 bps of return (18% - 14.88%).

The risk that Nante is exposed to is dispersion in the return on the public index fund and overall market volatility. ## Benchmarking

LPs may struggle to match aggregate reported returns of a private asset class for the following three reasons:

1. There is the drag on returns caused by the need to hold liquid investment while waiting for capital calls (demonstrated in the previous example).
2. Illiquidity of private investments makes diversification across GPs and vintage years difficult.
3. Private asset classes display a large dispersion between funds in the top and bottom quartiles.

## MODULE M2.3: PRIVATE INVESTMENT SELECTION AND VALUE CREATION

Video covering this content is available online.

**LOS M2.c: Discuss favorable characteristics of private investment targets and sources of value creation in private markets.**

The active role the GP takes in running the portfolio companies requires investments where a controlling interest or significant influence can be obtained. The GP then uses their specialized skills and experience to create value over the investment holding period.

## Private Equity Value Creation

Private equity funds target investments at different stages of the company life cycle, depending on the fund's strategy and the GP's skill set.

**Start-up companies** are firms without established products or revenues. Funds investing in portfolio companies at this stage of their life cycle are referred to as venture capitalists.

Characteristics of potential start-up company investments are as follows:

- Start-ups in new or emerging markets
- Disrupting existing markets with new products/business models
- Founders with patents and intellectual property, track record, and industry expertise

Value creation is as follows:

- Creating and testing prototypes
- Validating business concept
- Establishing go-to-market strategy
- Leveraging synergies with other portfolio companies
Growth companies are firms with established products and customers, with potential for significant growth, but that are facing capacity constraints.

Characteristics of potential growth company investments are as follows:
- Firms with strong revenue growth relative to their total addressable market
- Products facing high demand
- Firms requiring capital to scale up capacity
- Often minority positions with significant influence

Value creation is as follows:
- Developing sales and marketing initiatives
- Penetrating new markets
- Establishing new management, operational, and manufacturing processes
- Leveraging synergies with other portfolio companies
- Integrating new PP&amp;E and employees
- Developing new products and services

With mature companies, buyout funds target large, mature companies that are either underperforming or undervalued. Due to the size of the targeted investments, significant amounts of debt are used to finance the acquisitions, referred to as leveraged buyouts.

Characteristics of potential mature company investments are as follows:
- Firms with strong asset bases and stable cash flows to service debt
- Companies underperforming, given their potential
- Firms lacking consistent strategic direction
- Typically majority controlling interest

Value creation is as follows:
- Improving operational efficiency
- Divesting noncore operations
- Replacing or adding to management team
- Reducing leverage over the investment horizon

Private funds also operate in the decline phase of the industry cycle. These funds are often referred to as distressed investing and focus on the turnaround of the portfolio companies. This type of fund is addressed in a later reading.

## Key Sources of Value Creation

Value creation will focus on these areas, depending on the fund's strategy and the portfolio company's stage in the industry life cycle (start-up, growth, maturity, decline).

One focus is on strategic areas:
- Divesting noncore operations
- Growth initiatives (products, clients, regions, channels)
- Developing new business lines
- Acquiring business or companies

A second focus is on operational areas:

- Focusing on organic sales and revenue growth
- Improving assets' operating efficiency
- Increasing labor productivity
- Research and development
- Using technology
- Capex to expand capacity

A third focus is on financial areas:

- Improving working capital efficiency
- Optimizing leverage
- Efficiently managing balance sheet cash and receivables
- Improving balance sheet management

## Infrastructure Funds

Infrastructure funds share many characteristics with private property funds. However, the ability to adapt to changes in demand is limited for long-lived, single-use infrastructure funds. With **build-operate-transfer (BOT)** projects, a granting entity (often a public authority) enters a contract with the infrastructure fund, known as a concession agreement. Under the agreement, the fund will build the asset, normally within a contractual time scale, and then operate the asset before eventually transferring ownership back to the granting entity (and often for zero consideration). The focus of the GP is to generate returns for fund investors before ownership of the asset is transferred to the granting entity.

Key drivers of investment attractiveness include the following:

- Greater demand inelasticity for the services provided by the infrastructure asset
- Potential subsidies
- Low exposure to macroeconomic variability
- Ability to control construction costs
- Fixed versus variable price operating contracts
- Availability of ancillary revenue-generating opportunities

Consider a BOT for the construction and operation of a hospital facility. Ancillary revenue stream examples may include parking charges, and the operation of concessions within the hospital such as shops and restaurants.

The GP must perform due diligence on potential target investments to fully understand the risks and potential rewards of any investment and to help formulate the business plan.

## Company Due Diligence

Public fund managers rely on publicly available information to assess whether a company is incorrectly valued (intrinsic value relative to market price) or has growth opportunities. For private markets, the focus is on understanding the opportunity for value creation and formulating a business plan, using both public and nonpublic information.

Due diligence for portfolio company investments takes the following steps:

Step 1: Understand the company's current position.

Step 2: Estimate the potential market.

Step 3: Prioritize the strategy, investments required, and change required; the business plan is designed to close the gap between Steps 1 and 2.

Step 4: Understand key risk factors and potential mitigation of these factors; when looking at risk factors, the GP will consider firm, industry, and macro factors.

MODULE M2.5: PRIVATE INVESTMENT EXIT STRATEGIES

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M2.e: Discuss alternative exit routes in private investments and their impact on value.

In the case of private debt or BOT infrastructure projects, the exit value and timing is known with some certainty. Private debt will be determined by the maturity terms of the debt, and the exit for BOT infrastructure is typically zero at the end of the predetermined contractual life. However, for private equity and real estate, both the amount and timing of investment exits is less certain and has a potentially large impact on the fund's returns.

Private equity funds will consider potential exit routes as part of their overall business plan during initial due diligence. The actual route available at the time of exit will, however, depend on macroeconomic and industry conditions as well as the success of the GP in realizing the potential of the business plan.

For private equity, potential exit routes include the following:

- IPO
- Private sale
- Liquidation

The GP will choose an exit route designed to maximize returns and consider the potential route significantly in advance of exit. External advisors specializing in corporate finance activities are employed by the GP to help in this process.

# Public Sale

An IPO is the issuance of publicly traded shares on an exchange. To be a suitable candidate, the portfolio company will need to have a sufficiently large potential market capitalization, an established track record, and strong growth potential.

Advantages of an IPO include the ability of the GP to exit the investment in stages over time, which provides the company with continuity of its business plan and management team. The portfolio company also gains access to future equity investment via secondary issues.

Disadvantages include the impact of the public equity market booms and busts on the price achieved and the demand for the IPO. The need to employ corporate finance advisors and complying with exchange listing rules can consume up to 7% of the IPO gross proceeds.

The existence of lockout agreements prevent the GP from fully liquidating their investment at the time of the IPO. Instead, the GP liquidates their interest over a period of time; therefore, the GP is dependent on the public equity market remaining robust over this period.
# Private Sale

A private sale can be to a strategic or financial buyer. A strategic buyer seeks to integrate the purchased firm with its existing operations and benefit from synergies. A financial buyer seeks to generate investment returns and does not look for synergies with existing operations. Due to the presence of synergistic gains with existing businesses, a strategic buyer is often prepared to pay more to acquire the firm.

Strategic buyers may be private or public companies, but they can also include the firm's existing management in a management buyout (MBO). With an MBO, the management team agrees to purchase an equity stake in the company alongside a GP in a leveraged buyout (LBO).

A financial buyer will often be either another private equity firm or another fund run by the original GP. Given the finite life of private funds, the GP may seek to retain strongly performing companies by selling them to a new fund (referred to as a continuation fund) in the GP-led secondary market.

# Private Real Estate

The strength of the real estate market is the key driver in both timing and exit values. Provided that a property generates a strong flow of rental income, a real estate investment trust (REIT) is a natural purchaser of a fund's property assets. Some funds may seek to retain income-generating properties if this complements their fund strategy.

# Liquidation

For portfolio investments that are no longer a going concern, liquidation may be the best solution. Assets are sold off in an orderly manner, and creditors are paid off in liquidation order. For start-up companies, asset values may be relatively small, resulting in little value upon liquidation. For buyout funds holding large, established companies, the value of assets held are potentially significant. A liquidation represents a failure of the GP's strategic plan—and as a result, normally investors will realize a loss on their investment. The order of priority in a liquidation means that debtholders will receive returns before equity holders.

## Key Concepts

### LOS M2.a

The GP defines the fund’s strategy and identifies and invests in portfolio companies (investments). The GP then deploys value creation strategies to increase the value of investments before exiting and distributing returns to the LPs toward the end of the fund’s life.

The private asset investment life cycle can be described by the following terms:

- Precommitment. The GP outlines the fund’s strategy and proposed value creation based on the fund’s specialized skill set and experience.

- Capital commitment. Unfunded capital commitments, which may take the form of debt or equity, are secured by attracting investment from LPs.

- A limited partnership agreement is negotiated between the LPs and GP detailing the following:

- Rights of both parties
- Terms and conditions
- Fee structures

- Capital deployment. The GP finalizes due diligence on target investments. A detailed business and financing plan for each portfolio company is created, detailing the sources of value creation and how they will be realized. The GP bids on potential targets and calls down equity and debt finance to fund the purchases.

- Capital distribution and exit. The GP exits the fund’s investments toward the end of the fund’s life after the value creation process has been implemented, and the GP returns the proceeds to the LPs. Exit routes can include initial public offerings
(IPOs), trade sales, and liquidations (for failed investments). The proceeds generated from exits are uncertain, depending on the success of the GP in implementing the value creation plan.

## LOS M2.b

Management fees are designed to cover the operating costs of the fund. Private funds base management fees on a percentage of committed capital. Typical management fees equate to 1% for private debt and 2% for private equity.

Management fees are based initially on committed capital rather than deployed capital (invested capital). Once the fund has fully deployed capital, management fees are based on committed capital less the cumulative cost of investments exited or liquidated (net invested capital).

Carried interest consists of performance-related fees paid to the GP to incentivize and reward the GP for successful performance.

Carried interest is paid either at periodic intervals once committed capital has been distributed back to LPs, or alternatively, at the end of the fund's predetermined life. Carried interest typically ranges between 15% and 20% of returns above a hurdle rate. Hurdle rates of 7%-8% are common for private funds.

With a hard hurdle, the GP receives their carried interest percentage on returns that exceed the hurdle. These are often used when a fund's returns are predictable, or if the carried interest is only paid at the end of the fund's life.

With a soft hurdle, the GP receives carried interest on total returns of the fund. A common method of implementing a soft hurdle is to use a catch-up clause.

Returns below the hurdle = 100% to LPs.

Returns between hurdle and hurdle / (1 - carried interest %) = 100% GP.

Returns above hurdle / (1 - carried interest %) = split LPs and GP (80:20).

A high-water mark is used to avoid paying performance fees on gains that only offset prior losses. Performance fees are only paid to the extent that the current value of a fund is above the highest net-of-fees value previously recorded (at the end of a payment period). This feature ensures that LPs will not be charged performance fees twice on the same gains in their portfolio values.

LPs need to retain undrawn committed capital in liquid investments to meet the GP's future capital calls, which are uncertain in both amounts and timing. The opportunity cost of keeping funds in liquid public assets before capital deployment reduces potential returns.

## LOS M2.e

Value creation will focus on these areas, depending on the fund's strategy and the portfolio companies' stage in the industry life cycle (start-up, growth, maturity, decline).

One focus is on strategic areas:
- Divesting noncore operations
- Growth initiatives (products, clients, regions, channels)
- Developing new business lines
- Acquiring business or companies

A second focus is on operational areas:
- Focusing on organic sales and revenue growth
- Improving assets' operating efficiency
- Increasing labor productivity
- Research and development
- Using technology
- Capex to expand capacity

A third focus is on financial areas:
- Improving working capital efficiency
- Optimizing leverage
- Efficiently managing balance sheet cash and receivables
- Improving balance sheet management

Real estate target investment involves purchasing raw or developed land with the potential for adding value from the construction of buildings. A key driver of the value added will be specific to the type of property being constructed.

Key drivers of investment attractiveness include the following:
- Location and suitability
- Supply (existing and future construction) of the type of property proposed in proximity to the location considered
- Local, regional, and global macroeconomic conditions

Infrastructure funds share many characteristics with private property funds; however, the ability to adapt to changes in demand is limited for long-lived, single-use infrastructure funds.

Key drivers of investment attractiveness include the following:
- Greater demand inelasticity for the services provided by the infrastructure asset
- Potential subsidies
- Low exposure to macroeconomic variability
- Ability to control construction costs
- Fixed versus variable price operating contracts
- Availability of ancillary revenue-generating opportunities

Additionally, BOT = buy, operate, transfer.
LOS M2.d

Due diligence for portfolio company investments involves the following steps:

Step 1: Understand the company's current position.

Step 2: Estimate the potential market.

Step 3: Prioritize the strategy, investments required, and change required; the business plan is designed to close the gap between Steps 1 and 2.

Step 4: Understand key risk factors and potential mitigation of these factors; when looking at risk factors, the GP will consider firm, industry, and macro factors.

With start-up companies, focus on product viability and the steps that need to be taken to launch a viable product.

With buyout and growth, focus on financial performance.

LOS M2.e

For private debt or BOT infrastructure projects, the exit value and timing is known with some certainty. For private equity and real estate, both the amount and timing of investment exits is less certain and has a potentially large impact on the fund's returns.

For private equity, potential exit routes include the following:

- IPO
- Private sale
- Liquidation

The GP will choose an exit route designed to maximize returns and consider the potential route significantly in advance of exit.

# Venture Capital (VC)

Institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals usually access VC investments by committing capital as a limited partner (LP) to a closed-end fund. Investment decisions are made by the general partner (GP), a team of investment managers from a
PE investment firm. Note that the GP does not immediately draw down all the committed capital to the fund for investment. Instead, the fund will draw down capital from LPs over time on a cycle that is typically as follows:

- Drawdown phase. Drawdowns in the early years of the fund's life are primarily used to make **seed capital** equity investments in VC companies, financing the creation of a viable product and executing a "go-to-market" strategy to begin generating revenues. For the VC investor (i.e., the GP of the fund), the focus here is on technical knowledge of the company's product and market, and the experience and quality of the founders and their business plan.

- Investment phase. Due to the new and unpredictable nature of the investee VC company, failure rates are expected to be high. Businesses in the portfolio that do succeed will require more equity capital in subsequent years to meet market demand, referred to as **series financing** (or **follow-on financing**). This often occurs in multiple rounds known as "Series A," "Series B," and so on. In the later rounds of series financing, VC investors shift their focus away from nonfinancial milestones such as establishing product, and toward milestones linked to revenue growth and other financial metrics. Founders and seed investors in the VC company may have their interests in the VC company diluted by these later rounds of financing unless they contribute more investment.

- Returns harvesting. In the later years of the fund, the GP exits investments and distributes capital and returns to the limited partners.

The seed funding stage described previously is often preceded by **pre-seed funding** from the founder, their family, and friends, used to establish proof of concept and product prototypes over a relatively short time frame (usually less than a year). VC funds are not usually involved at this stage. At the seed and series financing stages, VC funds are often investing alongside **angel investors**, high-net-worth individuals (often entrepreneurs) who invest directly in VC companies.

VC investors are highly selective in their investments. For each 100 potential start-ups they review, they often only perform full due diligence on 10, of which only 1 will be selected for investment. (This is referred to as the **100/10/1 rule of thumb**.)

## Growth Equity (GE)

While VC investments are primarily made in the form of minority stakes in common equity, GE investments are often made in the form of convertible preferred shares (CPS). These securities rank senior to common equity in the capital structure of the investee firm in terms of dividend payment (which is usually fixed for preferred shares) and in the event of liquidation. They usually also have **liquidation preference value**, a redemption value on exit that is based on a preferred rate of return or multiple of invested capital. CPS investors will convert their preference shares into common shares when the value of the common shares they will receive is higher than this liquidation preference value, as demonstrated in the following example.
# EXAMPLE: Convertible preferred and common shares value vs. exit value

A GE company is financed as follows:

- 2 million CPS are issued at a price of USD 25 per share, with the following features:

- Liquidation preference value is $2 \times$ initial capital raised
- Conversion ratio of 3 common equity shares per convertible preferred share

12 million common shares are issued at a price of USD 5 per share.

Calculate the implied common equity share price at exit above which CPS investors will choose to convert to common shares (referred to as the optimal conversion point).

# Answer:

The initial proceeds from issuing 2 million CPS at a price of USD 25 per share are $2,000,000 \times \text{USD } 25 = \text{USD } 50$ million.

The liquidation preference value is $2 \times$ these initial proceeds: USD 50 million $\times 2 =$ USD 100 million.

CPS investors have the right to convert each of their preferred shares into 3 common equity shares, and therefore, they will own 2 million $\times$ 3 = 6 million common shares if they convert.

They will choose to convert when the value of their common shares is greater than the liquidation preference value of the preference shares:

- 6 million $\times$ common equity share price $&gt;$ USD 100 million
- Common equity share price $&gt;$ USD 100 million / 6 million = USD 16.67

This common equity share price corresponds with a common equity exit value of USD $16.67 \times 12$ million shares = USD 200 million, and a total equity exit value of USD 300 million when we include the value of the preference shares.

The optimal conversion point can be formulated and quickly calculated on a per-share basis as follows:

optimal conversion point per common share

## Buyout Equity (BO)

The target investments of BO investors differ from VC and GE investments in that they are larger, well-established companies with a management team who is failing to maximize value. Due to the mature nature of the business, BO investors can use more conventional valuation techniques involving historical financial statements that are often not applicable to VC or GE companies. Often, the post-LBO management will also have a (noncontrolling) stake in the company to align the interests of management and the BO firm, addressing the issue of agency costs.

LBO opportunities typically take one of three forms:

1. Take-private transactions. A public company with attractive market value and little management ownership offers potential for cost cutting and efficiency improvements.
2. Corporate divestures. The target is a division of a conglomerate, but it has few synergies with the parent and insufficient funding for the division, with potential efficiency improvements or cost-cutting opportunities.
3. Private company sales. The target is a private company that may have insufficient access to funding. These transactions may feature the ability to negotiate on price as well as potential efficiency improvements or cost-cutting opportunities.

The mechanics of a typical buyout deal involve the PE buyout fund (known as the financial sponsor of the deal) creating a new company (referred to here as "AqnCo") for the purpose of acquiring the target company ("TgtCo"). The deal itself follows a two-step process:

Step 1: AqnCo uses equity capital investment from the financial sponsor and other short-term borrowings to purchase the equity of TgtCo and pay fees to any advisors used in organizing the transaction. The change of ownership of TgtCo will likely trigger the requirement to repay its existing debt; hence, TgtCo will also likely take out short-term loans to replace this capital.

Step 2: Once the deal closes, AqnCo and TgtCo are merged. The post-LBO merged company raises new medium-term to long-term capital and uses it to repay any short-term loans raised in Step 1.

After the deal, the BO investor sets about making operational improvements at the company and uses the improved cash flows of the company to pay down the debt. If successful, the valuation of the company should improve upon exit, which will also be a source of return to the BO investor.

An LBO transaction is different to a dividend recapitalization of a company, where a company increases debt to fund a special dividend to existing shareholders. Both types of transaction increase the level of debt, but a dividend recapitalization is designed to decrease the firm's weighted average cost of capital due to the lower cost of debt versus cost of equity. In contrast, under an LBO, the investor has an action plan to improve operations and reduce debt levels after the deal to generate returns.

Venture Capital (VC) Valuation

We will demonstrate the VC method for valuing a VC investment through an example.

EXAMPLE: VC method for valuing VC—Series A financing

ExampleCo was launched two years ago by issuing 1 million shares to the founders at USD 5 per share. Since then, the company has successfully developed a product
and begun generating revenues. The company is now undergoing a Series A fund raising from a VC investor with the following details:

- New capital from Series A VC investor = USD 4 million
- Expected revenue in six years on exit = USD 100 million
- Expected price/sales valuation on exit = 2.5×
- Return on investment multiple (ROI) required by Series A investor = 20×

## Calculate each of the following:

1. The value of ExampleCo after the Series A financing (post-money valuation—combination of the pre-money valuation and new equity invested)
2. The value of the ExampleCo before the financing (pre-money valuation—implied value before an incremental new equity investment)
3. The price of the shares issued under the Series A financing
4. The number of shares issued to the Series A investor
5. The fractional ownership of founders and Series A investors after the deal

## Answer:

1. Given an expected revenue at exit in six years of USD 100 million and expected price/sales multiple of 2.5x, the expected exit value is USD 100 million × 2.5 = USD 250 million. With a required ROI of 20x, this implies a post-deal valuation today (post-money valuation) of USD 250 million / 20 = USD 12.5 million.
2. Pre-money valuation = post-money valuation - new Series A equity
= USD 12.5 million - USD 4 million
= USD 8.5 million.
3. Price of shares = pre-money valuation / shares in issuance before Series A financing
= USD 8.5 million / 1 million = USD 8.5 per share.
4. Number of shares issued to Series A investor = new equity / share price = USD 4 million / USD 8.5 = 470,588.
5. Fractional ownership of Series A investor = new equity / post-money valuation = USD 4 million / USD 12.5 million = 0.32, or 32%. Fractional ownership of founder post-deal = 100% - 32% = 68%.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

This example uses multiple formulas that are stated in the curriculum. Focus on understanding the steps of the process rather than learning the formulas. If you understand the process, the formulas simply become placeholders for each stage. These formulas are listed in the Key Concepts at the end of this reading for reference. Be aware that ROI here refers to a multiple (i.e., end value / start value) rather than an annual percentage return or IRR. For a general investment horizon of $n$ years with no interim cashflows, ROI = $(1 + \text{IRR})^n$; hence, we can quickly calculate IRR = ROI^(1/n) - 1. In this case, IRR = $20^{(1/6)} - 1 = 0.6475$, or 64.75%.
Young firms often offer employees stock options as part of their compensation package to attract and retain the best talent. These options will be dilutive to VC investors if exercised, as demonstrated in the following example.

## EXAMPLE: VC method for valuing VC, with employee stock option dilution

Assume that ExampleCo, the start-up undergoing the Series A financing in the previous example, had granted its management the right to buy 100,000 shares at an exercise price of USD 5. Calculate the impact on fractional ownership and valuation of the company on exit if ExampleCo reaches its target valuation and all options are exercised.

**Answer:**

If the options are exercised, then 100,000 extra shares would be issued to management. These shares, alongside the 1 million shares held by founders and 470,588 shares issued to Series A investors calculated in the previous example, increase the total number of shares issued in the VC company to 1,570,588.

- Founder's fractional ownership = 1,000,000 / 1,570,588 = 63.67%
- Series A investor's fractional ownership = 470,588 / 1,570,588 = 29.96%
- Management's fractional ownership = 100% - 63.67% - 29.96% = 6.37%

When the management exercises the options, the management transfers the exercise proceeds of 100,000 × USD 5 = USD 500,000 to the firm, increasing its value at t = 6 from the USD 250 million calculated in the previous example to USD 250.5 million.

This example makes some simplifying assumptions. The impact of the stock options on company valuation during the Series A financing is ignored, and it is assumed the options are exercised on exit.

- New capital from Series B VC investor = USD 10 million
- Return on investment multiple (ROI) required by Series B investor = 5 ×

## GE Valuation

Recall that GE investors look to invest in companies that have successfully navigated the venture start-up stage and now need capital to grow to meet market demand. ## EXAMPLE: Valuing growth equity with convertible preferred shares

Assume that ExampleCo completes the seed and series financing rounds in previous examples and has no management stock options. In its eighth year of operations, ExampleCo generates revenue of USD 90 million (less than the original target of USD 100 million) and looks to raise GE to help the company meet market demand. Details of the GE financing are as follows:

- New capital from GE investor = USD 20 million
- Liquidation preference payout: 2× initial investment at exit in four years
- Conversion ratio: three common shares per preferred share
- Convertible preferred share price: USD 200
1. Calculate the optimal conversion value for the GE investor on a total equity, common equity, and per common share basis.
2. Calculate the fractional ownership of the GE investor, should they choose to convert their preferred shares into common shares.
3. Describe the three different scenarios of expected payoff to the GE investor at exit.

Answer:

1. Recall from earlier examples that the number of common shares outstanding after VC seed and series financing rounds was 1,838,235.

The number of convertible preferred shares (CPS) issued to the GE investor = new equity / CPS price = USD 20 million / USD 200 = 100,000.

The liquidation value to GE investors is $2 \times$ USD 20 million = USD 40 million. GE investors will receive this if they choose not to convert their CPS into common shares.

With a conversion ratio of 3, the CPS are convertible into $3 \times 100,000 = 300,000$ new common shares.

GE investors will choose to convert into common shares at exit if the price of the common shares that they receive is greater than their liquidation value per new common share (i.e., USD 40 million / 300,000 = USD 133.33). This is the optimal conversion value per share.

With 1,838,235 common shares already issued, this implies an optimal conversion value for common equity of $1,838,235 \times \text{USD } 133.33 = \text{USD } 245,098,000$.

Adding the liquidation value of the CPS, this implies an optimal conversion value for total equity as $245,098,000 + 40,000,000 = \text{USD } 285,098,000$.

2. On conversion, the fractional ownership of the GE investor is calculated as follows: $300,000 / (1,838,235 + 300,000) = 14.0\%$.
3. If the total equity value at exit is less than the liquidation value of USD 40 million, the GE investor will choose not to convert and will receive all the value of the company due to their seniority over common shareholders. In this scenario, common shareholders receive nothing.

If the total equity value at exit is between the liquidation value of USD 40 million and the optimal conversion value of USD 285,098,000, the GE investor will choose not to convert and will receive their liquidation value. Common shareholders receive any excess above liquidation value.

If the total equity value at exit is greater than the optimal conversion value of USD 285,098,000, the GE investor will choose to convert and will receive 300,000 common shares worth more than USD 133.33 each, holding a fractional ownership of $14\%$. Common shareholders will own the other $86\%$ of common shares.

# Leveraged Buyout Equity (LBO) Valuation

Recall that LBO investors take a controlling stake in the investee company using significant amounts of debt capital. Returns to the buyout firm's equity stake come from:

- operational improvements that increase the company's earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA);
- paydown of debt; and
- increases in valuation as the company becomes more profitable.

Target investee companies are usually mature with a well-established record of financial statements from which to perform investment analysis, but with management teams that are not maximizing shareholder value. The next example demonstrates the method used to analyze an LBO deal.

# EXAMPLE: The LBO method

An LBO investor considers taking a controlling stake in Laggard Corp in an LBO deal. The details of the deal are as follows:

- Laggard currently trades in the public market at an EV/EBITDA multiple of $5 \times$ . The LBO investor expects to execute the deal at this valuation.
- The LBO will be financed with $70\%$ debt and $30\%$ equity.
- EBITDA is currently USD 500 million, and it is forecast to grow at $4\%$ per year after the deal.
- Debt can be reduced by $25\%$ over the next six years, at which time the LBO investor expects to exit.
- The exit EV/EBITDA multiple is expected to be $6 \times$ .
Calculate each of the following:

1. The equity investment made by the LBO investor in Laggard Corp
2. The expected ROI and IRR of the deal, assuming no interim distributions to investors and that debt can be repaid at par value

Answer:

1. Current enterprise value (EV) of Laggard = EBITDA × EV/EBITDA = USD 500 million × 5 = USD 2,500 million, or USD 2.5 billion.

This is financed by debt capital of 0.70 × USD 2.5 billion = USD 1.75 billion and equity capital (which is the LBO investor's entry value), equal to 0.30 × USD 2.5 billion = USD 750 million.

2. EBITDA at exit in six years' time after growing at 4% per year = USD 500 million × 1.04⁶ = USD 632,659,509.

The exit EV at an expected EV multiple of 6 × = USD 632,659,509 × 6 = USD 3,795,957,055.

The value of outstanding debt after reduction by 25% = USD 1.75 billion × 0.75 = USD 1.3125 billion.

The exit value of equity = exit EV - exit value of debt = 3,795,957,055 - 1,312,500,000 = USD 2,483,457,055.

Hence, ROI = exit equity value / initial equity value = 2,483,457,055 / 750,000,000 = 3.31 ×

So, IRR = 3.31(1/6) - 1 = 22.1%.

The EBITDA could be forecast by projecting sales growth and subtracting cost of goods sold (COGS) and sales, general, and administrative expenses (SGA) based on their expected future proportion of sales once costs have been reduced. The next example demonstrates this approach.

EXAMPLE: Projecting EBITDA through sales, COGS, and SGA

Consider the following scenario:

- The current sales of the LBO target company = USD 100 million.
- The expected sales growth for the next five years: 6%.
- Current COGS are 60% of sales and are expected to decline by two percentage points per year (i.e., COGS will be 58% next year).
- SGA is 30% of sales and is expected to decrease by 1% per year, starting in Year 3, to be 27% in Year 5.
- Depreciation in Year 5 is expected to be USD 10 million and is included within COGS.
Calculate the expected EBITDA on exit in Year 5.

Answer:

Sales in Year 5 at exit are projected to be USD 100 million × 1.06⁵ = USD 133.82 million.

COGS as a proportion of sales in Year 5 = 60% - (5 × 2%) = 50%.

SGA as a proportion of sales in Year 5 = 27%.

EBIT in Year 5 = USD 133.82 million × (1 - 0.50 - 0.27) = USD 30.78 million.

Add back depreciation to get to EBITDA = USD 30.78 million + USD 10 million = USD 40.78 million.

These kinds of projections have been featured in equity valuation and corporate securities at Levels I and II. There is nothing new here.

All of these examples have assumed a single forecast for the key drivers of exit value returns of a PE deal (e.g., sales, EBITDA). In practice, an analyst will want to conduct a series of scenario analyses that project varying assumptions for debt reduction, cost reduction, sales growth, and valuation expansion, to understand the key factors driving the risk and return of the deal. In particular, analysts will be interested in a downside scenario where revenues decline and planned cost cutting and debt reduction are not implemented at the investee firm. In such a scenario, if exit EV is less than the debt outstanding at exit, the investee company would be insolvent and all equity investment would be worthless.

## PE Risk and Return

PE investors can look to public equity market data as a starting point for assessing the relative risks and returns of PE. While the specialist nature of strategies used by PE managers should provide some diversification against the returns of public securities in normal market conditions, adverse market conditions usually decrease valuations of both public and PE. Thus, correlations increase and diversification benefits decrease in times of stress.

PE is generally seen as a riskier investment than public equity for the following reasons:

- Liquidity risk. Opportunities for LPs to exit an investment in a PE fund during its 7- to 10-year lifetime depend on the illiquid secondary market for PE funds, where they often trade at steep discounts to NAV.
- Valuation risk. The appraisal-based nature of PE investment valuation—along with less frequent valuations in private markets than public markets—can lead to valuations being smoothed, making volatility and correlations with other asset classes appear artificially low.
- Agency risk. The limited partner investor in a PE fund faces the principal-agent conflict issue when relying on a GP to manage the fund, in that the GP may act in their own interests rather than the interest of LPs. LPs may also find it more difficult to obtain information on environmental, social, and governance (ESG) issues for investments in a PE fund, relative to other asset classes.
- Extraordinary operating and business risks. These risks are present in PE investments compared to established successful public companies.
- Interest rate and leverage risks. There are these risks for LBO investments.
- Macroeconomic risks. There are macroeconomic risks related to achieving a profitable exit valuation.
- Dilution risk. This is the potential for a subsequent series of financing rounds to dilute early VC investments.

In addition to these risks, investors in PE funds often face high management fees, incentive (performance) fees, fees for arranging private transactions, fund setup costs, and administrative charges.

The risks of PE can be managed through diversification by vintage year, geography, industry, and investment strategy. LPs can also manage risk with thorough due diligence of the GP, the limited partnership structure, and co-investing alongside the GP in private assets.

## KEY CONCEPTS

### LOS M3.a

There are three major types of private equity (PE) strategy:

1. Venture capital (VC) has minority equity stakes in start-up companies.
2. Growth equity (GE) has minority equity stakes in established companies with significant growth potential.
3. Leveraged buyout (LBO) has controlling stakes in mature businesses at which management is not maximizing shareholder value.

### LOS M3.b

The VC fund life cycle typically has three stages:

1. The drawdown phase is where seed capital is used to establish new businesses.
2. The investment phase is where series financing (follow-on financing) is provided to portfolio companies that met investment milestones.
3. The returns harvesting phase is where the fund exits profitable investments and distributes capital and returns to fund investors.

Before seed capital is provided by VC funds, pre-seed financing is often provided by the business founder, their family, and friends, to establish proof of concept for the business.

GE investments can be made in the form of common shares or convertible preferred shares (CPS). Investors will choose to convert their CPS into common equity when the value of the company exceeds the optimal conversion point (OCP). The payoff to CPS investors on exit is as follows.

# LOS M3.c

Leveraged buyouts typically invest in private company sales, corporate divestures, or take-private transactions and involve significant debt capital. The mechanics of an LBO involve two phases.

1. In the initial phase, the buyout firm establishes a legally distinct corporation created to acquire the target company. Short-term loans are used to finance the purchase of shares from target company shareholders, and by the target company to replace any loans that must be redeemed due to the change in ownership.
2. In the refinancing phase, the acquiring company and target company are merged, and short-term loans are replaced by longer-term debt capital.

# LOS M3.d

The VC method calculates the return expected from a VC investment. Exit values are usually based on projected sales growth and expected price/sales ratio on exit. Key calculations include the following:

post-money valuation $= \frac{\text{exit value of equity}}{\text{ROI multiple}}$

pre-money valuation $=$ post-money valuation - new equity

price of shares issued on the deal $=$ pre-money valuation shares in issuance before the deal

Sources of dilution to early VC investors include the granting of employee share options, follow-on series financing rounds, and issuance of convertible preferred shares

The GE method is similar to the VC method; however, it must reflect the conversion decision of any CPS investors on exit.

If the CPS are converted, the amount of common shares outstanding increases by the number of CPS × conversion ratio.

1. Operational improvements increasing EBITDA
2. Paydown of debt over the investment horizon
3. Increase in EBITDA multiples as valuation improves

The exit value for LBO investments is usually estimated as a multiple of EBITDA, which itself can be forecast through expected sales growth and expected margins.

## LOS M3.e

While PE should diversify public securities due to the specialized strategies that are used, deteriorating macroeconomic conditions usually hurt private and public equity valuations at the same time, causing increased correlations at times of market stress.

Risks that are often elevated in PE markets versus public equity markets include liquidity, valuation, agency, operational, interest rate, leverage, macroeconomic, and dilution risks.

The term **private debt** refers to loans and bonds used by private companies and other privately funded projects (e.g., private real estate and infrastructure development projects) to raise debt capital.

In contrast to the unsecured fixed-coupon bonds typically issued by mature public investment-grade companies, private debt is often secured due to the riskier nature of private non-investment-grade issuers; may include prepayment options to give the borrower financing flexibility; or offers equity conversion rights to offer higher returns to investors when equity valuations rise and to decrease the coupon paid by the issuer.

# Venture Debt

Venture debt investing involves lending to venture capital (VC) companies in the startup phase of the company life cycle. Venture borrowers are typically already generating revenue, but they have negative cash flow and few assets other than potential intellectual property. However, they may have large cash balances from a VC private equity sponsor. Typically, the loans are relatively short term (up to three years) and are fully amortizing; they also have loan-to-value ratios of no more than $10\%$ and high interest rates due to the risky nature of VC companies.

Venture debt loans are usually structured with warrants, giving the investor the right to subscribe to new shares in the VC company at a fixed price, typically over several years. This can give venture debt equity-like returns if the value of the company increases significantly. This also reduces the cash coupon paid by the VC company and avoids the immediate dilution that would result from an equity share issue.

A venture company with established subscription-based revenues could use these cash flows to repay a venture debt loan, referred to as recurring revenue financing. This type of investment is similar to receivables financing (factoring) by more established companies that raise finance that is repaid by the future collection of unpaid invoices or other receivables.

# Mezzanine Debt

Mezzanine debt generally refers to long-term subordinated debt issued by private companies in the growth and mature phases of the company life cycle. It ranks below other classes of debt, but it is higher than equity (hence the name mezzanine). It is usually smaller in value than other types of growth/mature private company financing and is highly negotiable between the borrower and the lender. Mezzanine debt includes equity-linked features such as warrants or equity conversion rights—with interest that
may be paid in cash or payment-in-kind (PIK), where interest is added to outstanding principal. We will discuss these investments in more detail later in this module.

## Convertible Debt

Convertible debt is fixed-income securities that offer the investor the right to convert their debt into equity at a predetermined ratio. They are typically issued by companies in the growth stage. We will discuss these investments in more detail later in this module.

## Leveraged Loans

Leveraged loans (LLs) are senior secured floating-rate loans made to non-investment-grade companies that typically already have high debt levels. The loans are usually callable at par soon after issuance, providing borrowers with flexibility in their financing arrangements.

Other features of LLs include the following:

- Security is usually in the form of a first or second lien on specified assets of the borrower.
- Restrictive covenants are common, either in the form of **maintenance covenants** that must always be met, or **incurrence covenants** that are tested only when the issuer seeks to raise more debt (usually to place a limit of the amount of debt the company can issue). An incurrence covenant is an example of a situation where strong demand from investors for loans can lead to less strict covenants (referred to as a covenant-lite transaction).
- Investors usually access LLs through collateralized loan obligations (CLOs) that hold the LLs as collateral.

## High-Yield Bonds

High-yield (HY) bonds are subordinated fixed-coupon instruments issued by non-investment-grade companies, typically in the mature phase of the company life cycle. These bonds are usually callable by the issuer at a premium over par, but usually after a fixed **call protection period** to protect the investor against call risk in the early years of the bond's life.

## Direct Lending

Rather than being a type of investment instrument, **direct lending** refers to the situation where a private *nonbank* lender invests directly in the private company. These lenders may be specialized private credit funds; **business development companies (BDCs)**, which are a type of publicly listed closed-end investment company in the United States; or other types of institutional investors with specialist private credit expertise.

Direct lending by nonbank institutions increased dramatically after the global financial crisis of 2008–2009 due to a combination of tightening regulations (causing banks to
make fewer credit-risky loans) and zero-rate policies by central banks (pushing investors into new markets in search of yield).

Direct lending typically involves mezzanine debt or **unitranche debt**, which offers exposure to different types of instruments in one facility. We will discuss unitranche securities in more detail later in this module. LLs, while typically underwritten by a banking syndicate, can also be used in direct lending investments.

Direct loans to a company that has a private equity firm as a controlling shareholder are referred to as **sponsored loans** (recall that a private equity investor is often referred to as a financial sponsor of the private equity company). Direct lenders will typically deal with the sponsor when acquiring information and performing due diligence on their investment. Relative to a nonsponsored loan, this will likely decrease search, due diligence, and monitoring costs for the direct lender. The drawback is less bargaining power when negotiating with a financial sponsor versus negotiating the loan directly with the borrower—typically resulting in less favorable terms and pricing (and hence, returns) than a nonsponsored loan.

## Project-Related Debt

Outside of private companies, private debt is also often used in private real estate and infrastructure development projects.

In real estate development, **construction and development (C&amp;D) loans** are drawn down by project managers and used in the early years of the project to construct the real estate. Once complete, the C&amp;D loan is repaid through a mortgage secured on the property.

In **build-operate-transfer (BOT)** infrastructure projects, loans are used to construct an infrastructure asset (e.g., a railway or water treatment facility), and the loan is repaid through cash flows earned from operating the infrastructure. Once the operating phase is complete and debt has been repaid, the asset is usually transferred to the public authority that initiated the infrastructure project.

We discuss infrastructure investing in more detail in a separate module, as well as **distressed debt** investing, which entails lending to companies that are likely to file for bankruptcy protection or already have.

# Features of Leveraged Loans (LLs)

Recall that LLs are senior secured floating-rate loans made to non-investment-grade companies—typically, mature companies that already have high levels of leverage.

The coupon rate on a leveraged loan is a floating risk-free benchmark market reference rate (MRR) plus a fixed margin, referred to as the quoted margin (QM), based on the credit risk of the issuer relative to the MRR at issuance. The values of floating-rate instruments are more stable than those of similar maturity fixed-rate debt because the MRR component of the coupon rises and falls in line with benchmark rates. The rate used to settle a coupon payment at the end of the period is set at the beginning of the period, in the same way that interest rates on loans are agreed at the start of loan periods but paid at the end.

The required return of investors above the MRR is referred to as the discount margin (DM). At issuance, the QM paid is set to the DM of investors, and the loan is issued at par. If the credit quality of the issuer decreases after issuance, investors will demand a higher margin above MRR in compensation for increased credit risk. As a result, the fixed QM will be less than the DM, and the loan will be valued at a discount to par. If credit quality improves, the fixed QM will be greater than the DM, and the loan will be valued at a premium.

# PROFESSOR'S NOTE

This is analogous to the relationship between the coupon and yield for fixed-coupon instruments. The MRR + QM plays the role of coupon, and MRR + DM plays the role of yield. This idea has already been discussed in previous levels of the CFA program when looking at floating-rate notes (FRNs).

# EXAMPLE: LL valuation

A USD 100 million leveraged loan with a semiannual coupon pays benchmark MRR plus a QM of 250 basis points. It was issued at par when yield curves and the term structure of spreads were flat and benchmark rates were at 4%. On a reset date with five years remaining to maturity, both the MRR and the credit spread of the issuer have risen by 1%.

Demonstrate the impact of the following (independently) on the value of the leveraged loan:
1. The increase in MRR
2. The increase in credit spread

Answer:

1. When the MRR increases by 1% (from 4% to 5%), the loan will continue to trade at par because the coupon (MRR + QM) will rise by 1%, offsetting the increase in the discount rate (MRR + DM). The loan will continue to pay a coupon equal to the discount rate—and the loan will, therefore, be worth par.

Shown numerically using a face value of USD 100, 10 semiannual coupon payments of (MRR + QM) / 2 = (5% + 2.5%) / 2 = 3.75%, and a discount rate per period of (MRR + DM) / 2 = (5% + 2.5%) / 2 = 3.75%, we calculate the value of the loan as follows:


N = 10; I/Y = 3.75; FV = 100; PMT = 3.75; CPT PV = -100


2. When the DM increases by 1% (from 2.5% to 3.5%), we know that the loan will fall in value, because the coupon (MRR + QM) will remain the same while the discount rate (MRR + DM) will increase by 1%. When QM &lt; DM, the loan is valued at a discount to par.

Shown numerically using a face value of USD 100, 10 semiannual coupon payments of (MRR + QM) / 2 = (4% + 2.5%) / 2 = 3.25%, and a discount rate per period of (MRR + DM) / 2 = (4% + 3.5%) / 2 = 3.75%, we calculate the value of the loan as follows:


N = 10; I/Y = 3.75; FV = 100; PMT = 3.25; CPT PV = -95.89


The key takeaway, here, is that while leveraged loan investors face little interest rate risk due to the floating nature of the MRR, they still have significant credit spread risk due to the fixed nature of the QM.

While LLs are callable at par soon after issuance, early repayment of fixed-rate non-callable bonds usually requires a tender process, where the issuer makes an offer to bondholders to repurchase debt at a premium to par (which they can accept or reject). For highly rated bonds, the premium may be set by valuing the bond at rates close to sovereign yields (referred to as a make-whole provision), resulting in a price significantly above the fair value of the bond—making the early retirement of debt unattractive for the issuer.

PROFESSOR'S NOTE

This tender process is separate to the call provision usually embedded in high-yield bonds, described next. The distinction is that the tender process initiated by the issuer described previously is voluntary for bondholders, whereas a call provision gives the issuer the right to repurchase the bonds later in the bond's life, which cannot be refused by bondholders.
# Features of High-Yield (HY) Bonds

Relative to LLs, fixed-coupon HY bonds are typically more standardized, less negotiable, and offer less refinancing flexibility to the borrower.

HY bonds are usually callable by the issuer at a premium over par after a period of call protection lasting the first few years of the bond's life. This premium is likely to decline toward par over the remaining life of the bond.

Recall from previous levels of the CFA program that the value of a callable bond can be modeled as follows:


\text{callable bond value} = \text{straight bond value} - \text{call option value}


Because the call is held by the issuer, it decreases the value of the callable bond versus an equivalent straight bond.

Recall also that callable bonds can be valued using interest rate binomial trees. This approach can be adapted to assess whether a callable bond is likely to be called by the issuer.

For example, assume an investor holds a five-year, $6\%$ coupon, annual-pay bond priced at par (hence, yielding $6\%$), which is callable at a price of 101 from one year's time onward. The investor models that the bond's yield could evolve to be $6.598\%$ or $5.402\%$ with equal probability in one year's time (when the bond will have four years to maturity), as seen in Figure M4.3.

![img-7.jpeg](img-7.jpeg)
Figure M4.3: Binomial Tree

The valuations of the bond at Time 1 are calculated as follows:

- $V_{1,u}$: 4 N; 6.598 I/Y; 6 PMT; 100 FV; CPT PV: -97.96
- $V_{1,d}$: 4 N; 5.402 I/Y; 6 PMT; 100 FV; CPT PV: -102.10

Under the decreasing yield branch, the bond price becomes higher than the call price of 101 and the analyst would model the bond as being called, replacing the value of 102.10 in the node with a value of 101.

Higher rate volatility assumptions would increase the value of the short call option embedded in the bond (because all options are directly related to the volatility of the underlying asset), which would decrease the value of the callable bond versus an equivalent straight bond.
PROFESSOR'S NOTE

These trees are a simplification of the interest rate trees that were used extensively at earlier levels of the CFA program to value bonds with embedded options and other derivatives based on interest rates. The trees are not actually used here to price callable bonds. Rather, we simply have a reminder that these trees are used. In the unlikely event that you need to calibrate these interest rate trees, recall that—assuming rates follow a lognormal distribution, given a low path rate, $y_d$—the rate on the upper path can be calculated as $y_u = y_d \times e^{2\sigma}$. For example, in the tree just listed, the interest rate volatility assumed was 10%. Hence, given $y_{1,d} = 5.402\%$, $y_{1,u}$ could be derived as $5.402\% \times e^{2(0.10)} = 6.598\%$.

Credit risk is likely to be significant for HY bonds. An analyst would first calibrate their binomial tree to price credit risk—free securities such as Treasuries correctly, then use the tree to assess the spread required to price the credit-risky bond correctly. Recall from previous levels of the CFA program that the z-spread is the constant spread over Treasury spot rates that captures the spread investor's demand for credit risk, liquidity risk, and any impact of an embedded option. To compare across callable, putable, and straight bonds, an option-adjusted spread (OAS) should be used, which removes the impact of the option on spreads.

PROFESSOR'S NOTE

We have met these spreads in Fixed Income in each level of the CFA program. Remember to think of the OAS as the "option-removed spread" because it adjusts to remove the impact of the option.

Recall also that to assess the price sensitivity of bonds with embedded options, effective duration should be used, which looks at the sensitivity of the bond's price to a unit move in a benchmark curve. The following formula should be familiar:


\text{effective duration} = \frac{\mathrm{PV}_{-} - \mathrm{PV}_{+}}{2 \times (\Delta \text{Curve})(\mathrm{PV}_{0})}


where:

$\mathrm{PV}_{-} =$ price of bond when benchmark curve is shifted down by $\Delta$Curve

$\mathrm{PV}_{+} =$ price of bond when benchmark curve is shifted up by $\Delta$Curve

$\mathrm{PV}_{0} =$ price of bond before any curve shift

For example, consider the five-year 6% coupon annual-pay bond priced at par (hence, yielding 6%), discussed previously. If yields fall to 5%, PV is calculated as $N = 5$; I/Y = 5; PMT = 6; FV = 100; and CPT PV: 104.33. If yields rise to 7%, PV is calculated as $N = 5$; I/Y = 7; PMT = 6; FV = 100; and CPT PV: 95.90.

The effective duration is, therefore, given by the following:


\text{effective duration} = \frac{104.33 - 95.90}{2 \times (0.01)(100)} = \frac{8.43}{2} = 4.215


If yields shift by 1%, the bond value will move by approximately 4.215% (in the opposite direction to yields). The sensitivity to a unit change in spread (effective spread
duration) can be calculated using a similar formula with “ΔSpread” replacing the term “ΔCurve.”

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

A leveraged loan will have a low effective duration. Because its coupon rises in line with rises in benchmark rates, its price sensitivity will be low. However, it will have a higher effective spread duration due to the fixed QM not adjusting to changes in DM, and any increase in required return due to deteriorating credit conditions being reflected in a lower bond price rather than a higher coupon.

Recall that fixed-coupon bonds generally exhibit a convex relationship with rates. This means that prices rise faster and fall slower than duration implies. A special case of convexity—negative convexity—occurs for callable bonds at low yields, where price rises are suppressed by the expanding value of the short call embedded in the bond. This dynamic is displayed in Figure M4.4. The duration of an option-free bond, as illustrated by the slope of the price/yield relationship, is always equal to or greater than the duration of an equivalent callable bond.

![img-8.jpeg](img-8.jpeg)
Figure M4.4: Price-Yield Function of a Callable vs. Option-Free Bond With a Call Price of 102

There are times when spreads and benchmark rates are likely to move strongly in opposite directions. For example, in times of stress, credit-risky assets are sold by investors and safer investments are bought (referred to as a flight to quality). This forces credit spreads up at a time when safe benchmark rates are falling. As a result, the actual bond price sensitivity to changes in benchmark rates (referred to as empirical duration), which is driven by both benchmark rates and spreads, may be much lower than analytical duration based on mathematical models.

The covenants of HY bonds are typically less restrictive than the covenants of LLs, being incurrence covenants related to limiting debt issuance or preventing the sale of assets under certain conditions, rather than maintenance covenants that apply in all conditions. A common covenant for HY bonds is a change-of-control provision,
which requires the issuer to offer to repurchase the debt at a fixed price if a certain percentage of voting shares is acquired by a new owner.

# Features of Convertible Bonds

Convertible bonds are senior unsecured instruments, usually issued by growth companies, comprising a bond with the right to convert into equity at a predetermined conversion price. The right to convert is held by the investor in the convertible bond, and therefore, it adds value to the investment:


\text{convertible bond value} = \text{straight bond value} + \text{equity call option}


Convertible bonds are attractive to investors because they are a hybrid instrument. When the underlying equity price is high, the convertible bond will behave like equity; when the underlying equity price is low, the convertible bond will behave like a fixed-income instrument.

They are an attractive method for raising finance for issuers because the conversion option decreases the coupon that needs to be paid on the debt, thereby preserving cash. Issuers may also view convertible debt as a cheap way to issue new shares, should the bond eventually be converted.

While convertible debt typically has no financial covenants, there are usually change-of-control provisions or the right to adjust conversion prices downward in the event of equity dilution. For example, suppose a private company issues zero-coupon convertible bonds with the following features:

- Common shares outstanding: 750,000 shares
- Convertible bond debt issue face value: USD 10 million
- Conversion price (amount of par per common share): USD 125
- Share price at most recent series financing: USD 100

In this case, the conversion ratio for a holding of USD 1,000 par can be calculated as USD 1,000 / USD 125 = 8 shares. Investors would choose to convert the bond if the price of common shares at conversion is greater than the conversion price of USD 125 per share. For example, if the share price rises to USD 200, investors would choose to convert and receive a conversion value of $8 \times \text{USD } 200 = \text{USD } 1,600$, rather than hold the debt and receive a final par payment of USD 1,000. If the entire issue is converted, this will lead to (10 million / USD 1,000) $\times 8 = 80,000$ new shares. The fractional ownership of convertible debt holders once they convert their bonds will be $80,000 / (750,000 + 80,000) = 9.6\%$.

# MODULE M4.3: MEZZANINE AND UNITRANCHE DEBT

Video covering this content is available online.

**LOS M4.c: Contrast the use of mezzanine debt and unitranche debt in private market strategies.**

While the instruments we have discussed so far can be used to raise debt financing for private companies and projects, they usually involve a bank at some stage in the arrangement of the debt issue. For example, LLs are usually underwritten by a bank syndicate, while HY bonds and convertible bonds are often placed by investment banks (indeed, most private debt specialists would not view HY and convertible bonds as part of the private debt universe).

As mentioned earlier, lending to private companies/projects *without* the use of a bank as an intermediary is referred to as direct lending. Direct lending, perhaps the purest form of private debt in that it is a privately negotiated and confidential agreement between a private lender and private borrower, makes extensive use of mezzanine and unitranche debt forms.

## Mezzanine Debt

Usually smaller and longer-dated than other forms of private debt financing by growth/mature companies, mezzanine debt terms are highly flexible, giving rise to different forms. However, the debt is always subordinated to other debt and usually offers some equity participation in the form of attached warrants or conversion rights. Another feature commonly used in mezzanine debt is payment-in-kind (PIK) interest, which is added to principal outstanding rather than paid in cash.

The subordination of mezzanine debt may be either contractual or structural.

**Contractual subordination** occurs when an **intercreditor agreement** with other providers of finance is signed, which specifies mezzanine debt as having the lowest priority of debt-based claims on the borrower’s assets and cash flows. **Structural**
subordination occurs when the mezzanine loan is made to a holding company, which injects equity capital into the borrower. Subordination occurs because mezzanine debt investors do not have a direct debt claim on the assets of the borrower. Instead, their debt is with the holding company, which has a residual equity claim on the assets and cash flows of the borrower after other debt claims have been met.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

Contractual subordination is nothing new; this is how we assume subordination usually takes place (i.e., through legal agreement with other lenders to rank below them in the priority of claims of a borrower).

## Unitranche Debt

Unitranche debt combines senior and subordinated loans into a single debt issue with a single blended interest rate paid by the borrower. From the borrower's perspective, borrowing through a unitranche package rather than raising capital in various forms decreases fees, increases simplicity, and speeds the debt issuance process.

Investors in unitranche debt (typically private credit funds) can either agree to be treated pari passu (i.e., equally) or, through an agreement among lenders, agree to a priority of claims on the unitranche cash flows. For example, the unitranche facility could be divided into a senior class (referred to as a first-out tranche) and a subordinated tranche (last-out tranche), which is repaid after the first-out tranche and carries a higher interest rate. As a relatively new type of legal arrangement, the agreement among lenders' structure remains untested in times of financial distress.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

Take care to note that this senior/subordinated tranching structure exists among lenders only and does not directly concern the borrower, who remains focused simply on servicing the single interest rate loan repayable on the unitranche debt issue.

Risk and Return

The ultra-loose monetary policy and near-zero sovereign bond yields that followed the global financial crisis of 2008–2009 forced institutional investors to search for yield in new areas, such as the private debt market. Alongside direct lending to private companies, institutions such as pension funds introduced real estate financing and private structured products into their portfolios to help match fund liabilities. Other strategies in private debt include specialty financing, focusing on niche areas such as credit card lending or investing in leasing arrangements, and liquidity financing, focusing on private debt funds seeking to generate income from short-term obligations that maintain a stable NAV (similar to money market funds).
Key sources of risk in the private debt market include the following:

- Credit risk. A higher probability of default and higher loss given default imply that private debt investors should demand a higher credit spread.
- Interest rate risk. This is for fixed-coupon HY bonds, as measured by effective duration.
- Liquidity risk. Because no active secondary market exists, private debt investments are largely expected to be held to maturity, increasing the illiquidity of the investment and the return demanded by investors.
- Inflation risk. This is the erosion of purchasing power experienced by investors in fixed-coupon bonds. If the short-term MRR moves in line with inflation, floating-rate instruments exhibit some protection against the impact of inflation.
- Direct lending features. With a private debt fund playing the role of lender instead of a bank, investors rely on the GP to source attractive investments; perform adequate due diligence on borrowers; and structure, manage, and monitor private loan arrangements.

More generally, because private debt is a new asset class, investors face issues with a lack of historical data, as well as uncertainty as to how private credit legal agreements perform in times of financial distress. Proper due diligence of private debt funds and their GPs by institutional investors is a critical part of managing risk in their private debt allocations.

## KEY CONCEPTS

### LOS M4.a

The major types of private debt investments are as follows:

- Venture debt: small, short-term senior loans to early-stage companies, often with warrants attached
- Mezzanine debt: long-term subordinated debt issued by companies in the growth and mature phases of the company life cycle, usually with equity conversion rights or warrants attached; often, interest is structured as payment-in-kind (PIK)
- Convertible debt: fixed-income securities issued by companies, typically in the growth stage, that offer the investor the right to convert their debt into equity
- Leveraged loans (LLs): senior secured floating-rate loans made to non-investment-grade companies that typically already have high levels of leverage; the loans are usually callable at par soon after issuance
- High-yield (HY) bonds: subordinated fixed-coupon instruments issued by non-investment-grade companies, typically in the mature phase of the company life cycle; usually callable at a premium over par after a call protection period

The term *direct lending* relates to a nonbank lender investing in a private company or project. Typically, direct lending is made through mezzanine debt or unitranche debt investments.

Sponsored loans are direct loans made by a company with a private equity controlling stake (the sponsor) through which the private debt terms are negotiated. Nonsponsored direct loans, while having higher search and due diligence costs for investors, will likely have better terms and returns than sponsored loans due to the increased bargaining power of a direct lender when dealing directly with the borrower rather than with a private equity sponsor.

Private debt is also used in real estate construction and development (C&amp;D) loans, as well as infrastructure build-operate-transfer (BOT) projects.

### LOS M4.b

Features of LLs include the following:

- LLs pay a coupon of a floating market reference rate (MRR) plus a fixed quoted margin (QM). Investors demand a return of MRR + discount margin (DM):
- When QM &lt; DM, the loan will be valued below par.
- When QM = DM, the loan will be valued at par (this is usually the case at issuance).
- When QM &gt; DM, the loan will be valued above par.
- LLs will have a low effective duration (interest rate risk) because coupons move in line with benchmark rates. However, they will have a high effective spread duration (credit spread risk) because the QM is fixed. Therefore, any increase in DM must be reflected through a lower loan value.
- Restrictive covenants are likely to be stated as part of the indenture—usually, maintenance covenants that must be maintained. However, strong demand for LLs may see covenants loosened as part of a “covenant-lite” issue (i.e., covenants need only be met under certain conditions [referred to as incurrence covenants]).

Features of HY bonds include the following:

- Early repayment usually requires a tender process at a premium to par, which is voluntary to bondholders.
- HY bonds are usually callable by the issuer after a call protection period comprising the initial years of the bond’s life:

$\text{callable bond value} = \text{straight bond value} - \text{call option value}$

- A callable bond can be valued using an interest rate binomial tree. When rates fall and the value of the bond is greater than its call price at a time when the bond is callable, the value of the bond in the tree is replaced with the call price, representing the fact the bond would be called by the issuer at this node. Higher assumptions for interest rate volatility increase the value of the call and lower the value of the callable bond.
- The $z$-spread is the constant spread above benchmark spot rates that values a credit-risky bond correctly. The option adjusted spread (OAS) removes the impact of the option on spreads and leaves a spread that can be compared across bonds of different optionality (e.g., callable, putable).
- The effective duration of a fixed-coupon instrument is calculated as follows:

$\text{effective duration} = \frac{\mathrm{PV} - \mathrm{PV}_{+}}{2 \times (\Delta \text{Curve})(\mathrm{PV}_{0})}$

where:

$\mathrm{PV}_{-} = \text{price of bond when benchmark curve is shifted down by an amount } \Delta \text{Curve}\mathrm{PV}_{+} = \text{price of bond when benchmark curve is shifted up by an amount } \Delta \text{Curve}\mathrm{PV}_{0} = \text{price of bond before any curve shift}$

- Callable bonds will exhibit negative convexity at low yields due to the expansion in value of the short call position embedded in the bond.
- Empirical duration, based on actual changes in prices of bonds relative to benchmark rates, is likely to be lower than analytical duration—based on the theoretical relationship between benchmark rates and prices—because spreads tend to move in the opposite direction to benchmark rates.
- Covenants of HY bonds are less restrictive than those of LLs, being incurrence covenants relating to certain situations rather than maintenance covenants, which apply in all conditions. A common covenant for HY bonds is a change-of-control provision where a large stake by a new owner of voting shares triggers an offer to buy back bonds by the issuer.
Features of convertible bonds include the following:

- Convertible bonds are senior unsecured instruments, usually issued by growth companies, comprising a debt instrument with the right to convert the debt into equity at a predetermined conversion price.
- Conversion price = par value of bond / conversion ratio:

\text{convertible bond value} = \text{straight bond value} + \text{equity call option}


## LOS M4.c

Direct lending (lending by nonbank lenders) makes extensive use of mezzanine and unitranche debt.

Mezzanine debt is always subordinated to other forms of debt. This is either contractual, through an intercreditor agreement with other lenders to the borrower, or structural in that the mezzanine debt is issued by a holding company with an equity stake in the borrower.

Unitranche debt combines senior and subordinated loans into a single debt issue with a single blended interest rate paid by the borrower. Investors in unitranche debt (typically, private credit funds) can either agree to be treated pari passu (i.e., equally), or they can agree to a priority of claims on the unitranche cashflows through an agreement among lenders.

## LOS M4.d

Creditworthiness of a corporate borrower is based on the borrower's ability to generate sufficient operational cash flows to pay the debt. Higher credit quality is generally associated with the following:

1. Higher profitability, evidenced by a higher EBITDA margin (EBITDA / revenue)
2. Lower leverage, evidenced by a lower total debt/EBITDA or higher retained cash flow to net debt (recall retained cash flow = cash flow from operations – dividends)
3. Higher debt coverage, evidenced by higher EBITDA/interest expense

## LOS M4.e

Key sources of risk in the private debt market are credit, interest rate, liquidity, and inflation risks. Risks of direct lending agreements relate to the ability of a private debt fund to source investments and structure and monitor loan agreements with borrowers. Being a relatively new asset class, a proper test of the performance of legal agreements underpinning debt claims in times of financial distress is yet to happen.

Proper due diligence of private debt funds and their GPs is a critical part of managing risk in private debt investing.

LOS M5.a: Discuss the characteristics and risks of special investment situations.

Special situations are a private investment that focuses on distressed or stressed debt and equity investing, event-driven opportunities, and risk arbitrage, using both short-term and long-term time horizons. Special situations focus on both event-driven and financial distress opportunities that could have significant impacts on security valuation, and can cause financial dislocation where market prices deviate from fundamental values due to these stresses.

## Financial Distress

When an issuer is unlikely to make an interest or principal payment on a contractual loan or bond obligation, these loans and bonds are classified as distressed debt.

Financial distress strategies look at opportunities to invest in distressed debt, often following violations of financial covenants.

When a company is a going concern and the value of assets exceeds the value of debt, debtholders receive all scheduled interest and principal payments, and residual value is captured in equity. In a distressed scenario, the value of a company's fixed debt
obligations exceeds the value of its assets. This can lead to insolvency, where debtholders receive less than the value of the scheduled interest and principal payments, and shareholders receive nothing.

Distressed debt investors often look to either (1) buy the distressed bonds at a significant discount, hoping to capitalize on higher future prices; or (2) sell short the issuer's stock, anticipating insolvency. Investors may also assume an activist role by taking a noncontrolling or controlling position in the company. Investors may buy hybrid securities like convertible bonds, combine long and short positions, or even provide secured financing to companies facing distress. In any case, investors need to anticipate the significant complexities of the priority of various debt instruments in bankruptcy, as well as the significant time, legal, and other costs associated with the process.

## Measuring Distress

Default rates tend to increase during times of uncertainty and economic volatility, as we observed during the 1990-1991 and 2001 recessions, the 2008-2009 financial crisis, and the COVID-19 pandemic.

Credit rating agencies like Moody's and S&amp;P measure investor losses through the credit loss rate, which is the realized loss in par value due to a bond default multiplied by the loss severity, expressed in percentage terms. The highest credit loss rate for investment-grade bonds was 0.4% in 1983, while the credit loss rate for non-investment-grade (high-yield) bonds has often exceeded 5% in the last few decades.

Risk factors that influence financial distress include industry structure and concentration, competitiveness, value chain characteristics, long-term growth and demand, product market risk, execution risk, and excessive operating leverage. Risks particular to distressed investments include the following:

1. Credit risk. The credit valuation adjustment (CVA) measures the present value (PV) of expected loss (EL), where EL is expressed as a conditional probability by multiplying the probability of default (POD) by the loss given default (LGD).

The following steps are needed to calculate a CVA:

- Calculate expected exposure (EE) under default
- Calculate recovery rate (RR), or the percentage of losses recovered in default
- Calculate conditional POD
- LGD = EE × (1 - RR)
- EL = POD × LGD
- CVA = sum of [PV of ELs]

The POD increases for companies in distress and often approaches 100%, in which case the EL is essentially equal to LGD. Evaluating credit risk in these scenarios requires an analyst to contrast between factors affecting the company's short-term liquidity and factors affecting its long-term solvency.

2. Investor liquidity risk. In periods of volatility or distress, investors may be forced to sell their financial holdings at depressed prices. Public equities can be delisted if their prices fall below exchange-specified minimums, adding to liquidity risk.
3. Operational risk. A company's employees, suppliers, or customers may leave when the company is experiencing financial distress.
4. Legal risk. Investors should consider the cost of lawsuits, bankruptcy costs, and other legal costs when investing in distressed securities.

Sovereign borrowers in well-developed markets typically have low credit risk and are less susceptible to financial distress. They are characterized by strong economic activity, significant trade with other countries, and open markets, including freely floating currencies. When a country's currency is widely held globally by investors and central banks, it is referred to as a reserve currency. Examples include the USD, CAD, EUR, JPY, and AUD.

Sovereign borrowers in developing markets are typically characterized by higher credit risk and are more exposed to distress. Often, these countries have less economic diversification, are less open to global trade, and have currencies that are fixed or even pegged to other currencies. Foreign direct investment (FDI) is often a key source of capital, and debt is frequently issued in foreign currencies. This requires that developing countries hold significant foreign currency reserves.

A key feature that separates sovereign borrowers from corporates is sovereign immunity, a legal principle by which sovereign states cannot be sued without their consent. This limits the legal recourse against a sovereign by lenders and creditors. As a result, sovereign distress typically results in debt restructuring rather than asset sales and liquidation.

Foreign currency sovereign bonds are especially exposed to macroeconomic conditions that may weaken the country's domestic currency or lead to lower foreign currency reserves, both of which contribute to the sovereign's financial distress. For example, El Salvador adopted bitcoin (XBT) as legal tender. The fluctuation in the price of XBT since the adoption has caused significant fluctuations in El Salvador's credit conditions, as the price of XBT rose and then dropped significantly, resulting in downgrades in El Salvador's ratings. The country was able to escape default through securing an external loan in early 2023, and due to the price recovery of XBT.

The case of El Salvador is illustrative of a broader trend. When sovereigns experience significant financial distress, a restructuring is necessary, which often involves an external loan and cooperation with a multilateral financial institution. Debt investors often experience losses, with lower interest or principal amounts, grace periods, or other features favoring the debtor nation.

## Reputational Risk

Investors who purchase distressed debt securities face reputational risk. Reputational risks include the concern that these investors could facilitate layoffs, worker displacements, or liquidations—or cause the issuers to fail to meet environmental, social, and governance (ESG) objectives. These concerns are especially relevant for sovereign debt investments in heavily indebted poor countries (HIPCs), a term used for countries that are eligible for external funding support from the IMF and the World Bank. External debt financing is often provided at favorable rates that allow the
sovereign to repurchase outstanding debt, with the promise to use the proceeds to fund conservation efforts such as protecting marine life or endangered ecosystems. For example, in May 2023, under a debt-for-nature swap, Ecuador repurchased $1.6 billion in outstanding debt at $0.40 on the dollar, with the proceeds used to fund the conservation efforts and protection of the Galapagos Islands. Examples of other debt-for-nature swaps include Belize in 2022 and Gabon in 2023.

# Corporate Actions and Event-Driven Opportunities

Special situations investing is not solely related to financial distress. Investing opportunities also arise from corporate mergers, divestments, and acquisitions, including merger arbitrage—which seeks to generate return from price discrepancies relating to business combinations. Price discrepancies often relate to informational inefficiencies around the timing and terms of a transaction.

For example, consider an acquirer firm that announces a proposed takeover of a target company through some combination of cash and shares. Following these announcements, the target company's share price typically remains below the acquisition price because of regulatory, legal, and other uncertainties around the transaction. The acquirer's share price often drops, given the market's perception that the acquirer may overvalue synergies from the takeover. Investors using a merger arbitrage strategy often borrow funds to buy the target company's shares in anticipation of the price increasing later (thereby generating an arbitrage spread), and they short sell the acquirer's shares.

Not all announced mergers go forward. Common reasons for a merger to fail include the following:

- Debt financing is not available.
- Debt financing is available, but at unfavorable terms.
- Due diligence discovers fraud or significant irregularities at the target firm.
- Governments or regulators (including antitrust rules) prevent the merger.
- Changes in economic or industry conditions make the merger no longer attractive.
- Another company makes a counteroffer.

Following a merger, the acquirer receives all the assets and takes on all the debt of the target. The target's existing debt may include certain clauses that protect lenders, including a change of control clause, which requires the issuer to repurchase bonds from bondholders at a fixed price if a merger occurs and another entity gains significant control. This allows bondholders to sell their bonds back to the issuer at a higher price, or to negotiate better bond terms. Even in the absence of a change of control clause, an issuer may buy back existing debt at a premium under a bond tender offer.

Investors use other arbitrage opportunities, often involving significant leverage, to take advantage of mispricings caused by financial distress. A capital structure arbitrage strategy uses a combination of investment strategies, including long and short positions in debt, equity, and credit default swaps. A convertible arbitrage strategy uses
convertible debt instruments to take advantage of mispricings related to equity risk, interest rate risk, credit risk, and volatility.

# General Features of Distressed Debt

While there is no exact definition of distressed debt, issuers are commonly considered distressed if their bonds have a yield spread greater than 1,000 bps above their risk-free benchmark rate, or if their loans are priced below 90% of par value. Similarly, listed equities that trade at or near the minimum trading price specified by the exchange to meet listing rules are generally considered distressed.

Market participants often differentiate between scheduled (actual) default and **technical default**. Whereas actual default relates to missed payments of obligations, technical default relates to violating the terms and conditions of loan agreements, including covenant breaches as well as nonpayment of interest or principal on debt obligations. Breaches may be remedied within a grace period (typically 30 days).

Financial distress can result from numerous factors, including high leverage, adverse changes in balance sheet values, weak or declining operating performance, an uncompetitive business model, or management issues. Monitoring the causes of weakening creditworthiness is critical in helping to improve a distressed company's performance:

1. **High leverage.** High leverage, especially when combined with weak operating performance, can lead to financial distress, although high leverage alone may not be a sufficient cause. The duration and severity of stress events will determine the severity of distress. A company may need to adjust its debt structure to free up cash or reduce leverage when faced with declining sales or cash inflows. A lender may be willing to amend the terms of the company's short-term debt obligations if the lender concludes that the company is only facing temporary distress, but otherwise has strong operating performance and margins.

2. **Adverse changes in asset or liability values.** Declining asset values or increasing liabilities lead to a weaker balance sheet and may cause insolvency. Product recalls, lawsuits involving lengthy litigation, or liabilities exceeding insurance values can also lead to financial distress. An example of declining asset values is **asset impairment**, which involves an asset's fair value declining below the value recorded on the company's balance sheet. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic, many airlines and aircraft lessors saw significant declines in the value of their aircraft—especially for older models—leading them to accelerate retiring these older aircraft.

3. **Weak operating performance.** There can be many causes of weak financial performance, including cyclical downturns, industry decline, or some of the other factors discussed here, including high leverage or an uncompetitive business model.
4. Uncompetitive business model or structure. Companies that undergo restructuring may have difficulty servicing their debt obligations, leading to financial distress. For example, following a 2019 merger between Germany's two largest retailers, Galeria Karstadt Kaufhof (GKK) entered insolvency in 2020. This led to debt write-downs, store closures, and employee layoffs, only to enter another round of insolvency proceedings in 2023.
5. Crisis in confidence. Crisis in confidence in a company or its management can be the result of discovering aggressive accounting practices or significant accounting irregularities and fraud.

The consequences of financial distress vary, ranging from increasing credit spreads as the likelihood of default rises, to technical or actual default if loan or bond covenants are breached. There are many mitigants to financial distress, as detailed in Figure M5.1. $^{1}$

A company may experience temporary distress, with insufficient short-term assets to meet its obligations during the current operating period. In this case, lenders are often willing to negotiate with the company to extend loan maturities, waive or change covenants, or extend additional short-term liquidity facilities, including credit lines. Generally, lenders will require the company to address its liquidity issues on a more sustainable basis. This can be done in one of several ways:

- Selling or pledging assets. Selling assets may be necessary to reduce debt. Asset sales could include specific company assets or even entire businesses that are not core to the company's operations. Assets not pledged against secured debt could also be considered.
- Issuing equity or secured debt. Because unsecured debt is generally not available to distressed companies, issuing secured debt is another alternative to increase liquidity and solvency. A company also might raise equity from existing shareholders, which generally takes less time than a secondary offering to new investors. However, this will lead to some dilution of shareholder positions.

- Purchasing or exchanging debt. Distressed issuers can consider repurchasing their debt issues or certain tranches of debt, either through the open market or through tender offers. Alternatively, especially given limited available liquidity for repurchases, a distressed debt exchange may take place. This is where a company exchanges its existing bonds for new bonds with lower par values or longer maturities, to reduce its debt burden. Bondholders will agree to a distressed debt exchange if it means the company can avoid bankruptcy and defaulting on its debt, which could lead to significantly lower recovery for lenders. Because no new shares are created, a distressed debt exchange does not lead to dilution for existing shareholders.

- Exchanging debt for equity. An alternative to a distressed debt exchange is a debt-for-equity exchange, which results in extinguishing debt rather than maintaining debt and amending debt terms. A key issue to consider is the type of equity that debtholders receive (common shares, preferred shares, or convertible debt). A key drawback of a debt-for-equity exchange from a shareholder perspective is that it leads to dilution.

In general, both debtors and lenders need to consider the cost and time of each potential remedy. The amount of the expected shortfall in a distress scenario will determine how quickly stakeholders want to act. If short-term liquidity is the key concern, a company may not have enough cash to repurchase debt. In this case, the company could consider delaying payables or selling specific assets, which generates cash quickly and eases liquidity constraints.

# Bankruptcy

Restructuring may be difficult for companies with complex business, organizational, or capital structures. Complex capital structures include secured debt, structural or other subordination, covenant protections, or various intercreditor agreements that make it more challenging to negotiate with individual creditors. Unfunded pension obligations and labor contracts, especially relating to a unionized workforce, are additional barriers to quick reorganization and may force the company to enter bankruptcy proceedings.

In the United States, courts grant a debtor an automatic stay (protection from lenders) once the debtor files for bankruptcy. If the debtor transferred assets to third parties before the bankruptcy filing to keep creditors from having access to these assets, the courts may deem it to be a fraudulent conveyance, and they may void these transfers. This applies to transfers made up to two years before the bankruptcy filing. For example, FTX, the cryptocurrency exchange founded by Sam Bankman-Fried and Gary Wang, filed for bankruptcy protection in November 2022. In 2023, one of its successor companies, FTX Trading, sued FTX for fraudulent conveyance to try to recover funds
from transfers up to two years before the bankruptcy that were made by company executives for political donations and private purchases (including for luxury condominiums and private investments).

Under a Chapter 11 filing in the United States, companies may look to continue operating as going concerns and remain in control of their business under court supervision. In this case, a company becomes a debtor in possession (DIP) and will seek to obtain DIP financing, a form of temporary debt financing that is senior in claims to other lenders. To successfully argue for a Chapter 11 filing, a company must demonstrate that it will generate greater value as a going concern than as a bankrupt entity. If it fails to convince the courts, the result is liquidation under a Chapter 7 filing, involving a trustee that controls its assets and settles legal claims.

Many investors protect against credit stress through various forms of financial insurance. One of the most common insurance protections is through a credit default swap (CDS), which involves the protection buyer paying periodic premiums (typically in the form of a 1% coupon payment for investment-grade issuers and 5% coupon payments for non-investment-grade issuers) in exchange for payment by the protection seller in case of a specific credit event. A credit event can include an issuer's failure to make a debt payment, debt restructuring, or a bankruptcy filing. Under a credit event, the settlement payment equals the LGD times the contract notional amount, made by the protection seller to the protection buyer.

Bankruptcy laws in the United States are generally considered to be debtor friendly. However, many jurisdictions globally favor creditors over debtors, which often leads to liquidation rather than restructuring. For example, both Germany and Japan are seen as more creditor-friendly countries, given their strong and influential banking sectors.

When considering financial strategies, the level of control and expected timing are important, both outside and within the bankruptcy process. Out-of-court restructurings are successful only if all creditors agree to the process. Favorable and quick settlements can also occur within the bankruptcy process under a prepackaged bankruptcy, which requires two-thirds majority consent overall and consent of half the investors within each claimant class. The prepackaged bankruptcy requires a business plan and an exit strategy, and aims at speeding up the process, reducing the likelihood of liquidation and allowing the debtor to access equity capital post-bankruptcy.

In cases such as sudden stress events, when issuers may not have sufficient time to negotiate with all creditors, they are likely to file for Chapter 11 in a free-fall bankruptcy. This tends to be more expensive and requires more time than a prepackaged bankruptcy.

They seek to make significant management changes and facilitate creating efficiencies (e.g., cost reductions) to ensure a faster recovery.

# Structural Credit Risk Models

Firm value is determined by the value of assets relative to liabilities, which includes debt. Financial distress is triggered by a decline in asset values below the value of debt. Structural credit risk models look at this relationship by using option pricing theory.

A shareholder's position may be viewed as including a claim on the company's net assets (equity) and a long put option that gives shareholders the right to sell assets to debtholders at an exercise price $D$, the par value of the company's debt. Debtholders, on the other hand, may be viewed as having a risk-free claim at maturity of $D$ on the company's debt and a short put option exercisable by shareholders. The put option effectively represents default risk, or the company's credit spread, because the shareholders will only exercise the put if the company is insolvent (i.e., if the value of its assets is less than $D$). A default is synonymous with shareholders exercising the put option.

Using put-call parity, the underlying asset plus a long put option equals a long call option plus the PV of the face value of the risk-free bond (D), which is equal to the option's exercise price. We can set up the following equations for the value of the firm's assets, $V_0$:


\begin{array}{l}
V_0 + p_0 = c_0 + \text{PV}(D) \\
V_0 = c_0 + \text{PV}(D) - p_0 \\
\end{array}


The Merton model is a structural model that looks at the market value of a firm's equity (E) as the net value of the firm's assets (V) less its liabilities (D). At time $T$, this relationship can be denoted as follows:


V_T = E_T + D_T


where:


\begin{array}{l}
E_T = \max[(V_T - D_T), 0] \\
D_T = V_T - \max[(V_T - D_T), 0] \\
\end{array}


These equations illustrate the idea that shareholders own a call option on the firm's residual assets, while debtholders own a covered call position (assets minus a call option) on the asset value.

By using the Merton model (which is based on the Black-Scholes-Merton option model), we can price equity as a call option on the firm's residual value $(V_T - D_T)$, where $(V_T - D_T)$ is a substitute for the original option model's $S - X$ equation. The option price is then the PV of the expected option payoff at expiration:


E_t = V_t N(d_1) - e^{-\tau T} D N(d_2)

where:


d _ {1} = \frac {\ln \left(V _ {t} / D\right) + [ r + (1 / 2) \sigma^ {2} ] (T - t)}{\sigma \sqrt {T - t}}

d _ {2} = d _ {1} - \sigma \sqrt {T - t}


where:

$\mathrm{N}(\mathbf{x}) =$ cumulative standard normal probability of obtaining a value less than $\mathbf{x}\sigma =$ annual volatility of asset returns

$\mathbf{r} =$ continuously compounded risk-free rate

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

This is a formula that you should be familiar with from Level II. While the equation looks daunting, it is essentially the same as $\mathrm{E} = \mathrm{V} - \mathrm{D}$ , but it uses continuous discounting to generate a PV, and it uses normal probabilities. We believe it is unlikely that you would have to calculate $\mathrm{N}(\mathrm{d}_1)$ or $\mathrm{N}(\mathrm{d}_2)$ on the exam. However, you should be able to apply them in the formula, if given.

The difference between the PV of risk-free debt $(\mathrm{e}^{-rT}\mathrm{D})$ and the risky debt $(\mathrm{D_t})$ is essentially the put option on the firm's value. The price (i.e., premium) of this put option is the PV of expected loss, which (as we noted earlier) is the same as the credit valuation adjustment (CVA).

The put option price can also be described as the spread over risk-free debt. This better illustrates the difference between the risky bond and CDS values. The credit spread expressed by the put option can be modeled as the difference between the yield of the risky bond and the risk-free rate, or $R - r$ . The higher the credit spread, the more valuable the put option. And given put-call parity, a greater put option value implies a greater call option value.

## Capital Structure Arbitrage

Capital structure arbitrage often relies on both long and short positions on the same issuer's securities traded in different markets, and benefits from mispricing opportunities. Investors can use these models to compare credit spreads implied by markets for equities, bonds, and CDS.

We demonstrated that credit spreads based on share prices equal $R - r$ , where $R$ is the bond's yield to maturity and $r$ is the risk-free rate. Structural models show an inverse relationship between share price and implied credit spread. That is, as share price rises, the POD declines and credit spreads become smaller.

Credit spreads observed from bond prices tend to be close to, but not the same as, spreads based on equity prices. Investors can take advantage of these differences, leading to the following decision rules:

If $S_{\text{bond,observed}} &gt; S_{\text{equity,implied}}$ : buy bonds (underpriced), sell short equity (overpriced)

If $S_{\text{equity,implied}} &gt; S_{\text{bond,observed}}$ : buy equities (underpriced), sell short bonds (overpriced)
Investors trading based on these rules expect that mispricings will diminish, resulting in gains on both the long and short sides.

Because these relationships are not linear, investors should consider the hedge ratio when using capital arbitrage strategies. The hedge ratio can be determined based on delta used in the option formula. Delta reflects the expected change in bond price for a given change in equity price, $\mathrm{dDt} / \mathrm{dE_t}$:


\frac{\mathrm{dE_t}}{\mathrm{dV_t}} = \mathrm{N}(\mathrm{d_1}); \mathrm{D_t} = \mathrm{V_t} - \mathrm{E_t}

\frac{\mathrm{dD_t}}{\mathrm{dE_t}} = \frac{\mathrm{dV_t}}{\mathrm{dE_t}} - 1 = \frac{1}{\mathrm{N}(\mathrm{d_1})} - 1


In this equation, we assume that the risk-free rate is constant. In practice, the rate changes, which changes the credit spreads—and therefore, credit risk. Investors can hedge this risk by using **asset swaps**. For example, consider an investor who purchased a fixed-coupon bond. The investor can use an asset swap as the fixed-rate payer, receiving the market reference rate (MRR) plus a spread (or MRR minus a spread) from the floating-rate payer. The investor has effectively converted exposure to fixed-coupon rates to a fixed spread over the MRR. (If the bond defaults, the investor is still exposed to mark-to-market changes on the swap.)

Assuming that investors can borrow and lend at the risk-free rate (e.g., MRR), they can synthetically replicate a long position in either the bond or the credit spread by using asset swaps:

- Synthetic long bond position. Invest the bond purchase price at a risk-free rate (e.g., MRR), sell CDS protection receiving a premium plus a spread, and enter a receive-fixed, pay-floating swap. The investor earns the bond's total return.
- Synthetic long spread position. Borrow at the risk-free rate (e.g., MRR) for the period of the debt maturity, buy the bond, and enter a pay-fixed, receive-floating interest rate swap. The investor earns the spread.

The difference between the bond spread and CDS spread is the **CDS basis**, which can be positive or negative. The CDS basis exists because bond prices differ based on par values, different contract terms, and accrued interest. If the basis is large, arbitrage opportunities exist; when the bond spread and CDS spread converge, arbitrage opportunities diminish. If the CDS spread is negative, an investor can take advantage of an arbitrage opportunity by combining a synthetic long bond spread position with buying CDS protection.

Using capital structure arbitrage, investors can also take advantage of relative mispricings between implied equity and CDS spreads. If the CDS spread is above the implied equity spread, an investor should sell CDS protection and sell short the equity. If the implied equity spread is above the CDS spread, an investor should buy equities and buy CDS protection.

Volatility changes also affect spreads. An increase in volatility increases the expected credit spread. Investors can take advantage of arbitrage opportunities if the implied
equity volatility deviates significantly from the actual volatility observed in the equity options market.

## Convertible Bond Arbitrage

A convertible bond includes an embedded call option sold to investors that gives them the right to exchange the bond for the issuer's shares at a predetermined **conversion price**. Issuers of convertible bonds tend to be early-stage or high-risk companies. Investors are compensated for the higher risk through the call feature. Convertible bonds are often sold at a discount to entice investors to provide capital quickly. The issuer of a convertible bond effectively sells equity volatility to the investor, with a lower coupon relative to nonconvertible bonds.

Investors can replicate a convertible bond by doing the following:

- Investing the bond's purchase price at the risk-free rate (the MRR)
- Selling CDS protection
- Entering a pay-floating, receive-fixed swap
- Purchasing an equity call option matching the bond's conversion terms

Convertible bonds have important differences from straight nonconvertible debt. While equity volatility hurts share prices and credit spreads for straight debt, equity volatility benefits convertible bond investors by increasing the call option value.

The conversion feature also creates arbitrage bounds on the convertible bond that do not apply to straight bonds. Investors can use various arbitrage strategies to isolate credit risk, interest rate risk, or equity volatility risks:

- **Credit risk.** This arises from the default risk of the issuer. Investors can hedge against this risk by buying CDS protection.
- **Interest rate risk.** This arises from the impact of rising interest rates on the fixed-rate bond. Investors can use interest rate swaps to convert the fixed rate to floating rate.
- **Equity volatility risk.** This arises from changing equity market values. Investors can hedge against this risk through short equity positions or equity put options.

# Methods of Due Diligence

Investors need to conduct significantly more due diligence when investing in private markets than in public markets, given the long time horizons, including investment lockup periods. However, when considering distressed security investments, investors have limited time to make decisions and evaluate risks. Decisions are often aided by looking at credit ratings, market capitalizations, market prices, and financial metrics including leverage ratios like debt/EBITDA. This type of analysis and evaluating creditworthiness can be done using structural models like the Merton model.

Creditworthiness can also be assessed by looking at reduced-form models. Reduced-form models assess the POD over a specific period by using financial ratios or a z-score. A z-score is essentially a composite calculated using a range of financial factors to differentiate companies that are solvent or bankrupt. These ratios include liquidity (working capital / total assets), profitability (retained earnings / total assets), asset efficiency (EBIT / total assets), market-to-book value of equity, and asset turnover (sales / total assets).

By contrast, structural models estimate distressed security prices through assessing POD and expected LGD over a specific period, often by looking at models like the Expected Default Frequency (EDF) from Moody's Analytics and Bloomberg's Default Risk (DRSK). The DRSK model looks at equity market capitalization, equity volatility, and book value of liabilities to estimate market-based asset value, asset value volatility, and a default threshold.

Short-term investors need to make quick decisions regarding distressed securities, often in an environment of rapidly deteriorating company financial conditions. As a result, decisions are often based on publicly available information only and relative liquidity considerations. By contrast, long-term investors, including those who take an activist stake in a company, can engage in more in-depth due diligence. These investors may take a small investment position initially and build their stake over time once they are able to conduct proper due diligence.
Ultimately, both short-term and long-term investors face significant hurdles in due diligence analysis because companies may not want to, or be able to, share important information with third parties during periods of distress. Confidential or restricted information is shared on a need-to-know basis with third parties such as banking and lending groups. In general, investors have little bargaining power to obtain information. When companies are willing to share information, investors and creditors may need to sign confidentiality agreements that may limit their ability to trade securities.

Due diligence should include a worst-case scenario that assumes a company is dissolved and its assets are liquidated. If the company has already entered bankruptcy, due diligence should focus on reviewing court filings, inventory liquidations, material events, and operating reports that provide information on unsecured creditors. Legal due diligence should include a review of company structure and loan documents to identify structural or financial red flags. The review may discover that a company has given a creditor a **voidable preference** over other creditors that could be reversed in bankruptcy court.

## Valuation Approaches

Valuing special situations includes the same approaches as going concern analysis, but with numerous necessary analytical adjustments. In addition, analysts may use reduced-form models to obtain default probabilities for a liquidation scenario, while using income-based or multiples approaches to assume a going concern recovery under another scenario.

To the extent that the choices available to companies in making future decisions enhance shareholder value, they can be considered **real options** because these choices can alter future value in a distressed scenario. For example, a company could decide to sell assets or even sell entire divisions. Using this analysis, an investor may assess the value of each of the company's business segments (**sum-of-the-parts valuation**) and assume each segment would be sold separately.

## Income-Based Approaches

Under an income-based approach, value is determined based on discounted future cash flows of periodic income and terminal value, using a discount rate. A typical valuation would use free cash flow to the firm (FCFF) discounted at the weighted average cost of capital (WACC):


\text{intrinsic value}_t = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\text{FCFF}_{t+i}}{(1 + \text{WACC})^i} + \frac{\text{terminal value}}{(1 + \text{WACC})^e}


A distressed scenario changes both the numerator and the denominator. For example, financial distress could lead to higher WACC and lower FCFF, and investors would receive no future cash flow following liquidation. Therefore, terminal value assumptions are likely incorrect, especially because they rely on a terminal growth rate assumption. Analysts should adjust balance sheet and income statement figures as a result of financial distress. Analysts should also recognize that debt and capital become more illiquid during distress. At the same time, distressed bonds trade at substantial
discounts, which increases their yields, but these yields are misleading in that they do not reflect expected rates of return.

## Relative Value Approaches

Under a relative value approach, value is determined using price or enterprise value multiples. This approach works well when the company is assumed to be a going concern. However, applying relative value multiples to companies facing financial distress is likely to distort values. Market multiples and peer analysis can be used even for companies facing distress, assuming that the companies are able to eventually return to profitability.

## Asset-Based Approaches

Using an asset-based approach, a company is evaluated based on the value of its assets less the value of its liabilities (including secured, unsecured, and subordinated debt). Companies that cannot restructure and satisfy their obligations to creditors would need to sell assets. In this scenario, liabilities exceed assets, and the company has negative equity. The liquidation value can be thought of as the lower bound for valuation, as liquidation is an act of last resort. Liquidation typically occurs when the assets are outdated or obsolete, when the company is not competitive, or when management is ineffective. Under liquidation, senior secured lenders typically get repaid in full, while subordinated lenders need to accept a haircut on the value of the bonds they hold.

## LOS M5.e: Discuss the risk and return among special situations and compared to other forms of private debt as part of a strategic asset allocation.

Private market debt is generally non-investment grade (high yield), typically unrated, and often contains restrictive covenants including call or equity-type features. These types of debt securities are often subject to market stresses and sensitive to unfavorable macro conditions, which increases their idiosyncratic risk—although their high yield is often desirable to investors. Private funds, including private special situation funds, often provide funding to distressed companies while offering an attractive risk-return tradeoff to investors. This market is projected to grow to nearly half of the entire private debt market by 2027.³

Large institutional investors like wealth funds and pension funds need to carry out adequate due diligence in assessing special situations fund managers' capacity to add value and generate returns from distressed securities. These investors will only use a manager if the manager's expected return is higher than the returns of other private debt investments. Fund managers aim to generate returns from various risk factors, including credit, liquidity, and legal risks:

- Credit risk. A special situations fund manager can generate return from credit risk if the expected losses are lower (or the expected recovery is higher) than those implied by a distressed asset's discounted price. Fund managers can add value by identifying these mispriced assets, or by identifying scenarios where they can improve the
recovery rates of the securities they purchase. For example, in several U.S. cities, lenders to distressed hotel properties were able to foreclose on the hotel assets outside the reorganization process at favorable discounted prices.

- Liquidity risk. When the prices of assets that have become distressed decline, investors with loans tied to these assets may need to sell the loans in a forced sale scenario. Special situations fund managers actively look to purchase these loans at discounted prices.
- Legal risk. Fund managers who have experience with the bankruptcy process and with creditor disputes may look to invest in distressed securities and generate returns through favorable recoveries.

Whereas investors in private debt generate most of their return from interest income and principal repayments, special situations funds generate their return from price appreciation through these risk opportunities. Special situations can be considered a subcategory of private debt that offers investors an opportunity for diversification. In fact, some private debt funds supplement their returns from nondistressed debt by including distressed debt investing and can pivot between these strategies depending on market conditions. For example, contingent dislocation funds (CDFs) invest investor capital if a trigger event like a market disruption occurs. Trigger events can include various negative market developments or a geopolitical crisis. Several private debt funds include CDFs that only draw down investor funds when a trigger event occurs, allowing the funds to access additional investor capital when a stressed market event occurs.

Investor due diligence is similar for private debt and special situations funds in that both rely on managers with excellent credit analysis and structuring skills, as well as the ability to monitor, manage, and value debt obligations. However, special situations fund managers should also possess the legal knowledge to handle complex restructuring and bankruptcy rules and the liquidation process.

# KEY CONCEPTS

# LOS M5.a

Special situations relate to a private investment strategy focused on distressed debt and equity investing, event-driven opportunities, or risk arbitrage.

Debt can become distressed when an issuer is unlikely to make interest or principal payments. If the value of a company's fixed debt obligations exceeds the value of assets, the company could become insolvent or enter bankruptcy.

Distressed debt investors can take a short-term view and seek to generate returns through price gains on long positions in distressed bonds or price declines on short positions in the issuer's stock, or they can take longer-term views through activist positions in companies.

The key risk factors measuring investor losses related to an issuer's financial distress include credit risk, investor liquidity risk, operational risk, and legal risk.

Sovereign borrowers in highly developed markets typically have low credit risk, and their currency may be a widely held reserve currency. Sovereigns in developing markets typically have higher credit risk and often need foreign investments and loans. Debt investors often experience losses during periods of sovereign financial distress.

Investors face their own reputational risk if it is perceived that their actions or investments contribute to the worsening financial condition of an issuer.

Merger arbitrage generates returns from price discrepancies relating to business combinations. A capital structure arbitrage strategy combines long and short positions in debt, equity, and credit default swaps. A convertible arbitrage strategy uses convertible debt instruments to generate price gains.

Many mergers fail due to a lack of debt financing, lack of acceptable terms, due diligence uncovering fraud or irregularities, regulatory or government intervention, or a third-party offer.

Mergers may allow existing lenders to sell back their bonds to the issuer at a fixed price under a change of control clause. An issuer may also buy back existing debt at a premium under a bond tender offer.

# LOS M5.b

Issuers of debt instruments can face an actual default, which relates to missed payment of obligations—or they can face a technical default, which relates to violations of the terms and conditions of loan agreements.

Financial distress can result from high leverage, declining asset values or increasing liabilities, weak operating performance, an uncompetitive business model or structure, or a crisis in confidence in a company or its management.
Companies in temporary distress with insufficient short-term assets to meet obligations can benefit from lenders extending loan maturities, waiving or changing covenants, or extending additional short-term liquidity. In return, lenders require the company to address its distress and improve operations and liquidity in a sustainable way.

Restructuring can be challenging for companies with complex business, organizational, or capital structures. Courts may grant a debtor protection from lenders once a bankruptcy filing is made, but funds or assets that the debtor transferred to third parties up to two years before bankruptcy may be voided under fraudulent conveyance.

Under reorganization, companies continue operating as going concerns and remain in control of their business under court supervision as a debtor in possession (DIP).

Investors may protect themselves against credit distress through insurance products like a credit default swap (CDS). A CDS protection seller pays the protection buyer if a specific credit event occurs.

Companies may be able to negotiate out-of-court restructurings with creditors, including under prepackaged bankruptcies. However, during sudden stress events, issuers may not have time to negotiate with all creditors—in which case, they enter a free-fall bankruptcy.

# LOS M5.c

Structural credit risk models look at the relationship between the value of assets relative to debt and equity, and rely on option pricing theory.

Put-call parity can be used to calculate put and call option values that help calculate shareholder and debholder payoffs. Shareholders have a claim on the company's net assets plus a long put option to sell assets to debholders at an exercise price. Debholders have a risk-free claim on debt plus a short put option sold to shareholders.

The Merton model can be used to price equity relative to the firm's residual value. The model can be used to price the put option as the present value of the expected loss (EL), or simply the CVA. The put option price can also be expressed as the credit spread over risk-free debt. A higher credit spread indicates a more valuable put option.

Capital structure arbitrage can be used to compare credit spreads for equities, bonds, and CDSs.

The relationship between share price and implied credit spread is inverse. If the observed bond spread is greater than the implied equity spread, investors should buy the bond and sell short the equity. If the observed bond spread is less than the implied equity spread, investors should short the bond and buy the equity. Because these relationships are not linear, investors need to use a hedge ratio based on delta used in the option formula.

Investors can hedge the risk of changing interest rates by using asset swaps. Asset swaps allow the investor to convert exposure to a fixed coupon to a fixed spread over the risk-free rate.
The CDS basis is the difference between the bond spread and CDS spread. Arbitrage opportunities increase when the bond spread and CDS spread are large, or if they diverge. When the spreads converge, arbitrage opportunities diminish. Using capital structure arbitrage, investors can take advantage of relative mispricings between implied equity and CDS spreads.

The issuer of a convertible bond sells equity volatility to the investor. Investors can synthetically replicate a convertible bond by investing the bond's purchase price at the risk-free rate; selling CDS protection; entering a pay-floating, receive-fixed swap; and purchasing an equity call option matching the bond's conversion terms.

Investors can use various arbitrage strategies to isolate credit risk, interest rate risk, or equity volatility risk.

## LOS M5.d

Long-term investors typically engage in detailed due diligence to evaluate an issuer's creditworthiness. Short-term investors often have limited time to evaluate risks—and as a result, they look at credit ratings, publicly available information, and relative liquidity considerations, or structural models.

Structural models estimate distressed security prices through assessing probability of default (POD) and expected loss given default (LGD) over a specific period. Reduced-form models assess the POD over a specific period. The models use financial ratios or a z-score that incorporates various financial ratios to differentiate companies that are solvent from those that are insolvent.

Hurdles to due diligence include a lack of access to confidential or restricted information, and investors have little bargaining power to obtain this information. Information may be shared with investors who sign a confidentiality agreement, but this may limit their ability to trade securities.

Due diligence should include a worst-case liquidation scenario, as well as reviewing court filings, inventory liquidations, and company reports that provide information on unsecured creditors. If a company has given a creditor preference, this may be voided in bankruptcy court.

Investors should consider the availability of real options when valuing companies in a distressed scenario.

Using an income-based approach, future cash flows and a terminal value are discounted. Investors often use FCFF as cash flow, and WACC as the discount rate. However, financial distress could lead to higher WACC, lower FCFF, and zero terminal value.

Under a relative value approach, value is determined using price or enterprise value multiples, which is a useful model when the company is assumed to be a going concern. Using relative value multiples during financial distress distorts values.

Under an asset-based approach, company value is determined based on the value of assets less the value of liabilities. The liquidation value is essentially the lower bound for valuation.
# LOS M5.e

Private market debt is typically high yield, unrated, and contains restrictive covenants. These types of debt securities are subject to market stresses, which increases idiosyncratic risk. Private funds such as special situation funds prefer private debt because of its high return potential, and may provide funding to distressed companies.

When large institutional investors consider using special situations fund managers, they should carry out adequate due diligence to assess the manager's ability to add value and generate returns.

Special situations can be considered a subcategory of private debt that offers investors an opportunity for diversification. Special situations funds generate their return from price appreciation through risk opportunities, using various risk factors:

- Credit risk. Managers can generate return from credit risk if the ELs are lower than implied by a distressed asset's discounted price.
- Liquidity risk. Investors with loans tied to assets may need to sell the loans if the issuer experiences distress. Special situations fund managers purchase these loans at discounted prices and generate returns if these loan prices rise.
- Legal risk. Fund managers can use their experience with the bankruptcy process or legal disputes to invest in distressed securities and generate returns through favorable recoveries.

Investment funds often add special situations fund managers to their investments to take advantage of issuers' financial distress, including from market dislocation events. This can be done through a contingent dislocation fund (CDF), which draws down investor capital only if a trigger event occurs.

Private real estate investments include direct debt and equity investments in real estate. Investment types, in increasing order of risk and return, include the following:

- Senior debt
- Core (stable income-producing properties, diversified public real estate investment trusts [REITs], and sale and leaseback arrangements)

# Property Development Life Cycle

Property development has a life cycle with five phases: planning, land acquisition, construction, completion and lease-up, and operation. Expected cash flows are negative in the early phases and become positive in the last phase.

## 1. Planning

Planning involves determining the physical and economic feasibility of the project. Physical feasibility involves determining the best economic use of a parcel of land, within the rules for setback (distance to the property line) and circulation (set-asides for security and emergency access to the property).

## EXAMPLE: Physical feasibility

A real estate investor is considering a 3-acre parcel (1 acre = 43,560 square feet) for development of two apartment towers, each with a footprint of 36,000 square feet. Zoning laws call for 10% setback and 5% circulation. A parking lot measuring 40,000 square feet is needed to provide 75 parking spaces. Determine whether the parcel can accommodate these requirements.

## Answer:

| Total parcel size (3 × 43,560 sq. ft.) | 130,680 |
| --- | --- |
| (-) Setback and circulation (15%) | (19,602) |
| (-) Parking lot | (40,000) |
| Available to build | 71,078 |
| Needed space (36,000 × 2): 72,000 | |

Because the parcel has a shortfall of 922 square feet, the project with its current specifications is not feasible.

Economic feasibility is determined by comparing the expected future cash flows from the project to its construction and operating costs. Net operating income (NOI) is a key metric for evaluating real estate investments and is similar to EBIT:

NOI = effective gross income – operating expenses – property maintenance allowance

project return = estimated NOI / project cost

In real estate, the required rate of return on a project is the capitalization rate or cap rate:

project value = estimated NOI / cap rate
## 2. Land Acquisition

Assuming a property passes the planning phase, a developer would acquire land using a mix of debt and equity. Acquisition and development loans (ADLs) are used for debt financing. Proper due diligence is needed to ensure that the title is clear, no environmental risks are present, and any building permits or zoning exemptions are cleared before the acquisition.

## 3. Construction

Risk levels increase during the construction phase because of variability in the costs of materials and labor. Unanticipated stoppages due to weather, material shortages, or regulatory issues can increase the project cost. Sometimes, a project may need to be sold off at a significant discount before completion.

Debt financing during construction comes from construction and development (C&amp;D) loans. Equity is also drawn down at appropriate project milestones.

## 4. Completion and Lease-Up

Risks continue to increase as a project nears completion. Due to the long lead time from planning to completion, the market for the completed project may have changed considerably. Even projects that are built for specific tenants, with agreed-upon lease terms, may find the tenant facing insolvency.

Some commercial projects lock in major tenants with favorable initial lease terms. Upon project completion, the ADL and the C&amp;D loan are combined into a commercial mortgage. Commercial mortgage interest rates tend to be lower because risk decreases during the operation phase.

## 5. Operation

Risk starts declining during this phase as cash flows turn positive. Debt service and distributions to equity investors begin in this phase. Debt investors evaluate the credit risk of the project using leverage and coverage metrics:

- loan-to-value ratio (LTV) = debt outstanding / current property value
- Note: During the land acquisition and construction phase, current property value = debt outstanding + equity drawdown to date.
- debt service coverage ratio (DSC) = NOI / debt service
- Debt service includes interest and scheduled principal repayment.
- Equity investors use the equity dividend rate:
- equity dividend rate = (NOI - debt service) / (property value - loan outstanding)

# Private Real Estate Due Diligence

Due diligence of private real estate investments involves examining six factors:

1. Market and outlook. The local business outlook provides key insights into rental demand and rates. Evaluating income-producing real estate involves examining current trends in occupancy and rental rates and sale prices. Opportunistic investors also evaluate current prices of undeveloped land or refurbishment properties, as well as construction costs.

2. Property and project plan. This involves examining surveys as well as physical, environmental, and feasibility studies. Additionally, evaluating construction plans, the quality of the architecture and engineering (A&amp;E) firm, and the construction and coordination plans for the development is important for project success.

3. Developers, suppliers, and contractors. This involves a review of historical performance of contractors, their creditworthiness, incentive alignment, and liability insurance. In cases where the A&amp;E firm or general contractor delivers the project on a turnkey or fully completed basis, concentration of execution risk is a concern.

4. Lease commitments and cash flow projections. Projects with a single tenant or those with an anchor tenant often have lease commitments on completion. Investors should review those commitments as well as the assumptions behind income and expense forecasts.

5. Financing plan. Debt investors participating in ADLs and C&amp;D loans evaluate the expected leverage and commitment and the timing of permanent debt financing. Drawdowns used to finance fees or project management costs are riskier than those that fund project construction (the latter are more likely to be recovered from selling unfinished projects). Projects without a firm financing arrangement on completion and those that use higher leverage are riskier. Early-stage debt investors focus in particular on the financial condition of the developer, loan covenants, and project milestones.

Commercial mortgage investors finance the project on completion and evaluate the lease commitments, debt coverage ratios, and profitability of the completed project. Interest rates set before project completion are riskier than those set based on market conditions at the time of project completion.
Equity investors must balance leverage-induced higher returns with corresponding higher risk. They must be prepared to contribute additional bridge capital if debt agreements do not close, as the alternative is to walk away and lose their investment.

6. Partnership agreement and legal documentation. A partnership agreement specifies the rights of the LPs, including the ability to change GPs, lockup provisions, and other sale restrictions. A legal and tax review must ensure that the land is free from any liens or tax obligations.

## Private Real Estate Valuation Approaches

Valuation approaches for private real estate include income, cost, and sales comparison. Income-producing real estate can be valued using direct capitalization. However, direct capitalization is only suitable when the NOI is stable or has a constant growth rate. A discounted cash flow approach, where the property is valued as the sum of present values of all cash flows and the terminal value, can accommodate other patterns in cash flows. Terminal values are often derived using direct capitalization.

Non-income-producing properties such as raw land can be valued using a sales comparison approach. For a redevelopment project or for newer properties, a cost approach is often used to validate valuations from other approaches.

## Real Options

So far, we have discussed a simple, single course of action for developers. In practice, developers have real options during the life of development project. Options have a nonnegative value and are exercised only if they enhance a property's value.

1. A delay option allows developers to wait for market conditions to improve before starting a project.
2. A sequencing option involves dividing a project into parts and staggering the execution of the parts, to reduce overexposure during periods of high supply.
3. A switching option allows the developer to change a project's use (e.g., changing an office building to a residential building if office rents have declined).

To evaluate real options, we can do a couple things:

- We can determine the NPV of the project without the option. The project NPV without the option is the minimum return. If it is positive (i.e., project IRR &gt; hurdle rate), including the option can only make the project more attractive. We can then value the option separately and add it, net of cost, to the project's NPV:


\text{project NPV} = (\text{NPV without option}) + (\text{option value} - \text{option cost})


- We can use decision trees or option pricing models to value the option.

## Timberland

The value of timberland is derived from its current and future economic use. Timberland is initially prepared and planted. In the second year, weeding and fertilizing require additional cash outlays. Subsequently, smaller outlays for management costs are required. A typical life cycle from planting to harvesting is 25 years, with intervening thinning (in approximately 15–18 years).

Private contractors harvest timberland and pay a **stumpage fee** to the landowner. The stumpage fee depends on the quality and type of timberland harvested. Lower-quality, small, and thinned trees are used as pulpwood for paper products and fetch lower prices. Top-quality **sawtimber** is used in construction, and to manufacture plywood and particle board.

Unharvested timber appreciates in value due to tree growth and because older trees are used as sawtimber. The long cycle of cash flow recovery makes timberland investment suitable only for investors with a long time horizon. Like all real estate types, timberland provides a good hedge against inflation and diversification benefits for a stock/bond portfolio.

Instead of NOI, we use the metric net future value (NFV) of the cash flows for timberland over a harvest cycle:


\mathrm{NFV} = \mathrm{PV}(\text{cash inflows}) - \mathrm{PV}(\text{cash outflows})

\mathrm{NFV} = \mathrm{NPV}(1 + r)^n


where:

$n =$ length of timber rotation in years

NFV is then used to calculate the **land expectation value (LEV)**, the value of raw land used for perpetual timber production:


\mathrm{LEV} = \frac{\mathrm{NFV}}{(1 + r)^n - 1}

| Year 25 inflow from final harvest | $3,000 |

Using a required rate of return of 5%, calculate the LEV per acre and the total value of the land.

= \left[650 / (1.05)^{16} + 3,000 / (1.05)^{25}\right] - \left[400 + 50 / (1.05) + \right. \\
\left. \left. \mathrm{PV}(\$3 \text{ annuity for } 25 \text{ years})\right] \right. \\
= \left[297.77 + 885.91\right] - \left[400 + 47.62 + 42.28\right] \\
= 1183.68 - 489.90 \\
= 693.78 \\
\end{array}

\mathrm{NFV} = 693.78(1.05)^{25} = 2,349.39

\mathrm{LEV} = \frac{2349.39}{(1.05)^{25} - 1} = 984.51


Total value of land = 984.51 × 100 acres = $98,451.

In addition to cash flows from timber, timberland may be able to generate additional cash flows from sales of carbon capture credits. Real options are also available to timberland owners in the form of timing the harvest based on market price and planting the type of timber.

Timberland is attractive because while the demand for paper pulp is increasing, the availability of land for planting timber is limited due to environmental concerns. Timberland also has favorable cyclical characteristics because growing trees appreciate in value, regardless of economic conditions. While economic conditions may cause the prices of final products to fluctuate, stumpage fees have been remarkably stable and far less volatile. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic, lumber prices increased dramatically, even as stumpage fees remained relatively unchanged.

Timberland investment is subject to risks from fire, drought, pests, and plant disease.

## Farmland

Land suitability for farming depends on soil condition, weather, rainfall, and irrigation.

Row crops are seasonal crops harvested annually, while permanent crops such as vineyards and orchards are planted for multiple seasons. Permanent crops yield a higher return per acre. Crops are used as raw materials in food processing industries or as an intermediate input as feed crop used for raising cattle.
Investment in farmland takes two forms: **own–operate**, which involves using the farmland to cultivate and grow crops, and **own–lease** where the farmland is leased out for rent. Own–lease is common for financial investors and generates cash flow in the form of rent less any property taxes, with no other significant income or expense. Capital appreciation is the primary source of return. Farmland investment also provides diversification benefits, a hedge against inflation, and relative lack of correlation with the economic cycle.

While farmland is not eligible for carbon offset credits, government incentives in the form of minimum support prices or mandated use of biofuels provide farm subsidies. Real options to rezone and repurpose (develop) farmland may provide additional value for investors, especially for farmland close to urban areas and transportation centers.

# Risk and Return

# MODULE M7.1: INFRASTRUCTURE INVESTMENT FEATURES

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M7.a: Discuss important infrastructure investment features.

This reading focuses on investments in private unlisted infrastructure projects. These projects typically involve the financing, construction, and operation of assets, which provide services essential to the functioning of the economy and society as a whole (e.g., energy and transportation sectors).

# Infrastructure Risk/Return Spectrum

Recall that the risk/return spectrum for real estate investment comprises core properties at the low-risk end, opportunistic investments at the high-risk end, and value-add investments in between. A similar spectrum exists for infrastructure investments as follows:

- Core (safest) infrastructure investments require no further development, are fully operating, and generate stable cash flows in a developed market.
- Core-plus investments require some development, but construction risk is low. This infrastructure is likely already operating and requires updating or repositioning (referred to as brownfield) rather than being constructed as new (referred to as greenfield).
- Value-add investments are greenfield or brownfield assets with higher construction risk, where the market for the service provided by the infrastructure is not as well established.
- Opportunistic (riskiest) investments are greenfield projects, typically in developing markets.

# Unique Features of Infrastructure Investments

While infrastructure investments are similar to real estate development projects in that they are large, illiquid, and can use significant leverage, key differences are as follows:

- Due to the essential nature of the service provided by the infrastructure, a government agency (i.e., the "public" sector) is usually involved in the organization of projects. The exact nature of public sector involvement varies dependent on the project; however, roles for the government include one or more of the following:
- Striking agreements with private sector developers to build and operate the infrastructure
- These concession agreements between the government (the grantor) and a private developer/operator (the concessionaire) specify terms and conditions of the project, including the right of the private developer to charge fees for use of the infrastructure to recover costs and generate returns for investors
- Providing and/or guaranteeing debt financing for the project
- Providing payments to stabilize revenue related to the infrastructure or compensate the private sector for making the infrastructure available for use
- The assets created by infrastructure projects are often designed for a specific purpose and are difficult to repurpose for other income-generating activities. Therefore, the value of the asset is driven primarily by the contractual arrangements of the project. Oftentimes, the assets are constructed on public land and transferred to the government at no extra cost at the end of a specified operating period, effectively implying a terminal value of zero.
- Single infrastructure projects are usually governed via a special purpose entity (SPE), also known as a special purpose vehicle (SPV). This legal vehicle is set up to raise debt and equity financing from investors and establish their claims on the net cash flows of the project, while providing them with limited liability.

# PROFESSOR'S NOTE

Recall that limited liability is the legal concept of limiting the amount of capital that investors are legally obliged to contribute to an investment. With limited liability, investors know that if an investee company or project goes bankrupt, they are not legally liable to meet the debts of the project as long as they have contributed the capital they promised.

The revenue for infrastructure operators can take one of following three forms:

1. Commercial payments (merchant payment schemes). These are fees from use of the asset, common for assets such as toll roads or power generation where users pay at the point of use.
2. Fixed or availability-based payments. These are fixed payments made by users (usually the government) if the asset is available for use. They are common for social infrastructure such as schools and hospitals where fees are not generated directly at the point of use.

3. Regulation-based payments. This is where public authorities either set prices while regulating a monopoly or engage in transactions designed to stabilize revenues for infrastructure operators.

An example of a regulation-based payment is the use of contracts for difference (CfDs) for renewable energy projects in the United Kingdom. These cash-settled forward contracts can be used to stabilize the price received for electricity generated by renewable energy infrastructure.

## EXAMPLE: Contract for difference (CfD)

A wind farm operator enters into a CfD agreement with government-owned Low Carbon Contracts Company (LCCC). The contract has a size of 20 megawatts (MW) of power and a fixed strike price of GBP200 per MWh (megawatt hour).

If actual power prices fall below the fixed strike of GBP 200 per MWh, LCCC makes a payment to the developers relating to the difference. Conversely, if power prices rise above GBP 200 per MWh, the developers make a payment to LCCC.

Assume that power prices are GBP 190 per MWh for the most recent quarter lasting 90 days (referred to as the intermittent market reference price [IMRP]).

Demonstrate how the wind farm operator is guaranteed a net price of GBP 200 per MWh assuming 24-hour daily operation and constant 20 MW per hour output.

## Answer:

Income generated by the wind farm from selling 20MW of power at a price of GBP 190 per MWh for 24 hours a day for 90 days = GBP 190 × 20 MW × 24 hours × 90 days = GBP 8,208,000.

P/L from CfD = (GBP 200 - GBP 190) × 20 MW × 24 hours × 90 days = GBP 432,000.

Total income to wind farm = GBP 8,208,000 + GBP 432,000 = GBP 8,640,000.

The effective power price in MWh for the wind farm = GBP 8,640,000 / (20 MW × 90 days × 24 hours) = GBP 200.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

This is a simple derivative hedge by the wind farm operator. Note also that the wind farm operator does not participate in any upside in power prices—any increase in income from prices rising above the CfD strike of GBP 200 would be paid away to LCCC as losses on the CfD. Note also that if the wind farm produces more or less power than specified in the CfD, then their income is not fully hedged under the CfD. Hence, the revenue risk relating to variable levels of power output remains.
Relative to other asset classes, infrastructure investments tend to have the following features:

- Higher cash yields. Due to the single-use nature of a stand-alone infrastructure project, there is low opportunity for reinvestment of cashflows; hence, cash flows to debt and equity investors in infrastructure projects tend to be relatively high.
- Diversification. The essential nature of the service provided by the infrastructure means that demand is insensitive to market cycles and changes in prices (referred to as inelastic), which should lower the correlation of returns with other asset classes.
- Potential for liability matching. Relatively predictable cash flows over long periods offer the opportunity for investors, such as defined benefit pension plans, to match long-dated liabilities.

# The Infrastructure Company Classification Standard (TICCS)

TICCS is a framework used to analyze the risk and returns of an infrastructure project and has four features to consider: (1) business risk, (2) industry, (3) corporate governance, and (4) geoeconomic. Specific issues addressed for each feature are listed as follows:

- Business risk. This relates to how revenue from the infrastructure is earned. Recall that there are three main revenue generation methods:

- Commercial payments. Changes in macroeconomic factors driving demand for the use of the infrastructure will cause volatility in revenue from the asset.

- Availability-based payments. While fixed in nature, risk lies with the developer/operator failing to meet all conditions required to classify the asset as available for use. Investors will also be subject to the legal risk relating to the contracted payments.
- Regulated payments. Government action on setting prices lowers the volatility of revenues from the asset but increases regulatory risk relating to a change in government, leading to changes in regulation.

- Industry. Is there cyclicality in demand for the product of the infrastructure? Is the industry competitive? What are the growth prospects?
- Corporate governance. While single projects are typically executed through SPEs with a finite life, multiproject governance is usually performed through a corporation with a portfolio of infrastructure assets, which evolves over time. Investors likely have greater control over the cash flows of single-project investments through an SPE. Therefore, they may be willing to lend more to a single-project SPE than to a multiproject corporation.
- Geoeconomic. Does the asset rely purely on local economic activity, or is there exposure to national or international factors? Is the national government policy involved in structuring the public-private partnerships (PPPs) that drive projects likely to change? Does the project rely on global trade (e.g., ports)?
# Developed vs. Developing Markets

Relative to developed markets, infrastructure projects in developing markets are more likely to be greenfield than brownfield, and they rely more on global development banks such as the World Bank or the International Finance Corporation (IFC) for advice in structure investments and raising capital. Currency risk is a concern in developing markets, and the performance of the infrastructure asset may be dependent on a market for a key commodity or specific industry due to lower levels of diversification in the economy.

Infrastructure investing requires a high level of expertise in structuring contracts, meeting legal and regulatory requirements, and assessing the economic feasibility of an investment. As discussed earlier, a single infrastructure project is usually governed by establishing an SPE. The SPE enters into concession agreements with public authorities; raises debt and equity capital; and engages with suppliers, construction contractors, and insurance companies to build and operate the infrastructure asset. The SPE may be set up as a holding company that raises capital and negotiates the concession agreement with the public authority. The SPE also holds a controlling stake in a separate operating company that deals with the suppliers and construction companies involved in building and operating the infrastructure.

The debt capital for the project is likely to come from bank loans and other private debt investors. - Investors do not face risks associated with any obligations or liabilities of the public authority outside of the infrastructure project, and they can focus their due diligence exclusively on the project and its expected cash flows.
- Contractors/suppliers can interact with specialist staff at the SPE that are skilled in negotiating and executing contracts, rather than having to liaise with the private equity general partner (GP) and/or government authority involved in the project.

The bidder needs to show that they have access to capital, as well as technical and regulatory expertise to execute the requirement of the project subject to the procurement procedures of the public authority. Once RFP submissions have been evaluated and negotiated, the concession agreement is awarded by the grantor to the successful bidder and plans are approved, at which time the raising of capital and construction can begin.

# MODULE M7.3: INFRASTRUCTURE INVESTMENT PROCESS

Video covering this content is available online.

LOS M7.c: Discuss the infrastructure investment process over the project life cycle and the roles of infrastructure debt and equity financing.

Single stand-alone infrastructure projects are often structured as a build-operate-transfer (BOT) project. In the build stage, which could last several years, capital is drawn down, the infrastructure asset is constructed, and cash flows and returns are negative. This is followed by a predetermined operate period (potentially lasting decades), during which the infrastructure asset generates cash flows used to meet maintenance and operating expenses, service debt, and provide a dividend return to equity investors. Typically, debt is fully repaid during the operate period, leaving cash flows post expenses to accrue as dividends to equity investors. In the transfer phase, the asset is transferred to the public authority at zero terminal value.

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

Recall how the specific nature of the infrastructure asset and the inability to reposition it for other uses mean that the asset often has zero terminal value once the rights of the concessionaire to operate and generate cash flows elapse.

The creditworthiness of the SPE debt can be enhanced by the creation of reserve accounts, which accumulate cash flows to meet later obligations of the SPE. For example, a debt service reserve account (DSRA) would accumulate early cash flows to meet debt payments required later in the project's life. Similar reserve accounts could be established for meeting operating and maintenance costs of the project or meeting the costs of decommissioning equipment at the end of the project.

Credit analysis of the debt of an infrastructure project can be performed using familiar creditworthiness ratios such as loan-to-value (LTV) and the debt service coverage ratio (DSCR), where debt service is the interest and principal payments made on debt:


\mathrm{LTV} = \frac{\text{debt outstanding}}{\text{current project value}}

DSCR = \frac{\text{net cash flow from operations}}{\text{debt service}}


To consider the risks and returns to equity investors, we can evaluate the equity dividend rate:
equity dividend rate
=
net cash flow from operations - debt service equity invested

This equity dividend is calculated before the fees of the GP sponsoring the project. Fees for the GP usually take two forms: (1) an assets under management (AUM) fee applied periodically to the end-of-period value of assets, and (2) a performance fee, calculated as a percentage of growth in equity after AUM fees as long as it exceeds the hurdle rate of the fund. This can be formulated as follows:

max{0, p × [ending equity value - AUM fee - initial equity(1 + hurdle rate)]}

where:

$\mathbf{p} =$ performance fee (in %)

ending equity value $=$ initial equity value $^+$ net cash flow from operations - debt service

## PROFESSOR'S NOTE

The assigned reading assumes that the AUM fee is zero, which greatly simplifies the calculation.

\mathrm{DSCR} = \frac{\mathrm{USD\ 90,000,000}}{\mathrm{USD\ 16,500,000}} = 5.45

\mathrm{LTV} = \frac{\mathrm{USD\ 386,048,896}}{\mathrm{USD\ 550,000,000}} = 0.702

Therefore, in Year 4, this is the rate:


\text{equity dividend rate} = \frac{\text{USD\ 90,000,000} - \text{USD\ 16,500,000}}{\text{USD\ 150,000,000}} = 49\%


The figures on the numerator were calculated in the solution to DSCR, and the denominator represents the full drawdown of equity of 30% × USD 500 million by the end of Year 3.

4. The hurdle rate applied to the equity invested by the end of Year 3 = 0.10 × USD 150 million = USD 15 million.

LOS M7.d: Discuss the due diligence and valuation processes for infrastructure investments.

Infrastructure Investment Due Diligence

There are three key areas of due diligence for an infrastructure project investment:
1. Jurisdictional, legal, and regulatory issues. Equity investors need to fully understand the legal framework within which agreements with public bodies are struck, their rights under concession agreements, and the means of dispute resolution. Lenders must understand covenants and security over project assets. Investors in developing markets should also consider the level of technical and financial support from supranational institutions, such as the World Bank. In addition, ESG issues should be considered.
2. Engineering, procurement, and construction contract issues. Investors must understand the technical, legal, and regulatory requirements of project assets to deliver the contracted service. The risk of cost overrun or delays in construction should be assessed. Procurement and contracting processes of the project, usually driven by the laws of the local market, must be understood and adhered to. Third-party developers, contractors, and suppliers should be subject to due diligence based on their reputation and financial stability.

3. Concession scope, specifications, and term. Investors must understand the likely size and timing of all revenue and costs, including maintenance and upgrade costs that need to be met by the project.

# Infrastructure Investment Valuation

The preferred method for infrastructure project valuation is the discounted cash flow (DCF) approach, which is based on the periodic posttax dividends from the project.

The discount rate used should reflect the risk-free rate plus an asset-specific risk premium based on the risk factors of the project. Typical risk factors include the following:

- Size. Larger projects are typically less liquid, so they demand a higher liquidity premium. Note that this premium is likely to rise in times of market stress.
- Leverage. Higher leverage implies a higher risk premium.
- Profitability. Lower profits imply a higher risk premium.
- Capital investment. Greenfield projects command a higher risk premium than brownfield projects due to the higher level of capital investment and higher uncertainty regarding forecast revenues.
- Country risk. Developing markets imply a higher risk premium than developed markets.
- Currency risk. Unhedged foreign currency risk implies that a risk premium is required.

Note that these risk premiums could change over the life of a project. For example, the leverage risk premium is likely to fall in line with decreases in leverage as debt is repaid over the life of the project. Also, note that the risk-free rate will reflect the term structure of interest rates over the life of the project (i.e., the rate will vary according to the yield curve).

We demonstrate an example of an infrastructure project valuation using a continuation of the Runway2 Limited example introduced earlier in this reading.

# Sensitivity and Scenario Analysis

Once a "base case" for the expected cash flows of the project has been established, an analyst would perform scenario and sensitivity analysis on the project to assess the impact of the uncertainty regarding the projections used in the valuation. Scenario analysis reassesses assumptions under more favorable and more adverse conditions and assigns probabilities to these scenarios. Sensitivity analysis assesses the impact of a change in one or more of the crucial variables used as inputs to the valuation.

For example, in the case of Runway2 Limited, sensitivity analysis could be performed by varying the assumed growth rates in passenger numbers and revenue per passenger to assess impact on the internal rate of return (IRR) earned over the construction period. It would also be prudent to assess the impact of costs increasing as a proportion of revenues when revenues fall, due to some costs being fixed. A less profitable project would also imply that higher discount rates should be used. When revenues rise, a prudent forecast would include increased capital expenditure to sustain a higher growth rate in passenger numbers, which would lower cash flows to equity and increase the investment risk premium.

An analyst may also wish to assess the impact of cost overruns and/or delays in the construction period of the project. In the case of Runway2, there is the following:

- If construction costs overrun by $10\%$ of total costs (USD 50 million), assumed to be paid at completion in Year 3, then this reduces the value of the project at the end of Year 3 to USD 520,768,889, lowering the IRR over the construction period to $(520,769,889 / 150,000,000)^{(1/3)} - 1 = 51.4\%$.
- If the construction is delayed by a year to be completed at the end of Year 4, the IRR in the construction period falls from $56.1\%$ to $(570,769,889 / 150,000,000)^{(1/4)} - 1 = 39.7\%$.
- Note also that if the project completion is delayed, but project debt financing flows are not, then this could affect the ability to meet debt service payments. For example, if Runway2 is delayed by a year, we can see from Figure M7.5 that net CFO in Year 10 will be 120,895,480 (the number previously earned in Year 9). This lowers the DSCR in Year 10 to 120,895,480 / 106,000,000 = 1.14 from the previously calculated value of 1.21.

## KEY CONCEPTS

### LOS M7.a

Infrastructure investing has the following risk/return spectrum:

- Core (safest) investments require no development and generate stable cash flows in a developed market.
- Core-plus investments require some development, but construction risk is low (i.e., brownfield) rather than being constructed as new (i.e., greenfield).
- Value-add investments are greenfield or brownfield assets with higher construction risk, where the market is not as well established.
- Opportunistic (riskiest) investments are greenfield projects in developing markets.

The unique features of infrastructure projects include the following:

- A public authority (e.g., local government) is usually involved in the organization of projects.
- The assets in a stand-alone infrastructure project are usually transferred to a public authority at zero terminal value the end of the project.
- Single infrastructure projects are usually governed by a special purpose entity (SPE), set up to raise capital and establish debt and equity claims on the cash flows of the asset with limited liability.

The revenue generated by infrastructure can take one of three forms:

1. Commercial payments for the output of the infrastructure
2. Fixed or availability-based payments made by private or public entities if the infrastructure is available for use
3. Regulation-based payments where payments are set or stabilized by a public authority
Relative to other investments, infrastructure investments tend to have a high cash yield, low correlation with market cycles, and the potential for matching long-term liabilities through long-duration, stable cash flow streams.

The Infrastructure Company Classification Standard (TICCS) used to analyze risk and return has the following four main considerations:

1. Business risk. Are payments commercial, availability based, or regulation based?
2. Industry type. Is it cyclical? Is the industry competitive?
3. Corporate governance. Is it a single project governed through an SPE, or are there multiple projects governed through a corporate structure?
4. Geoeconomic factors. How much does project success rely on local, national, and international factors?

## LOS M7.b

Smaller, less experienced investors are less likely to have the size or expertise to invest directly in infrastructure and will either invest through a fund or coinvest with more experienced managers.

The right to build and operate the infrastructure asset may be granted to the SPE governing the project through a concession agreement between the public authority (i.e., the grantor) and the SPE (i.e., the concessionaire).

The project is initiated by the public authority through a request for proposal (RFP) from the public sector to the private sector detailing the project requirements, inviting them to bid for the project.

## LOS M7.c

Stand-alone infrastructure projects are usually structured as a build-operate-transfer (BOT) project:

- Build. Capital is drawn down, and the asset is constructed.
- Operate. The private entity recovers capital and earns returns through operating the infrastructure for a predetermined period.
- Transfer. The asset is transferred to the public authority with zero terminal value.

Credit analysis of the debt of an infrastructure project can be performed using familiar creditworthiness ratios such as loan-to-value (LTV) and the debt service coverage ratio (DSCR):


\mathrm{LTV} = \frac{\text{debt outstanding}}{\text{current project value}}

\mathrm{DSCR} = \frac{\text{net CFO}}{\text{debt service}}


When considering the risks and returns to equity investors in the project, the equity dividend rate is considered:


\text{equity dividend rate} = \frac{\text{net CFO} - \text{debt service}}{\text{equity invested}}

The periodic fees of the GP managing the infrastructure investment will be (1) an AUM fee based on assets valued at the end of period, and (2) a performance fee, calculated as follows:


\max \{0, p \times [\text{ending equity value} - \text{AUM fee} - \text{initial equity}(1 + \text{hurdle rate})]\}


where:


p = \text{performance fee (in \%)}

\text{ending equity value} = \text{initial equity value} + \text{net CFO} - \text{debt service}


## LOS M7.d

There are three key areas of due diligence for an infrastructure project investment:

1. Jurisdictional, legal, and regulatory issues
2. Engineering, procurement, and construction contract issues
3. Concession scope, specifications, and term

The preferred method for infrastructure project valuation is the discounted cash flow (DCF) approach based on the periodic posttax dividend from the project.

The discount rate used should reflect the risk-free rate plus an asset-specific risk premium based on the risk factors of the project. Typical risk factors include size, leverage, profitability, capital investment, and country/currency risks.

An analyst should perform both scenario analysis (base case, good, bad) and sensitivity analysis (impact of changing a key variable) when assessing the risk of an infrastructure project.

## LOS M7.e

Core infrastructure projects offer stable cash flows over long-duration contracts. Therefore, they can provide an attractive, higher-yielding alternative to fixed income when managing portfolios to meet long-term liabilities (albeit, at a lower level of liquidity). Cash flows from infrastructure investments will likely have low correlation with general market cycles, so they provide good diversification with other asset classes. The impact of inflation on the returns from an infrastructure project will depend on the nature and structure of the investment.
