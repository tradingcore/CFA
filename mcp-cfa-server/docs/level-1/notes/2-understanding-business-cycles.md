## Business Cycle and its Phases

## Business Cycles

### 1. Classical Cycles
- An economic cycle that shows fluctuations in GDP.
- It has shorter contraction phases and longer expansion phases.
- However, it’s not commonly used due to its inability to distinguish short-term fluctuations from long-term trends.

### 2. Growth Cycle
- Focuses on variations around the long-term trend growth level.
- Emphasizes the interplay between actual economic activity and trend growth.

### 3. Growth Rate Cycle
- Has economic cycle defined by changes in GDP growth rate.
- Peaks and troughs are detected earlier than with classical or growth cycle.
- Does not require long-term growth trajectory calculation.

## Business Cycle Phases

### 1. Recovery Phase
- Narrowing negative output gap.
- Business and consumer activity are below average but increasing.
- Businesses reduce layoffs, rely on overtime, then transition to hiring, stabilizing unemployment.

### 2. Expansion Phase
- Positive output gap widens.
- Business and consumer activity have above average growth rates.
- Businesses shift from using overtime and temporary employees to hiring, leading to a stabilization and eventual decrease in the unemployment rate.

### 3. Slowdown Phase
- Positive output gap narrows.
- Business and consumer activity are above average but declining.
- Businesses continue to hire but at a slower pace, causing the unemployment rate to continue falling.

### 4. Contraction Phase
- Negative output gap widens.
- Business and consumer activity below average.
- Businesses first reduce working hours, eliminate overtime, and freeze hiring before resorting to layoffs.

- Credit cycles describe how credit availability, and its costs change over time.
- In a strong economy, creditors are willing to offer credit and at good terms.
- In a weak economy, creditors make it more expensive and less available.
- Credit cycles are typically longer, deeper, and more pronounced than business cycles.
- Investors observe credit cycles in order to:
- Better understand developments in the housing markets.
- Better assess business cycles.
- Better anticipate policy makers’ actions.

## Fluctuations in Capital Spending

### Recovery Phase

- Companies focus on efficiency improvements rather than capacity expansion.
- Capital spending gradually increases as companies start to enjoy better economic conditions.
- Initial orders often target equipment with a high rate of obsolescence.

### Expansion Phase

- Companies benefit from favorable business conditions and increasing demand.
- Capacity utilization levels rise from previously low levels.
- Increased earnings and cash flows provide businesses with the financial capacity to increase their investment spending.
- Phase is significantly characterized by heavy investments in complex equipment, warehouses, factories, and infrastructure.

## Slowdown Phase

- The economy is still growing, but the rate of growth begins to slow down.
- Interest rates tend to rise during this phase to prevent overheating and encourage investment slowdown.

## Contraction Phase

- Characterized by reduced demand, declining profits, and cash flows.
- New orders come to a halt, and some existing orders are canceled due to decreased expansion needs.
- Reductions in spending on technology and light equipment, followed by cuts in construction and heavy equipment as the economic situation worsens.

## Fluctuations in Housing Sector Activity

### Recovery Phase
- Increase in consumer and business confidence.
- Housing construction and sales begin to pick up.
- Increased demand for housing as consumers feel more secure about their financial situation.
- Real estate prices may start to stabilize or rise moderately.

### Expansion Phase
- Consumer incomes rise, and employment improves.
- Further growth in housing demand as people have more disposable income to invest in homes.
- Home construction and real estate sales thrive.
- Property prices tend to rise.

## Fluctuations in External Trade Sector Activity

### Recovery Phase

- Gradual increase in trade volumes as demand begins to recover.
- Increased domestic consumption and higher demand for goods and services.

### Expansion Phase

- High levels of trade activity, with both imports and exports at elevated levels.
- Exports from a country rise, driven by stronger demand abroad.
- This phase can contribute positively to a country's trade balance.

## Slowdown Phase

- Economic growth starts to moderate.
- Reduction in consumer spending may impact the demand for imports.
- Exports may also experience a slowdown, especially if trading partners are experiencing economic challenges.
- The trade balance might become more balanced or even show a surplus.

## Contraction Phase

- Both domestic and global demand for goods and services can decrease significantly.
- Substantial reduction in both imports and exports.
- Many countries may experience a trade deficit during this phase as both imports and exports decline.
- Trade-related industries, such as shipping and logistics, can also be negatively affected.

## Fluctuations in Inventory Levels

### Recovery Phase

Economic activity starts to pick up, and demand for goods and services begins to increase.

Businesses may start rebuilding their inventories to meet the rising demand.

They may increase production to replenish their stocks and prevent shortages.

### Expansion Phase

Economic growth gains momentum, leading to stronger consumer spending and business investment.

The demand for goods and services continues to rise.

Businesses often increase their inventory levels to ensure they can meet the growing customer demand.

Economic indicators give information about the condition of the economy and are used for determining the current state and trends of the economic cycle.

1. **Leading Indicators**
- Provide insights into potential future economic trends and turning points.
- Include share prices, average weekly hours in manufacturing, and initial claims for unemployment insurance.

2. **Lagging Indicators**
- Only change when the economy has started following a certain pattern.
- Includes average duration of unemployment, inventory-sales ratio, and CPI.

3. **Coincident Indicators**
- Exist during specific economic activities and move in tandem with changes in the overall economy.
- Includes index of industrial production, retail sales, and real personal income.

## Big Data and Nowcasting: Real-Time Insights

The advent of big data and nowcasting has revolutionized economic analysis. It enables real-time insights by harnessing data from various sources. It also provides timely estimates for low-frequency economic indicators.

## Examples of Nowcasting Applications

**GDPNow**

An estimation tool that utilizes real-time data to forecast current-quarter GDP growth.

**EuroCOIN**

A measure that employs diverse data sources to assess the state of the Eurozone economy.

**Purchasing Manager's Indexes (PMIs)**

Surveys that capture current business conditions and act as leading indicators.
