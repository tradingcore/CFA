LOS: Interpret interest rates as required rates of return, discount rates, or opportunity costs and explain an interest rate as the sum of a real risk-free rate and premiums that compensate investors for bearing distinct types of risk.

- Would you prefer $1,000 today or $1,000 in five years?
- Obviously, you would rather have the $1,000 today.
- This concept is called the time value of money.
- Expressed more generally, 1 dollar **received today is worth more** than a similar amount be received some time in the future, due to earning potential.

- Time allows you the opportunity to postpone consumption and earn interest.
LOS: Interpret interest rates as required rates of return, discount rates, or opportunity costs and explain an interest rate as the sum of a real risk-free rate and premiums that compensate investors for bearing distinct types of risk.

Interest rates can be thought of in three ways:

i. Required rate of return: The minimum rate of return an investor must receive in order to accept an investment.

ii. Discount rate: The rate used to discount future cash flows to allow for the time value of money (to determine the present value equivalent of some money to be received some time in future).

iii. Opportunity cost: The value of the best forgone alternative; the most valuable alternative investors give up when they choose what to do with money.

- The real risk-free interest rate is the **single-period interest rate** for a completely **risk-free security** if no inflation was expected.
- The inflation premium compensates investors for **expected inflation**.

## Major Return Measures

### 1. Holding Period Return

- The holding period return, R, refers to the change in the value of an investment over the period it is held, expressed as a percentage of the originally invested amount.
- The period may be any specified period such as a day, a month, or 10 years

- It also captures any additional income that one earns from an Investment:


R = \frac{\text{Ending value} - \text{Beginning value} + \text{Asset income}}{\text{Beginning value}} = \frac{(P_1 - P_0) + I_1}{P_0}

## Major Return Measures

### 1. Holding Period Return

- What if the period is longer than one year?
- Consider a holding period return for a five-year period.
- We need to compound the five annual returns as follows:

R = \left[ \left(1 + R _ {1}\right) \times \left(1 + R _ {2}\right) \times \cdots \times \left(1 + R _ {5}\right) \right] - 1

- The current share price has grown by 50% of the purchase price.
- So far, he has received 10 dividend payments, each amounting to $0.50 per share.

## 2. Arithmetic return

- It’s a simple **average** of the **holding period returns**.
- It is calculated by adding all the returns in each period and then dividing this figure by the total number of returns.
- It is denoted by $\bar{R}_i$ for asset $i$, and is given by:


\bar{R}_i = \frac{R_{i,1} + R_{i,1} + \cdots + R_{i,T-1} + R_{iT}}{T} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} R_{it}


## Where:

- $R_{it}$ = Return of asset $i$ in period $t$.
- $T$ = Total number of periods.
LOS : Calculate and interpret different approaches to return measurement over time and describe their appropriate uses.

## Example: Arithmetic return

If a share has returned 25%, 10%, 12% and -3% over the last four years consecutively, then the arithmetic mean can be calculated as:


\bar{R}_i = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} R_{it}

= \frac{25\% + 10\% + 12\% + (-3\%)}{4} = 11\%

## 3. Geometric Mean Return

- The geometric mean return gives the average rate per period on an investment that is compounded over multiple periods.
- It is denoted by $\bar{R}_{Gi}$ and is given by the following equation:


\bar{R}_{Gi} = \sqrt[T]{(1 + R_{i,1}) \times (1 + R_{i2}) \times \cdots \times (1 + R_{i,T-1}) \times (1 + R_{iT})} - 1

= \sqrt[T]{\prod_{t=1}^{T} (1 + R_{t})} - 1

## Example: Geometric Mean Return

If a share has returned 25%, 10%, 12% and -3% over the last four years, then the geometric mean can be calculated as:


\bar{R}_{Gi} = \sqrt[T]{\left(1 + R_{i,1}\right) \times \left(1 + R_{i2}\right) \times \cdots \times \left(1 + R_{i,T-1}\right) \times \left(1 + R_{iT}\right)} - 1 = (1.25 \times 1.10 \times 1.12 \times 0.97)^{\frac{1}{4}} - 1 = 10.6\%

## 4. Harmonic Mean

- Measure of a central tendency that is efficient when the variables are rates or ratios (such as P/Es).
- It is denoted by $\bar{X}_H$ and is given by the following equation:


\bar{X}_H = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{\bar{X}_i}}, \quad X_i &gt; 0 \text{ for all } i = 1, 2, \ldots, n


- "Harmonic mean of observations $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$."

## Trimmed Mean

- It is a measure of central tendency in which mean is calculated by excluding a small percentage of lowest and highest values, e.g., by removing the lowest 2.5% and the highest 2.5% of values.
- For example, a data set consists of 10 observations: 12, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 27, 30, 35, and 40.
- If we remove, the highest and lowest values: 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 27, 30, and 35.
- The trimmed mean is:


\frac{15 + 18 + 20 + 22 + 25 + 27 + 30 + 35}{8} = \frac{192}{8} = 24

## Winsorized Mean

- It is a measure of central tendency in which mean is calculated from the mean of the lowest values equal to one specified low value and a stated percentage of the highest values equal to one specified high value.
- Consider a dataset of 12 observations: 8, 12, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 27, 30, 35, 40, and 50.
- Replace extreme values at both ends with the values of their nearest observations, respectively: 12, 12, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 27, 30, 35, 40, and 40.
- The Winsorized mean is:


\frac{12 + 12 + 15 + 18 + 20 + 22 + 25 + 27 + 30 + 35 + 40 + 40}{12} \approx 24.67

## Money-Weighted Rate of Return

- The **money-weighted return** accounts for the money invested and provides the investor with information on the return earned on the actual investment.
- Its calculation is like that of the **internal rate of return (IRR)**.
- Its equation is given as:


\sum_{t=0}^{T} \frac{CF_t}{(1 + IRR)^t} = 0


## Where:

- **T** = Number of periods.
- **$CF_t$** = Cash flow at time $t$ (can be positive or negative).
- **IRR** = Internal rate of return (or money-weighted rate of return).
LOS: Compare the money-weighted and time-weighted rates of return and evaluate the performance of portfolios based on these measures.

## Money-Weighted Rate of Return

### Example

An investor invests $200 in a mutual fund at the beginning of the first year. At the beginning of the second year, the investor adds another $250. At the beginning of the third year, an additional $150 is invested into the fund. At the beginning of the fourth year, the investor withdraws $400. The fund closes at the beginning of the fifth year, and the investor withdraws the remaining $290.

If the investor earned 10% in the first year, 6.6% in the second year, and 6% in the third year, the money-weighted rate of return can now be calculated with the following cash flows:
LOS : Compare the money-weighted and time-weighted rates of return and evaluate the performance of portfolios based on these measures.

## Solution

| | 1 | 2 | 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Beginning balance | 0 | 220 | 501 |
| New investments | +200 | +250 | +150 |
| Investment return | +20 | +31 | +39 |
| Withdrawals | 0 | 0 | -400 |
| Ending balance | 220 | 501 | 290 |


\sum_{t=0}^{T} \frac{CF_t}{(1 + IRR)^t} = 0

CF_0 = -200

CF_1 = -250

CF_2 = -150

CF_3 = +400

CF_4 = +290

\frac{-200}{1} + \frac{-250}{(1 + IRR)^1} + \frac{-150}{(1 + IRR)^2} + \frac{400}{(1 + IRR)^3} + \frac{290}{(1 + IRR)^4} = 0

IRR = 5.72\%

## Time-Weighted Rate of Return

The time-weighted return measures the compound rate of growth of $1 initially invested in the portfolio over a stated measurement period.

It is not sensitive to additions and withdrawals of the funds.

## How to Calculate the Time-Weighted Rate of Return?

- Step 1: Value the portfolio immediately before any significant cash inflow or outflow of funds. Divide the evaluation period into subperiods based on dates of significant additions or withdrawals of funds.
- Step 2: Compute the holding period return on the portfolio for each period.
- Step 3: Compound or link the holding period returns to the annual rate of return, which is the time-weighted rate of return.
LOS : Compare the money-weighted and time-weighted rates of return and evaluate the performance of portfolios based on these measures.

## Time-Weighted Rate of Return

☐ If we value a portfolio in a regular intervals (summing to a year), say daily, then the time-weighted return for the year is:


\mathbf{T W R R} = \left\{\left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_1} \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_2}\right) \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_3}\right) \dots \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_{n-1}}\right) \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_n}\right)\right\} - \mathbf{1}


☐ However, if we are given yearly time-weighted rates of return, then the annualized time-weighted rate of return is their geometric mean :


\begin{array}{l}
\overline{\mathbf{R_{Gi}}} = \sqrt[T]{\left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_1}\right) \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_1}\right) \dots \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_n}\right)} - \mathbf{1} \\
= \left[\left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_1}\right) \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_1}\right) \dots \times \left(\mathbf{1} + \mathbf{R_n}\right)\right]^{\frac{1}{n}} - \mathbf{1}
\end{array}

## Example

An investor buys 4 shares of UUA stock at $44. During the year, the company pays a $3 special dividend per share. Then, at the end of the first year, the investor buys 5 more shares at $46. Lastly, at the end of the second year, he sold all the shares for $57. If there was no dividend during the second year, the time-weighted rate of return of this investment is closest to:

## Solution


\text{HPR for the first year} = \frac{46 - 44 + 3}{44} = 0.1136

\text{HPR for the second year} = \frac{57 - 46}{46} = 0.2391

\text{Time-weighted return} = \left[(1 + 0.1136) \times (1 + 0.2391)\right]^{\frac{1}{2}} - 1 = 17.4\%

It considers withdrawals and/or contributions from the fund.

Time-weighted rate of return

Is not sensitive to withdrawals or contributions.

## Annualized Return


PV = FV_N \left(1 + \frac{R_S}{m}\right)^{-mN}


### Where:

$m$ = No. of compounding periods in a year.

$R_S$ = Quoted annual interest rate.

$N$ = Number of years.

To annualize a return for a period less than one year, the return for the period must be compounded by the number of periods in a year.

### Example:

## Continuously Compounded Return

- Defined as the natural logarithm of one plus a holding period return of a period.
- Example:
- If a month’s holding period return is $1.2\%$, the continuously compounded return is $\ln (1.012) = 0.01192$
- Generally, continuously compounded from $t$ to $t + 1$ is given by:

r_{t,t+1} = \ln \left(\frac{P_{t+1}}{P_t}\right) = \ln (1 + R_{t,t+1})

- Assume investment horizon is from time $t = 0$ to time $t = T$:

r_{0,T} = \ln \left(\frac{P_T}{P_0}\right)

- Apply the exponential function on both sides of the above equation:

P_T = P_0 e^{r_{0,T}}

### Continued...

☐ Note that $\frac{P_T}{P_0}$ can be written as:


\frac{P_T}{P_0} = \left(\frac{P_T}{P_{T-1}}\right) \left(\frac{P_{T-1}}{P_{T-2}}\right) \dots \left(\frac{P_1}{P_0}\right)

## Real return

- Recall the relationship between the nominal rate and the real return:


(1 + \text{Nominal Risk free rate}) = (1 + \text{Real risk free rate})(1 + \text{Inflation premium})


- The equation above can be extended to show the relationship between the nominal and the real returns so that:


(1 + \text{Real return}) = \frac{(1 + \text{Real risk free rate})(1 + \text{Risk premium})}{1 + \text{Inflation premium}}


- Real returns are especially helpful when comparing returns across different time periods and between countries

- When returns are expressed in local currencies rather than a constant investor currency.

- Inflation rates differ between the countries.

- After-tax real returns are the amount the investor receives as payment for delaying consumption and taking on risk after paying taxes on investment.

## Gross and net returns

- Gross return is earned prior to the deduction of fees (management fees, custodial fees, etc.).
- A net return is the return post-deduction of fees.
- An investor has a gross return of $120,000. If management fees is 5% of an investor's gross return, the investor's net return would be $114,000.


120,000 \times (1 - 5\%) = \$114,000


## Pre-tax and after-tax return

- All returns are pre-tax nominal returns unless stated otherwise. Capital gains and income may be taxed differently in different jurisdictions.
- The after-tax nominal return is computed as the total return minus any allowance for taxes on dividends, interest and realized gains.
- An investor that earns a pre-tax return of 15% on their investments. Applying a 25% tax rate, their after-tax return would be


= 15\% \times (1 - 25\%) = 11.25\%

## Leveraged Return

- If an investor uses **derivative instruments** within a portfolio or **borrows money** to invest, then leverage is introduced into the portfolio.
- ☐ Amplifies returns both upwards and downwards
- ☐ Leverage return considers actual return on the investment and the cost of the borrowed money.


R_L = \frac{\text{Portfolio return}}{\text{Portfolio equity}} = \frac{[R_P \times (V_E + V_B) - (V_B \times r_D)]}{V_E} = \boldsymbol{R}_P + \frac{\boldsymbol{V}_B}{\boldsymbol{V}_E} (\boldsymbol{R}_P - \boldsymbol{r}_D)


## Where:

- $\boldsymbol{R}_L$ = Return earned on the leveraged portfolio.
- $\boldsymbol{R}_P$ = Total investment return earned on the leveraged portfolio.
- $\boldsymbol{V}_B$ = Value of debt in the portfolio.
- $\boldsymbol{V}_E$ = Value of equity in the portfolio.
- $\boldsymbol{r}_D$ = Borrowing cost on debt.
LOS: Calculate and interpret major return measures and describe their appropriate uses.

## Example: Leveraged Return

A $7,500,000 equity portfolio is 35% financed by debt at a cost of 5% per annum. If the equity portfolio generates a 9% annual total investment return, the leveraged return is closest to:
LOS: Calculate and interpret major return measures and describe their appropriate uses.

## Example: Leveraged Return

A $7,500,000 equity portfolio is 35% financed by debt at a cost of 5% per annum. If the equity portfolio generates a 9% annual total investment return, the leveraged return is closest to:

## Solution


\begin{array}{l}
R_{L} = R_{P} + \frac{V_{B}}{V_{E}} (R_{P} - r_{D}) \\
= 9\% + \frac{\$2,625,000}{\$4,875,000} (9\% - 5\%) = \mathbf{11.15\%}
\end{array}
