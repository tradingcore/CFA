## Breakeven Analysis

- This is when a company generates revenue equal to its economic costs effectively compensating for all factors of production, including capital.
- Economic costs encompass both total accounting costs and implicit opportunity costs.
- When a company's Total Revenue (TR) or Average Revenue (AR) under perfect and imperfect competition equals its Total Cost (TC) or Average Total Cost (ATC), it is considered to have achieved break-even point.

## Perfect Competition

- A firm is required to accept the market price of its output as a given, leading to a perfectly elastic and horizontal demand curve.
- Consequently, the firm's marginal revenue (MR), average revenue (AR), and the price (P) of its product are all equal.

## Imperfect Competition

- A firm encounters a **demand curve** with a negative slope, necessitating a price reduction to sell an additional unit.
- As a result, the firm's **marginal revenue (MR)** is lower than the price (P) of the product.

Shutdown Analysis

| | Short-term decision | Long-run decision |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Total cost = Total revenue | Remain in the market | Remain in the market |
| Total revenue < Total variable cost | Cease production | Leave the market |
| Total revenue = Total variable costs but < Total costs | Remain in the market | Leave the market |

If a firm is unable to generate economic profits, it may continue to operate in the short term but in the long run it should cease operations.

## Factors that can lead to economies of scale:

- Effectively reducing waste and lowering costs by selling by-products and enhanced quality control measures.
- Using market information to make more effective managerial decisions.
- Purchasing resources in larger quantities on discount.
- Achieving economies of scale through increasing returns to scale.
- Implementing a division of labor and management.
- Enhancing productivity by investing in more expensive and efficient equipment.

## Factors that can lead to diseconomies of scale:

- Experiencing diseconomies of scale due to decreasing returns, where output increases that are proportionately smaller than the increase in inputs.
- Struggling with proper management due to excessive size.
- Encountering inefficiencies caused by overlapping and duplicating business functions.
- Facing higher resource prices due to supply constraints when purchasing inputs in large quantities.

## Market structure is influenced by five key factors:

- The **quantity** and **relative magnitude** of firms involved in supplying the product.
- The level of **product differentiation** present in the market.
- The seller's power over **pricing** decisions.
- The strength of **barriers** regulating market entry and exit.
- The extent of **non-price competition** observed in the market.

## Perfect Competition

- Very large number of buyers ands sellers in the market.
- Homogeneous products.
- Perfect information.
- Firms accepts prices (“price taker”).
- No barriers to entry.
- Profit maximization of sellers.
- No single seller/producer is large enough to influence the market price.

## Examples:

Agricultural products.

## Monopolistic Competition

- Multiple buyers and sellers in the market.
- Sellers use branding and differentiation to gain market share.
- Sellers have some control over pricing.
- Few barriers to entry.

## Examples:

Fast food chains, clothing, etc.

## Monopoly

- A monopoly is a **profit maximizer**.
- Monopolies are **price makers**.
- There are very **high barriers to entry** for other firms.
- There is a **single seller** that controls the whole market.
- **Price discrimination**: Monopolies can change both the price and quality of their products.
- Pure monopolies are **regulated** by the government.

## Examples:

Providers of water, natural gas, telecommunications, and electricity are often granted exclusive rights to service.

## Oligopoly

- ☐ Profit maximization is a condition in this market.
- ☐ Firms under this market structure set their own prices, although there is a great deal of pricing interdependence.
- ☐ Barriers to entry are high.
- ☐ Make abnormal profits in the long-run.
- ☐ Products may be homogeneous.
- ☐ A relatively small number of firms supply the market.

## Examples:

Industries like oil &amp; gas, airline, automakers, etc.

## Optimal Price and Output

- The short-run **profit-maximizing choice** occurs at the point where marginal revenue is equal to marginal cost:

\mathbf{MR} = \mathbf{MC}


- **Total cost** is calculated as the product of average cost and total quantity:

\mathbf{TC} = \mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{Q}


- The **economic profit** is calculated as the difference between total revenue and total cost:

\Pi = \mathbf{TR} - \mathbf{TC}

## Cournot assumption

- Each firm determines its optimal production level assuming that the output of other firms will remain unaltered.
- Their goal is to maximize profits while expecting the other firm to maintain a constant output in the future.

## Nash equilibrium

- The firms have no intent to deviate from their respective equilibrium strategies, even after considering and anticipating their opponents' strategies.
- The equilibrium is achieved when all firms are striving to do their best in their own interest, considering the actions of their rivals.

## Collusions are more likely to succeed when:

- The number and size distribution of sellers is small.
- The products are homogeneous and indistinguishable.
- The participating firms have similar cost structures.
- Orders are frequent, regular, and of relatively small sizes.
- The fear of severe retaliation discourages firms from breaking collusive agreements.
- External competitors face higher production costs.

## Concentration Ratio

- It’s the sum of market share for largest firms in industry.
- Usually calculated for largest 3, 4, or 5 firms.
- Value between 0% (perfect competition) and 100% (monopoly).

## Herfindahl-Hirschman Index

- It’s the sum of squares of top market share companies.
- Highest value of 1 for monopoly.

## Example: Concentration Measures

If the top 2 suppliers in a market each hold 20% and 30% of the market share, then:

- The concentration ratio is a sum of the two suppliers' market share: 20% + 30% = 50%.
- For the HHI, we take 0.20² + 0.30² = 0.13.
