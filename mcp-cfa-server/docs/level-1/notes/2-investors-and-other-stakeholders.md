## Comparison between Debt and Equity Claims

### Debtholders:

- Provide **finite-term** financial capital to a company.
- In return, the issuers agree to make **regular interest payments** and repay the principal on predetermined dates.
- Do not possess decision-making authority.
- Terms of the debt agreement can safeguard them by establishing **financial requirements** and **legal claims** on specific company assets.
- Debtholder interest payments are usually considered **tax-deductible expenses**, reducing the company's taxable income.
LOS: Compare the financial claims and motivations of lenders and shareholders.

## Comparison between Debt and Equity Claims

### Shareholders:

- Equity investors contribute **permanent capital** to the company.
- Typically, do not commit to providing future dividends or repayments to shareholders.
- Equity represents a **residual claim** on the company's cash flows.
- Distribution of cash to equity investors is subject to the **discretion of the board of directors**.
- The company must meet other financial obligations (including bondholders) before any distributions are made to shareholders.

## Equity and Debt Risk-Return Profiles

### Investor Perspective: Equity holders

- The maximum loss that equity holders can incur is capped at their investment amount in the company.
- Gain if the share prices increase in futures.
- No limit on the gains to an equity holder if the company succeeds.
- Gains in terms of distributions from the company and residual claim after a profitable corporation settles its obligations.
- Risks: Investment risk is higher for the equity holders than bondholders.
- Stocks are riskier for investors since no contractual obligation is set to distribute to the shareholders or repay the capital investment.
- Equity holders may lose entire investments if the company goes bankrupt.

## Investor Perspective: Bondholders

- Have fixed **priority claims** on a company through contractually promised interest payments and return of the principal amount.
- Do not receive more than promised interest payments when a company becomes profitable.
- **Upside gains**: Capped at the interest payments plus the principal.
- **Downside losses**: If a company falls below the book value of the debt.
- **Risks**: Investment risk is **lower** for bondholders than equity holders.
- When a company is financially healthy, bondholders are assured of their returns since the company can easily service the loan or has sufficient assets that serve as collateral.

## Risk-Return Profile of a Bondholder

![img-2.jpeg](img-2.jpeg)

- In contrast to equity holders, bondholders have **recourse opportunities** when a company is in financial distress.
- Use the contractual agreement to force the issuer to liquidate assets to repay their debt.
- **Note**: Bondholders could lose their entire investment too.
- Bondholders are keener on the **default risk** of the company and its ability to meet debt obligations.

Risk-Return Profile: Issuer Perspective

- Corporations prefer debt capital over equity capital.
- Cost of debt is lower than that of equity capital, and the return to the lenders is capped.
- Equity capital dilutes ownership and is only appropriate when the issuer's cash flows are absent or unpredictable.
- Bonds are riskier than shares since it involves contractual agreements which must be honored.
- Bondholders elevate the risk to the corporation by increasing leverage.

## Conflicts of Interest between Bondholders and Equity Holders

- Since shareholders' loss is capped at their respective initial investment amount and return potential is unlimited.
- Shareholders refer a company's management to invest in areas with **high calculated risks** and potential returns.

- Potential return for the bondholders is limited to the face value of the bond and the coupon payments, and they do not receive any benefits from the risky investment decisions of the company.
- Bondholders prefer a company to invest in **less risky projects** with **certain cash flows**, however small, to secure timely interest and principal repayments.
- Bondholders cannot control a company's investment decisions, but they can use **covenants**.

## Corporate Stakeholders

- Corporate governance systems can be influenced by several stakeholder groups which may or may not have conflicting interests.
- A company's primary stakeholders include:
- Investors: Debt and equity holders.
- Creditors: Banks and private lenders, and Public Debtholders.
- Managers: CEO, senior executives and high-level managers.
- Suppliers: Provide resources required to produce goods and services
- Board of Directors: Elected by shareholders to safeguard their interests and to provide strategic direction

Others include customers, governments, and regulators.

## Shareholder vs. Stakeholder Theory

In shareholder theory, shareholders elect the board of directors in turn hire managers to serve shareholders’ interests.

- The interests of other stakeholders, such as creditors, employees, customers, and suppliers, considered only to the degree that they have an impact on shareholder value.

Stakeholder theory emphasizes the interest of all stakeholders and not just those of the shareholders by considering environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors. This comes with several challenges:

I. Balancing multiple objectives.
II. Defining and measuring non-shareholder objectives.
III. Competing globally with competitors that do not follow similar standards may be difficult.
IV. Considering these other interest increases a company’s costs.

## Debtholders

### Private Debtholders:
- Include banks, credit facilities, institutions that provide loans and leases.
- Hold the debt of a company to maturity.
- They can directly reach a company's management and access non-public information concerning a company.

### Public Debtholders
- Depend on public information and credit rating agencies in their decision-making.
- Include institutional investors and asset managers.
- Bondholders have little to no authority over the operations of the issuing company.
- Can have significant influence over the company experiences financial distress, and the public debt needs to be restructured.

## Board of Directors

- Elected by the shareholders of the company.
- Responsibilities: Protecting shareholders’ interests, hiring the CEO, monitoring performance.
- Single-tier board:
- Company's board of directors includes both executive directors and non-executive directors (independent members).
- Two-tier board structure:
- Company's board of directors is divided into two separate boards:
- i. Management Board: The management board is responsible for the company's day-to-day operations and strategic management.
- Consists of executive directors who are actively involved in running the company and making operational decisions.
- ii. Supervisory Board: The supervisory board oversees the management board and ensures it acts in the company's and its stakeholders' best interests.
- Consists of independent directors from among corporate stakeholders, including shareholders, employees, labor unions, public and sometimes from government.

## Evaluating ESG Risks and Opportunities

- Influence of ESG factors on corporate cash flows depends on how they impact the value of debt and equity claims:
- Once identified, the financial impact of material ESG factors must be quantified in terms of how they positively or negatively affect the firm's future discounted cash flows.
- Significant long-term adverse ESG-related events have an immediate and disproportionate impact on equity claims since they represent residual claims to future company cash flows.
- Although adverse ESG-related events impact the value of debtholder claims, their effect is generally less pronounced compared to equity, except in cases where the company's ability to fulfill interest and principal payments is adversely affected.
- The effects of adverse ESG-related events often differ depending on the maturity of the debt.

- In conclusion: Analysts assess the potential positive and negative effects of significant ESG-related factors.
- Reflected in a company's projected financial statements and ratios.
