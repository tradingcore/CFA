## Why technique matters more than hours

Two candidates can put in 350 hours each. One passes comfortably; the other fails by 5 percentage points. The difference is rarely intelligence or background — it's the **techniques they used during those 350 hours**.

This article is about the techniques. Not what to study (that's covered in our [Level I prep plan](/cfa/best-way-to-prepare-for-cfa-level-1)) — but **how to study so the time actually sticks**.

## The biggest enemy: passive reading

The default mode for most candidates is read → highlight → re-read → highlight more. This is **passive reading**, and decades of cognitive science research show it produces the **illusion of competence** — you feel like you know the material, but you can't recall or apply it under exam conditions.

Symptoms you're stuck in passive mode:

- You spend 90% of your study time reading and 10% practicing.
- You re-read the same chapter and "still don't really get it."
- Your highlighter is your most-used tool.
- You feel productive when you're studying, but mock scores are stuck.

The fix is uncomfortable: **flip the ratio**. 70% practice, 30% reading. Read a topic once, then test yourself relentlessly.

## Active recall: the single biggest lever

Active recall = the act of pulling information out of your head, rather than putting more information in. Examples:

- Closing the textbook and writing out everything you remember about a topic.
- Doing practice questions before re-reading a section.
- Explaining a concept out loud as if teaching a friend.
- Drawing the income statement → cash flow statement bridge from memory.

A meta-analysis of 200+ studies found active recall outperforms passive review by **40–60%** for long-term retention. For a credential exam tested at the end of a 5+ month prep, this is the single highest-leverage habit.

**Practical implementation for the CFA:**

1. After reading a chapter, **close the book** and write a 1-page summary from memory.
2. Compare to the chapter. Note gaps. Re-read only the gap areas.
3. Do 20–30 practice questions on the topic immediately.
4. Schedule a recall test 3 days later (open with a blank page, write what you remember).

## Spaced repetition: where Anki helps

Forgetting is exponential — without review, you lose ~70% of what you learn within 24 hours. Spaced repetition counteracts this by reviewing material at expanding intervals (1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days).

For the CFA, spaced repetition is **best for**:

- Formulas (DCF, WACC, Sharpe ratio, duration formulas)
- Ratio definitions and components
- Ethics standards summary cards
- Specific definitional terms (e.g., "what is convexity?")

It is **less useful for**:

- Conceptual understanding (better gained through practice problems)
- Multi-step calculations (better drilled through worked examples)

**Tooling:** Anki is the gold standard — free, customizable, syncs across devices. Some candidates use the AI-spaced repetition built into providers like Trading Core.

## Mock exam review: the most underrated skill

Most candidates think the value of a mock exam is the score. The score is the **least important** part. The value is the review.

For each wrong answer (and any answer you guessed and got right):

1. **Identify the LOS** the question tested. Write it down.
2. **Classify why you got it wrong**: concept gap, calculation error, misread the question, ran out of time, knew the concept but applied wrong formula.
3. **Re-read the LOS** in the curriculum or your notes.
4. **Do 5 more questions** on the same sub-topic. If you get less than 4/5, repeat.

A typical mock exam (180 questions) generates **30–60 review items**. Plan for **8–12 hours of review per mock**. Yes, more time on review than on the mock itself. Candidates who do this religiously gain 10–20 percentage points on subsequent mocks.

## The "teach it" test

A simple but brutal test: pick any LOS at random and explain it out loud as if to a friend who hasn't studied finance. Time yourself. 2-minute limit.

If you stumble, hedge, say "it's kinda like…", or skip steps, **you don't actually understand it**. You recognize the words, but you can't generate the explanation.

This test is especially useful for:

- Income statement → cash flow statement bridge
- Why DCF and multiples can give different valuations
- The relationship between bond price, yield, and duration
- Why beta isn't the only measure of risk

Schedule a "teach it" session weekly. Pick 5 LOS, explain each out loud. The ones you fumble go on your weak-area list.

## Pomodoro and session structure

Long, unstructured study sessions are inefficient. Better structure:

| Session structure | Use case |
|---|---|
| **25 min focus + 5 min break** (classic Pomodoro) | Practice question batches, light reading |
| **50 min focus + 10 min break** | Mock exam sections, deeper concept work |
| **90 min focus + 30 min break** | Full mock simulation, intense review |

Cap your daily study at **3–4 deep sessions**. Beyond that, retention drops sharply and the marginal hour is mostly wasted.

## Note-taking: yes, but selectively

Notes done right amplify learning; notes done wrong waste time.

**Worth taking notes on:**

- Formulas (one master sheet by topic, ~3–5 pages total)
- Ratio components (one page)
- Ethics Standards summaries (one page per Standard)
- Tricky concepts where your own words help (e.g., why goodwill arises, when LIFO inflates COGS)

**Not worth taking notes on:**

- Re-writing the textbook (just slow re-reading)
- Summaries of every reading (too much volume to revisit)
- Color-coded "study aesthetic" notebooks

## How to use videos

Videos shine when:

- An instructor adds context or analogies the textbook doesn't
- You're a strong auditory learner
- You're commuting or doing laundry (yes, audio-only counts)

Videos waste time when:

- You already understand the topic but watch out of habit
- You watch passively without taking notes or doing follow-up questions
- The video pace is much slower than your reading pace

Use videos for the **3–4 hardest topics for you personally** — for everyone else, reading is faster.

## The role of AI tutors

Modern AI tutors (Trading Core, ChatGPT-style tools) are most useful for:

- **Instant explanations** when you're stuck on a concept and don't want to wait for forum responses
- **Adapting practice difficulty** based on your performance
- **Generating fresh practice questions** on weak LOS
- **Reviewing your wrong answers** with personalized explanations

They're less useful for:

- Replacing structured curriculum coverage
- Memorization (use Anki)
- Grading your essay-style answers (Level III)

The general-purpose chatbots (vanilla ChatGPT) are useful but often hallucinate finance details. CFA-specific tools constrained to the official curriculum (like [Trading Core](/register)) are more reliable.

## Routine: the 80/20 of consistency

Discipline > motivation. Build a routine:

| Element | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Study at the same time | Pre-work morning OR post-work evening — pick one and stick to it |
| Same place | Reduces decision fatigue and triggers focus |
| No phone in study area | Single biggest lever for productive study |
| Review yesterday's material before starting today's | 5-min recall warm-up |
| End each session with a "tomorrow" plan | Removes start-up cost the next day |

Two 1-hour sessions on a weekday will beat one rushed 4-hour Saturday session.

## Stress, sleep, and exam-day mechanics

The week of the exam, **technique matters more than knowledge**.

- **Sleep 7–9 hours** for the 5 days before. Sleep consolidates memory.
- **Eat normally**, no experimental food on exam day.
- **Practice the calculator** until your fingers know which button is where without looking.
- **Practice timing**. Aim for ~90 seconds per question average. Skip and flag instead of stalling.
- **Read the question first**, then the answer choices. Most exam mistakes come from rushing the question stem.

## What to do if mocks are stuck

If you're 4 weeks out and mock scores are stuck below 60%:

1. **Stop new content.** You don't have a knowledge problem; you have an application problem.
2. **Drill the bottom 3 topics**: 100 questions per topic, with deep review.
3. **Add one extra mock** in week -3.
4. **Re-do the questions you got wrong** (not the entire mock) 5 days later.

This usually adds 5–10 points. If you're still stuck below 60% one week out, consider deferring to the next sitting and saving the registration cost — better than failing.

## Summary: the technique stack

| Technique | When to use |
|---|---|
| Active recall (write from memory, teach out loud) | Every reading session |
| Spaced repetition (Anki) | Formulas, ratios, Ethics standards |
| Practice questions (high volume) | Daily, all topics |
| Mock exam review (deep) | 5–7× in final month |
| Pomodoro structure | Daily sessions |
| Selective note-taking | Formulas + Ethics summaries only |
| Videos | Hard topics only |
| AI tutor | Stuck concepts, fresh practice |

Use all of them. Reading + highlighting alone doesn't pass Level I.
