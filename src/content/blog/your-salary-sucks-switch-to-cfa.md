The median CFA charterholder in the US makes **\$300,000** a year.

Look at your paycheck. We good? Cool, you can close this tab.

Still here? Right.

That \$300K is **average total comp** for a US charterholder per the [CFA Institute 2024 Compensation Study](https://www.cfainstitute.org/insights/compensation-study-2024) — 17,000+ members surveyed across 132 markets. Median base alone is **\$180K**. The rest is bonus and equity.

Your "I'm doing great in my career" salary, whatever it is, is almost certainly less than that. And the reason isn't that you work less hard. It's that **you're in the wrong industry for your skills**.

## The number nobody can argue with

Pushback you're already typing: *"yeah but charterholders are senior people, of course they make more."*

Fine. Let's strip seniority out.

[300hours' 2026 analysis](https://300hours.com/cfa-salary-increase/) does exactly that. Same age, same experience, just compares people at different stages of the CFA. The result:

- Pass Level I → **+30%** salary
- Pass Level II → **+36%**
- Get the charter → **+59%** vs a Level I candidate
- An MBA, isolated the same way → **+18%**

The CFA, **per credential**, moves your pay **3× more than an MBA**. And costs **40× less**.

That's not a "marketing study." It's published methodology, and the same number shows up across every survey since 2018. You can argue with the magnitude. You can't argue with the direction.

|---|---|
| Chief Investment Officer | **\$394K** |
| Equity Portfolio Manager | **\$252K** |
| Director of Investments | **\$235K** |
| FI Portfolio Manager | **\$213K** |
| Buy-side Equity Analyst | **\$190K** |
| Risk Manager | **\$173K** |
| Sell-side Equity Analyst | **\$133K** |

That last line — sell-side analyst, the **entry-level** investment job — is **\$133K**.

What's the median for a senior corporate FP&A analyst with the same 5 years of experience? Glassdoor says ~\$95K–\$115K. Same brain, same hours, same bachelor's degree. **35% less.**

The only difference is which floor of the building you sit on.

## The math everyone avoids

Cost of the charter:

- \$3K–\$5K in fees
- ~1,000 hours of study (50/hr after-tax = \$50K opportunity cost)
- 3 years

All-in: **~\$55K** spread over three years.

Conservative return: **\$50K/year** salary lift. Stay in finance until 60. That's \$1.5M undiscounted, ~\$770K NPV at 5%.

**14× present-value ROI.** Cut every assumption in half — half the lift, half the years, double the cost — and it's still **6–7×**.

Find me another credential, in any field, at any price, with that math.

You won't.

## Who this is NOT for

I'm gonna lose half the audience here. Fair.

- **You're in IB / M&A / quant.** CFA is fine to have. Doesn't move comp. MBA, deal experience, or PhD do. Don't bother.
- **You're in pure corporate finance / FP&A.** CPA or CMA on-target. CFA is overkill and won't move your comp progression.
- **You won't actually make the move into an investment seat.** The charter is the easy part. Getting hired into research / PM / risk / wealth is the hard part. If you stay in your current non-finance role, the credential prints zero.

If any of those is you, the rest of this post doesn't apply. The data still says what it says — but for *your* career, the lift won't show up.

## The honest reframe

The headline says "switch to CFA" because that's the part that gets clicks. The actual claim is more subtle:

> **Most people optimize their career inside the path they're already on. Almost no one stops and asks if the entire path is the wrong one.**

The CFA Level I is the **cheapest test** of that question. **One year, \$1,200, 300 hours.** Take it. If you hate the material, you've spent a year and \$1,200 — you got off cheap. If you love it, you just found a 30-year track that pays 30–60% more than whatever you're doing now.

The asymmetry is the whole game. "Quit your job" is theatrical. **Stop assuming your current path is the right one** is what actually matters.

## What 2026 changed

Three things make this argument louder than it was 4 years ago:

- **AI is eating generic analyst jobs.** Corporate FP&A, junior consulting, basic research. Not investment management — that's *judgment under uncertainty with real money*. AI tools amplify it instead of replacing it. The CFA curriculum is, conveniently, the curriculum for that exact job.
- **Higher rates → fees back at active managers → comp up.** Asset management base salaries have grown faster than the broader market for the second straight year, per CFA Institute.
- **The credential is harder to fake.** With LinkedIn diluted by certificates and bootcamps, the three-letter, 4,000-hour, 10–15% completion-rate signal is louder than it's ever been.
